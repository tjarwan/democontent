Another topic with a similar profile is the comprehension of language.
Phenomenologically, we simply hear sounds as meaningful speech. But
there are complicated mechanisms underlying the processing of sounds
into phonemic, syntactic, and lexical units, and the integration of these
units into an interpretation as meaningful speech via sentence parsing.
Both the operation of these capacities and their acquisition seem to require
special mechanisms, which operate on nonintentional, nonrational prin-
ciples (Chomsky 1965, 1966). Unlike the mechanisms for perception and
motor control, those for comprehension of language seem likely to be
unique to the human species.
In each of these cases, we seem to have phenomena that have one foot
in the world of concepts, intentionality, and the space of reasons, and the
other in the world of neural mechanisms that operate on different prin-
ciples—principles that are in a broad sense “mechanical.” And the relation
between the intentional states and the mechanistic neural processes in this
case is not between a state at a high level of organization and its “realizing
substrate” but more like different links in a causal chain. When we perceive,
early perceptual processing causes conceptually laden perceptual gestalts
and subsequent intentional states and reasoning. When we act, intentional
states and practical reasoning cause the processes that direct bodily move-
ment to come into play.
This, in turn, suggests a basic way of extending the standard view to
accommodate phenomena such as perception, action, and language pars-
ing. There are, as it were, two importantly different types of cognition. On
the one hand, there is “central” cognition, which is the subject matter of
standard philosophical theories and described by the three-tiered picture,
involving concepts, intentional states, and reasoned inference. On the
other hand, there are a variety of “peripheral” processes, operating on dif-
ferent, nonintentional, nonrational principles, which can either produce
outputs into central cognition (in the cases of perception and language
parsing, which indeed might be seen as a specialized form of perception) or
take inputs from central cognition (in the case of the motor systems) and
produce behavior as a consequence. In the next section, I describe a familiar
approach to this division, which contrasts central and peripheral processes
by treating peripheral processes as “modular.”
