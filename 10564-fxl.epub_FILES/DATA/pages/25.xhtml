This actually dovetails nicely with the distinction between belief and
judgment in the previous section. Dennett’s characterization of belief
ascription helps us make sense of ascriptions of beliefs that cannot plausi-
bly be construed as ascriptions of judgments: they are, in effect, ascriptions
of normative commitments. Of course, judgments involve normative com-
mitments as well, even though we often do make judgments that are not
ideally rational. Philosophers have raised good and important questions
about whether epistemology does or should apply to beliefs that are not
judgments and, conversely, whether the question of which beliefs are also
judgments is really salient to the concerns of epistemology. (For example,
do questions of warrant really make sense if beliefs are imputed from an
assumption of ideal rationality?) But I will not address those questions here,
as my principal concern is with cognitive architecture.
Another important issue should be addressed. There seem to be clear
cases of language-like judgments: if nothing else, when someone mentally
endorses a proposition by thinking it to herself in a natural language. Like-
wise, clear cases exist in which we can sensibly ascribe to a person a belief
that P in full knowledge that she has never had any thought with the con-
tent P. For example, I recall one college friend describing another by saying,
“He believes he is entitled to half of the speaking time in any conversa-
tion, regardless of how many people are in the conversation.” It was an
apt characterization, but it is unlikely in the extreme that said friend ever
formed, much less endorsed, a thought with that content. Indeed, had he
been presented with that description of himself, he would doubtless either
have disputed it or tried to modify his behavior thereafter. But a wide range
of cases also fall in between. The phone rings, and I reach to pick it up.
