Modest foundationalism avoids one of the problems with strong foun-
dationalism: its overly high epistemic standard for properly basic beliefs
or judgments. But it retains the core foundationalist commitment to the
assumption that beliefs are warranted individually, some by being properly
basic and others by being inferred from properly basic beliefs. And it is this
commitment that is most directly at odds with a model-based cognitive
pluralism.
From a cognitive pluralist standpoint, the claim that some judgments
are warranted because of how they are inferred from other judgments
need not itself be regarded as problematic. A cognitive pluralist can cer-
tainly recognize that we do sometimes reason using various forms of logical
inference ranging over sentence-sized units, and the validity of the infer-
ence form and the warrant of the premises are surely relevant to the epis-
temic status of the conclusions thus derived. The problem is that this is
neither the only nor even the most fundamental source of warrant, and the
kind of warrant conferred by models does not simply attach to individual
judgments one by one. At the least, some of our beliefs that have excellent
credentials for counting as knowledge, such as those involved in scientific
theories, are neither individually properly basic nor justified by inference
from other beliefs that are already warranted as properly basic. Rather,
the beliefs and the whole framework of concepts and inferential patterns
are warranted together, as a unit. And if the cognitive pluralist is correct
that this is not an idiosyncratic feature of scientific theories, but a much
more general feature of model-based understanding, foundationalism
lacks the resources to explain how an extremely large number of our beliefs
are warranted. At the least, foundationalism is incomplete as an account
of knowledge and requires resources of a very different sort to supple-
ment it.
