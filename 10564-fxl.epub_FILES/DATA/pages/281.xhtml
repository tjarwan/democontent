that the judgments they produce are trustworthy, and we can judge that
they are unreliable under particular conditions on the basis of experience.
These beliefs and judgments can be (or fail to be) warranted, but that does
not mean that the mechanisms themselves are warranted or unwarranted.
The application of the notion of “warrant” to such perceptual mechanisms
seems to be something of a category error.
But this does not imply that belief-producing mechanisms in general are
not epistemically evaluable beyond the question of whether they reliably
produce true beliefs. Special features of the mechanisms involved in percep-
tual preprocessing may debar them from specifically epistemic evaluation:
the mechanisms do not operate on conceptually laden content, operate
automatically, and are not subject to scrutiny or revision. Models can also
operate automatically; and as some modules may also be models, some
models may not be subject to scrutiny or revision, either. But other models
have inference patterns that operate on propositional content, can be used
deliberately as well as automatically, and are subject to scrutiny and revi-
sion in light of reasons to do so.
I have used scientific theories as one paradigm example of mental mod-
els. And scientific theories are clearly held to standards of epistemic evalua-
tion: they are compared with one another for their adequacy, and we speak
of them as being “evidenced” or “confirmed.” It is perhaps a matter of
semantic choice whether we should speak of theories themselves as being
“warranted” or say instead that beliefs about their credentials are warranted
and use some other term (like “confirmed”) for theories themselves. But
some form of epistemic evaluation clearly seems appropriate. So at least
some mental models are subject to epistemic evaluation, whether we call it
“warrant” or “confirmation” or by some other name.
