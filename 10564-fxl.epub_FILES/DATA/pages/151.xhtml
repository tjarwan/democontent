It would be possible to enter code for computing the area within M1,
but if it is an operation that will be called on by more than one module, it
is more efficient to define it as a separate modular procedure. So we might
define a separate module
M_HERON for computing area from the lengths of
sides. It would take three input parameters (one for the length of each side)
and pass back a single output parameter for the computed area.
Of course, when we are doing explicit mathematical calculations, we
can proceed in a fashion that fairly closely resembles the steps of such a
program. (Turing’s notion of “computation” was, after all, framed on the
model of what human mathematicians do in calculation.) But in most cases
that use mental models without such explicit calculation, the relation with
parameter passing is probably only analogous. There is some way in which
one model “requests” information that can be supplied by another, and
some means of accessing and then using it.
Language and Language-Like Cognition
I have tried to make a case here that there are forms of intermodel triangula-
tion that do not require a language or language-like thought processes. This,
of course, does not mean that we do not also possess linguistic resources.
At least some representations found in models can also be “exported” to be
represented (or “re-represented”) in a language or a language-like format,
typified by a grammar and the ability to combine, through syntax, thoughts
whose content may be derived from different models. This seems more or
less to correspond to what Fodor calls “central cognition,” albeit with two
important caveats. First, the alternatives to this language-like thinking are
not limited to Fodorian modules but include a wide variety of models as
well. Second, if models form the basis of much intuitive inference, the con-
tent of nonsensory representations is not to be understood wholly within
the context of central cognition (and so need not be “Quinean and isotro-
pic”) but is determined in large measure by their models of origin. But the
other main assumption about a language-like mode of cognition—that it
is a kind of ecumenical representational medium that is not domain spe-
cific—remains largely intact. (It remains only largely intact because there
may be many model-bound representations that cannot be effectively inte-
grated into central cognition. Think of all the things that are difficult to put
into language.)
