We saw in chapter 12, however, that different models involve differ-
ent, and sometimes conflicting, commitments. If we are committed to the
implications of our models—whether their general commitments or the
implications of their application to particular cases—it would thus seem
that we would turn out to be committed to conflicting beliefs as well. This
may initially seem like a problem, but really it is something we should want
a psychological account of belief to imply or at least accommodate. On the
one hand, a wealth of psychological studies show that our judgments are
quite sensitive to context and priming. A natural explanation of this is that
different contexts and priming conditions activate different models, gener-
ating one set of judgments rather than another depending on which model
is activated. On the other hand, we often find ourselves inclined, even after
much reflection, to endorse two propositions we know to be contradictory
when each is intuitively recommended by a model and we are not in a
position to see why one model is inapt or otherwise to adjudicate between
them. It is not clear that the fact that we have paradoxical sets of beliefs
should itself be regarded as paradoxical. But if it is, we seem to gain some
resolution by understanding that the situation is not one of simultaneously
and assuredly endorsing both P and not-P, but one of feeling the pull of the
conflicting implications of two models to which we are committed.
If the phenomenon we call “believing P” amounts to having a model
that can readily generate a judgment with the content P in an appropriate
evoking context in which the model is treated as apt, and we encode this
in the locution “S believes P,” clearly information is lost in the transla-
tion. Here we see the wisdom behind Dennett’s and Sellars’s cautions. Belief
ascriptions look like reports of assertions, and it is tempting to assume that
what is reported must, like an assertion, be propositional and episodic. But
really it is dispositional and normative. It may be a disposition to produce
a certain type of episode—a judgment—but its nature must be understood
in terms of the dynamics of models, and models are not judgments or other
sentence-sized units, and the processes by which they produce judgments
are not logical argument forms operating on sentence-sized units. More-
over, the analogy with assertions may tempt us to suppose that beliefs,
