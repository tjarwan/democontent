algorithmic method that ensures a way of integrating the factors.
Let us also consider a more serious sort of case, in which two models
applicable to the same situation are either incommensurable or inconsis-
tent. Wave and particle models of the same phenomenon—the propaga-
tion of light—lead to contrary predictions of behavior because they use
different representational systems, inference rules, and processes for simu-
lation. General relativity and quantum mechanics are inconsistent with
each other, as are classical and relativistic gravitation. We might view these
cases as analogous to the relationship between Euclidean and non-Euclid-
ean geometries: the models in question have incompatible representational
systems and rules. Each may be apt for a wide range of cases individually,
but their formal structure ensures that when they are combined, problems
will occur.
This may be puzzling from the standpoints of epistemology and truth
theory—I will examine whether it should be deeply and abidingly puzzling
in the final part of the book—but not from the perspective of psychology.
Each model must use some particular representational system. And what
representational system is employed will be constrained by (a) the demands
of the real-world invariants to be modeled; (b) the general constraints of
cognitive architecture (the specific set of types of representational systems
that are available to a mind like our own); and (c) pragmatic demands such
as utility for providing understanding, calculation, and prediction. As these
constraints operate to shape models individually, we have no assurance that
