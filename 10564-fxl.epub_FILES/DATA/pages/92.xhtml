6.1 Scale Models (Target Domains, Idealization, and Aptness)
We have all interacted at one time or another with scale models, ranging
from children’s toys to models of molecules used in a chemistry class. Scale
models are typified, first and foremost, by their structural similarity to what
they are models of, which we may refer to as the target of the model. A
model of a 1964 Mustang has approximately the same shape and propor-
tions as a genuine 1964 Mustang, only much smaller. In some such models,
the similarity is limited to the surface contours and perhaps the colors.
Other models may reproduce other features in miniature—the doors, trunk,
and hood may open realistically, the steering wheel may spin and turn
the wheels, and so on. A very fine model may even be composed of parts
matching those of the real car: little pieces shaped like the engine, the cata-
lytic converter, the struts and beams, and so on. Some may be powered, so
that the model can move on its own power.
But, of course, many other features will not be modeled. Even a powered
model car will likely run on batteries (of a shape and relative size unlike
those of an automobile battery) and not on a tiny internal combustion
engine. The parts will generally not be made of the same materials, and
hence the balance and the ratio of size to weight will be different, and so
on. In models designed for different purposes, some of these features may
in fact be modeled as well. Engineers designing a car or an airplane may
build a scale model first and use it to test properties like wind drag and
stability. If a model is to be used for such purposes, it will need to model
more properties of the target than will a child’s toy. Design models are also
different from models of production-model cars or airplanes in that they
are typically built before their targets.
Scale models are also used in the sciences and in science classrooms.

of different sizes and colors for the atoms and tubes for the bonds. These
are used to roughly approximate the geometric configurations of mole-
cules, and as such, they allow both students and researchers to understand
