221 Disunities of Knowledge, Science, and Understanding
We might summarize the second and fourth points by saying that the
mind has a kind of drive toward finding ways to unify a system of beliefs
and of understanding, respectively. This, of course, is a point famously
made by Kant. Kant’s attendant observation should be added: the mere fact
that we have a drive to unify what we believe or the ways we understand
things does not itself entail that we will in fact be able to do so. And to this
we may add a fifth point, which is also a variant on Kant: we have notions
of particular ways that knowledge and understanding might be brought
into greater unity. Kant had his own hypotheses about what these are and
their cognitive underpinnings. I would suggest that we see evidence of a
distinct set of these in the types of unification that philosophers and sci-
entists have in fact sought: global consistency of beliefs, reduction, and a
single all-encompassing theory.
I will explore, in chapter 14, a way of developing a sixth point, also
foreshadowed by Kant: the ways that features of cognitive architecture can
generate particular types of illusions, some of which have to do with the
mind’s drives to unify knowledge and understanding in particular ways. For
present purposes, it is perhaps enough to say that our dissatisfaction with
disunities is clearly a feature of something about our minds, but a psycho-
logical drive to unify by no means ensures that the project can be carried
out to its desired ideal conclusion.
Why might our beliefs and models, as we find them, be disunified? We
have already explored one part of an answer to this question with respect
to models: having various models of different aspects of the world is a good
strategy for building a capacity for understanding many features of the
world into finite minds, and each model must represent things in some
particular way. What we have yet to explore is how the ways models are
idealized can lead to disunities of understanding. If beliefs are constrained
by the ways we can frame thoughts through the resources of models, some
problems about disunities of belief may also be explained in the process. But
in general, there need be no deep mystery about why a given finite mind,
at any given time, might encounter conflicts among its beliefs and models.
No one ever thought that everyone’s system of beliefs was in fact compre-
hensive or consistent, and anyone who thought that about the models used
to frame such beliefs was psychologically naive. Claims about unification of
knowledge and understanding are claims not about our beliefs and models
as we find them but about what it might be possible to do to bring them to
greater unity.
