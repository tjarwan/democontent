6.6 Features of Models
Models are cognitive tools that allow ready understanding of some set of
features of the objects or systems they are models of. Models can be ana-
lyzed into parts or aspects that correspond to various features of the target
system: objects, properties, relations, processes, and transformations. They
thus provide a means of representing these features and reasoning about
them. A model does not simply represent one fact about an object or sys-
tem but provides a framework for representing various sorts of facts about
them, and reasoning about these. A model is thus not itself simply a rep-
resentation of an object or system but a kind of system for representation of
things about them.
It is particularly important to note that the kind of understanding
afforded by models is tied to the ways they are systems. And in this way
models are unlike lists of propositions. If you have a list of propositions and
delete one of them, you may lose some ability to infer things that depend
on that proposition, but all the others are retained. But if you delete a road
or rail connection from a map or remove the labels, the ability to use the
map as a way of navigating from one point to another is more globally
impaired. If you lose information about what kind of projection it is, or
what the various sorts of symbols and lines stand for, the map cannot be
used for all the same purposes and may become utterly useless as a map. If
you delete a section of code from a program, it may cease to operate at all or
result in vastly different behavior. If you alter the meaning of one theoreti-
cal term in a scientific theory or alter one of the laws, you generally must
make a host of other alterations as well, or else the resulting theory will
cease to have the same fit with the phenomena it is a theory of.
Models are useful for understanding in part because they provide a
stand-in for the target object or system in which a certain set of its features
is made available for inspection, reasoning, simulation, and manipulation
in a fashion that is readily grasped. This almost invariably involves ideal-
izing away from other features of the real-world system being modeled
to reduce complexity and hence cognitive load. Of course, there will be
problems for which the things that have been idealized away from will
matter, and this will affect the aptness of the model for thinking and
reasoning about such cases. The way features or aspects of a real-world
system are modeled may also diverge from their real nature, and this can
affect the aptness of a model as well. Often, such distorting idealizations
