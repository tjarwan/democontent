Of course, the term ‘model’ also has a number of existing uses, including an
importantly different use in cognitive science explored by Philip Johnson-
Laird (1983) and a philosophical use in logic, and I will attempt to situate
my notion of a model in relation to these and other existing uses in chapter
9, and to develop at greater length what I mean by a “mental model” in
chapters 6 and 7.
The claim that mental models are fundamental units of understanding
does not require us to deny that we also possess a more ecumenical form
of language-like thinking, in which concepts and beliefs related to differ-
ent domains can be brought together through syntactic construction and
formal inference. Indeed, this would be a strange thing to deny. Even if you
are skeptical about how much of our thinking takes the form of occurrent
sentence-like thoughts, there is no way around the fact that we think in
this fashion, at the least, when we think in a natural language such as Eng-
lish. Language-like thinking and model-based thinking are good for differ-
ent things and are complementary to each other. Models provide a basis for
semantic understanding and intuitive inference, but each model provides
insight only into its own domain. In my view, semantic understanding and
semantic inference are not really a part of language (or language-like think-
ing) at all. Our concepts and the things they allow us to represent and
reason about are primarily a product of the models we possess. Language
allows us to take these and combine them regardless of their models of
origin and to perform types of inference that are content neutral. Language
also allows us to make our thoughts explicit, and thereby to examine, test,
and refine them, both individually and in interpersonal dialectic. Much of
what is distinctive about human cognition would be impossible without
language or language-like thought. But language would be largely devoid
of content without models. (I develop these themes further in chap. 11.)
