In short, holism gets some things right: (1) some changes in meaning
do or ought to constitute changes in other concepts and in belief and vice
versa, and (2) what we believe is in some ways constitutively tied into the
semantics of the terms we use. But holism errs in being a completely global
and ecumenical thesis. Not all changes in belief and meaning have the
same sorts of implications for every other element of a person’s epistemic
space, and holism lacks the resources to deal with the differences.
16.3 The Multiple Lives of Concepts
Our discussions thus far might suggest a particular view of concepts and
their relationship to models. If a concept used within a model—such as
CHESS_KNIGHT or the concept MASS used in a relativistic model of gravitation—
is constitutively interdefined with the other concepts used in the model
and with the model’s rules and representational system, this seems to sug-
gest that each concept resides within a single model. It might additionally
be used in linguistic inferences that combine concepts drawn from differ-
ent models, and there may be associations between it and concepts drawn
from other models, but these are not constitutive of what concept it is. I will
refer to this thesis that each concept resides in a single model as the residen-
tial view of concepts.
If we accept the larger thesis that models form tightly integrated units,
wherein concepts and inferential commitments are constitutively intercon-
nected, this might recommend the residential view of concepts. But there
are also reasons to suspect that the residential view cannot be correct, or
at least that it cannot be the full story about concepts. On the one hand,
there is reason to think that some concepts—or something closely akin to
concepts—do not need to be situated in models at all but play a role more
directly connected to grouping stimuli into categories. Much of the litera-
ture on concepts in psychology is primarily about categorization, and it is
important to examine the relationship between the idea that concepts are
tools for categorization and the residential view. On the other hand, there
is reason to think that a single concept can play a role in more than one
model. At the least, we frequently use the same word in expressions of judg-
ments and inferences grounded in multiple models, and at first glance the
situation often looks not like a case of simple homonymy but like a single
concept straddling several models.
