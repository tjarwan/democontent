Scientific theories have their own proprietary ways of representing and rea-
soning about phenomena, tightly interconnected internally, but only more
loosely connected to everything else. This is precisely what I have been
saying about mental models more generally. I do not claim credit for this as
a completely novel insight. A number of writers before me have suggested
that we view understanding generally, even the infantâ€™s understanding
(Gopnik 1996), on the model of scientific theory. I simply prefer to put it
the other way around: scientific theories are a special case of how we rou-
tinely come to understand the world through mental models. Model-based
views have become increasingly popular in philosophy of science over the
past thirty years, and I will discuss the relation of some of these to my own
view in chapter 9. Here, however, I will try to develop the idea that scien-
tific understanding is based in mental models in the same sort of intuitive
fashion I have employed with other mental models.
In most of the types of models we have already explored, a model is
something that contains elements that are analogous to the objects, fea-
tures, relations, and processes found in their target systems. A modeling
system must have features that mirror key features of the target domain to
be apt. This is also the case with scientific theories and laws. These postulate
entities and their characteristic properties and relations, as well as invari-
ants expressed by laws and physical constants. Like other types of models,
theories and laws are a kind of surrogate that allow us to deal, in an ideal-
ized way, with features of the world in a fashion that affords both under-
standing and prediction. A theory of gravitation or a law like the inverse
square law is in this sense a model of the dynamics of gravitational influ-
ence. Theories and laws about collision are models of how objects behave
when they collide.
Like other models, scientific theories and laws are idealized in several
ways. First, they employ bracketing idealizations in what they exclude from
their scope. A gravitational model is concerned only with gravitational
interactions, not with collisions or electromagnetism. This affords insight
into one invariant in nature, but at the cost of resulting in a model that
is increasingly inadequate for kinematic predictions (i.e., predictions of
how objects will actually move) in cases where nongravitational forces
play larger roles. Second, they employ distorting idealizations. A particular
model of particle collisions, for example, may treat them as ideally elas-
tic. We know this assumption to be false, but such a model may still be
apt for low-energy collisions among some types of particles. Third, scien-
