We learn about systematic regularities we find in a wide range of things
we experience, use these regularities to organize our subsequent interac-
tions with them, and think about them off-line in ways faithful to their
actual behavior when they are not present. The formation of mental mod-
els on the basis of perception and interaction is actually a ubiquitous form
of human learning. The sole difference in the internalization of an external
model is that the property of being a model of something else (say, mole-
cules)—perhaps something we have never directly perceived and interacted
with—is transferred from the external model to the mental model. Starting
with external models that are then internalized helps us to get a basic grasp
of the idea of a mental model by analogy with the more familiar examples
of external models; but not all mental models are formed in this way, and
the relationship probably really works in the opposite direction: the ability
to work with external models likely depends on the ability to form and use
mental models. Indeed, many advocates of mental models believe that the
capacity to form mental models originally arose as a way of internalizing
regularities encountered in bodily engagement with the world.
Mental models can also be formed in other ways. As I have already sug-
gested, some are formed through experience without an external model.
Some are developmentally canalized. Some are formed through processes
of verbal learning, social interaction, or explicit theory formation. In the
ensuing sections, I describe examples of each.
