In the remainder of this chapter, I will first stake out a position on the
relationship between model-based cognition on the one hand and both
language and language-like thinking on the other and then briefly explore
a number of ways in which the addition of language and language-like
thinking augments and transforms the forms of thinking available through
model-based cognition alone.
11.2 Language and the Priority of Models
One clear feature of language and language-like thinking that distinguishes
them from models is their form. Language is a medium characterized by a
syntactic structure and a stock of lexical elements that can occupy syntactic
slots. Models, of course, also have their own kinds of “formal shape,” but it
is not one characterized by sentence-like structures or transformational and
inferential rules that operate on their syntax. In a model, the conceptual ele-
