Often, more than one model is available for what is, in some sense, the
same phenomenon: maps with different projections, scales, and types of
representations; scale models that use different scales and model different
features. Different models make different sorts of information about a sys-
tem available and are useful for different purposes, and we may need more
than one model if we are to have ways of accessing all the information they
jointly afford. But the very idealization of models that makes them useful
aids to understanding can also result in their being difficult to combine,
incommensurable, or inconsistent. Going between Mercator and sinusoidal
maps requires careful calculations, and it is probably easiest to move from
one to the other by going back to the spherical geometry from which they
are both derived.
6.7 Models as Cognitive Tools
All the types of models we have surveyed can be viewed as cognitive tools for
understanding, predicting, and interacting with phenomena in the world.
They can afford both understanding and practical grip on the phenomena
of which they are models. Some of them, like scale models, are physical
objects that can be perceived and manipulated. In the case of computer
models, our scientific understanding is encoded into a computer program
and its data structures, and the computer does much of the computational
work for us in tasks of simulation and prediction.
There is a sense in which one might say that all models are mental mod-
els already. That is, they are models only by dint of the fact that they are
used in a particular way, by human subjects, in the course of various cogni-
tive and practical tasks. Indeed, one might even go so far as to say that the
very notion of a model carries within it the idea that something is a model
only by relation to one or more minds. But this does not really help in get-
ting us toward a clarification of the claim that cognition is, quite generally,
accomplished through mental modeling, for that claim is not the claim
