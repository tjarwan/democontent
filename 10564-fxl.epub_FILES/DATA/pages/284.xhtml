Models as Integral Epistemic Units
A model-based view of understanding has implications for epistemology
because models turn out to be important epistemic units in their own right,
over and above beliefs. If models are themselves epistemically evaluable
and furthermore provide warrant for judgments and beliefs, an episte-
mological theory that deals exclusively with beliefs leaves out something
important. But incorporating mental models into epistemology also has a
further implication that will prove particularly important in assessing the
adequacy of two of the major epistemological theories, foundationalism
and holism.
Models are integral units. Often the commitments of a model can be
made explicit in the form of judgments and implication rules, but a model
is not simply an assemblage of beliefs and implication rules that can be
independently specified, as these are constitutively interrelated—or, as it
is sometimes phrased, “interdefined.” This has been a familiar claim about
scientific theories for several decades, and that alone should have been
enough to cause a rethinking of belief-based epistemology. If it is a general
characteristic of mental models, and models are the basis of understanding,
the implications are all the more urgent.
In philosophy of science, theories are commonly regarded as principal
units for confirmation. The credentials of relativistic claims about mass can-
not be evaluated apart from relativistic claims about space and time, and
vice versa. Likewise, applications of the theory to particular cases are war-
ranted by the aptness of the theory as a whole. An adequate epistemol-
ogy of science must accommodate the role of models—as integral units—in
receiving justification and conferring warrant. If similar considerations
apply to models generally, the implications for epistemology are consider-
ably broader. I argue in the next section that this has a serious implication
for the field of epistemology: neither foundationalism nor global coherent-
ism can be a fully adequate epistemological theory, because neither accords
model-sized units their proper role.
