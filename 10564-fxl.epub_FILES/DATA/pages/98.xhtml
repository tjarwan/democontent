The third map (fig. 6.3), an updated version of one I recall seeing on
trains when I lived in Boston, does not preserve the spatial relationships
between stations. Train lines are represented (in the original) by distinctive
colors as before, and stops with labeled dots. But the lines do not preserve
the geometric properties of the routes—the north–south and east–west
directions or the comparative distances between stops. The map represents
the rail system topologically rather than geometrically. This is actually an
elegant way of presenting the information needed to quickly determine
how to get from one rail station to another. In navigating by rail, one in fact
needs to think topologically.
The fourth map (fig. 6.4), of a more limited portion of Boston, is topo-
graphic. It represents isotopic elevations with specially colored lines and
marked with elevations, types of landform with colors, and landmarks with
labels. Topographic maps are particularly useful for reconstructing three-
dimensional features of a landscape from a two-dimensional map. While
their use is something of an acquired skill, they are useful for orienting
oneself to one’s environment, and for calculating elevation-dependent
affordances, like the steepness of a route, optimal locations for radio and
microwave towers, and patterns of water runoff.
Each of these four maps represents certain features of the area it models
and abstracts away from others. Just what is modeled differs from map to
map, and the choice of what to include and what to leave out has con-
sequences for the purposes for which each map may aptly be employed.
Indeed, it is safe to assume that each map was carefully designed with a
particular set of practical purposes in mind, and the mapmakers sought to
optimize each for use in a particular set of tasks. The kinds of information
needed by the driver, the rail passenger, the cross-country hiker, and the city
planner are not the same. And a given type of information is made most
readily available by excluding from the map all unnecessary information.
Consider now a second set of maps (figs. 6.5 and 6.6). These are both
maps of the surface of the globe. Their most obvious difference is in how
they project the surface of a sphere onto a flat surface. The skin of a spheri-
cal surface cannot simply be flattened onto a plane without distortion. One
must either stretch it, or make cuts in it, or both. The first map, a Mercator
projection (fig. 6.5), is a result of something like stretching the surface. It

line is equally long, stretching from left to right. Since different latitude
