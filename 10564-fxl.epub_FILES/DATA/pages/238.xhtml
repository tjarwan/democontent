13.2 Intuitive and Counterintuitive Judgments
There may be more than one way a judgment might come to seem intuitive
or counterintuitive. I will be offering hypotheses about one way this might
happen, but I will speak of it, to avoid continual hedging, as the way it hap-
pens. The hypotheses, in their general forms, are the following:
1. A judgment is intuitive with respect to a model M if its truth can be
“read off” the rules of M.
2. A judgment is counterintuitive with respect to a model M if it violates
the rules of M in a fashion that is detectable—for example, because the
rule violation triggers a cognitive dissonance.
There are several ways a model-based inference process can lead to a judg-
ment being, and being recognized as being, intuitive or counterintui-
tive with respect to a model, and I will explore several of these by way of
examples.

basis of their beliefs and desires.” It is probably rare that anyone who is
not involved in philosophy or the cognitive sciences would ever explicitly
entertain judgments with these abstract contents at all. Even when phi-
losophers or scientists do so, it is often in the context of debating specific
theories (such as eliminative materialism) that deny these theses. To com-
