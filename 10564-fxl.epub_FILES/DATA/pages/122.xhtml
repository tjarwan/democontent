Let us now take it a step farther. Suppose I am looking into some ques-
tion about my house by looking through the house itself—say, I want to
know how many outlets there are in the bedrooms. Consider how different
this task is if I am asking it with respect to my own house as compared with
being deposited in the doorway of an unknown building. In the case of my
own house, I know the layout, I know where I am situated with respect to
that layout, and I know which rooms are the bedrooms—and hence, implic-
itly, the efficient routes to get there. In the case of an unknown house, there
is a lot I have to explore and figure out to know how to carry out the task.
Having a mental model that supplies this needed background information
radically simplifies the task. Indeed, it seems to me that forming a mental
model that includes information such as what rooms are bedrooms is a nec-
essary part of the task of counting the number of outlets in the bedrooms of
a previously unfamiliar house.
Let us now turn to another form of imagination: creative imagination.
Suppose I am an architect, and my task is to build a house that answers to a
particular set of parameters. I do not do this by first slapping together walls
randomly and then exploring. Rather, I conceive the house imaginatively,
imagine the consequences of my design, amend the design in light of these,
draw up blueprints, and then turn it over to the builders. The architect may
understand a house quite well and imagine it quite concretely before the
foundation has been laid. Blueprints, cardboard models, and CAD models
may indeed assist in this process. But an architect with a detailed imagina-
tion may well be able to conceive and design a home without these aids.
