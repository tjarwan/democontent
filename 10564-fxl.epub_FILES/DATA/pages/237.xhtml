Other psychologists speak of concepts and judgments as being intuitive
or counterintuitive. In cognitive science of religion, Paul Bloom (2004,
2007) claims that both children and adults are “intuitive dualists.” Pas-
cal Boyer and Justin Barrett, by contrast, claim that supernatural concepts
like those of immaterial souls and ghosts are “minimally counterintuitive”
(Boyer 2001; Barrett 2004a, 2004b, 2009). Calling a judgment or a concept
“intuitive” here seems to mean that it is “intuitively recommended”—that
is, seemingly true, plausible, or, in the case of concepts, unproblematic.
‘Counterintuitive’ seems to mean the opposite of this: a counterintuitive
judgment or concept is one that comes with an internal sense that there
must be something peculiar about it.
These references to contemporary psychology, however, do not tell us
much that we did not know before. Beyond the important claim that exper-
tise is characteristically exercised through intuitive processes, they largely
just confirm the distinctions we have already developed. The claim that
intuitive processes are “automatic” amounts to little more than the claim
that they are not the kind of processes found in explicit reasoning, in which
we can control and monitor the steps. The fact that they are fast—that is,
fast by contrast with methodical explicit reasoning—may be no more than a
consequence of the fact that the latter sort of process is slow and labori-
ous. Moreover, intuitive processes do not always seem to be fast: the devel-
opment of a creative idea or an intellectual insight might require a great
deal of time. When I reach an impasse in writing, I often do something
else—take a walk or sleep on it—allowing unconscious parts of my mind to
work on the problem. Sometimes it is a matter of hours, days, or months
before an answer pops into my awareness. Of course, precisely because the
process is unconscious, it is difficult to determine just when it is at work
or how long it takes. It may be that the actual process takes milliseconds
