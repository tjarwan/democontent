The situation is different in the case of quantum complementarity.
Wave and particle models represent phenomena differently, and wavelike
behavior is different from particle-like behavior. In classical optics, particle
theories (Newton) and wave theories (Huygens) were assumed to be com-
petitor accounts, and experiments were designed to attempt to settle the
question of which was correct. The underlying assumption was that there
must be a single model that captures the “real nature” of light and can be
applied to all the cases. Classical optics became absorbed into a broader
theory of electromagnetism, and classical electrodynamics was superseded
by quantum theories. But the availability—indeed, the need for—both wave
and particle models persists in quantum theory. In some experimental set-
ups, phenomena are aptly described by wave models, in others by particle
models. This description, however, comes with an important caveat: the
experimental setups are mutually exclusive, and so quantum duality is
not understood in terms of two rival accounts of nature. But neither are
the wave and particle models descriptions of separate forces, like gravity
and magnetism.
Niels Bohr proposed the doctrine of complementarity to account for this
counterintuitive situation. His description of the issues reflects both cogni-
tive and pragmatic elements of his analysis.
However far the [quantum physical] phenomena transcend the scope of classical physical
explanation, the account of all evidence must be expressed in classical terms. The argu-
ment is simply that by the word “experiment” we refer to a situation where we can
tell others what we have done and what we have learned and that, therefore, the ac-
count of the experimental arrangements and of the results of the observations must
be expressed in unambiguous language with suitable application of the terminology
of classical physics.
