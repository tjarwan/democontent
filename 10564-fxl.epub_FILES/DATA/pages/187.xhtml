While other species doubtless possess a modeling engine, and surely
many can form models of things that we cannot because they are attuned
to different affordances in the world through different perceptual organs
and forms of embodiment, human beings vastly outstrip other species in
the number and variety of things we can form models of. Perhaps this is
due in part to some difference in the neural mechanisms that ultimately
underwrite the formation of models or the greater sheer volume of such
resources available in the human cortex. But it is just as plausibly related
to other abilities we possess that are complementary to model formation.
Capacities for tool use have often been singled out as prime factors lead-
ing to distinctively human intelligence, and the ability to use tools (and
particularly to readily learn their uses, invent new ones, and pass them
on through social instruction) seems to go hand in hand with the abil-
ity to learn new models. In one way, this is just obvious: one needs to
learn a model for the use of a particular tool to employ it effectively. But
beyond this, there are connections to broader systems of models. On the
one hand, we donâ€™t just hammer or saw for the sake of hammering and
sawing. These activities make sense, and the conditions for their optimiza-
tion become manifest, only in the context of some further set of projects
that require them. Hammering and sawing make sense only in the broader
context of building things through carpentry and the requirements these
impose on shaping and joining particular types of materials. More gener-
ally, having an expanded range of interests, and models related to those
interests, creates a market for a capacity to invent and use tools. On the
other hand, tools expand our ways of recognizing and exploiting the affor-
