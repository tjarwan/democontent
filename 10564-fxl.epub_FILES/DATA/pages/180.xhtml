Real Patterns, Idealized Models
Models may aim at tracking real patterns, but some of them fail. And those
that succeed in biological terms—that is, track them well enough to be of
use to the animal—may not track them perfectly. Which is to say, once
again, that models are idealized. We have already addressed one type of
idealization: the bracketing idealizations involved in isolating one set of
features by ignoring others. But we have not addressed distorting idealiza-
tions, such as treating bodies as point masses or remapping wavelengths of
light onto a subjective color space. Distorting idealizations can, of course,
be a bad thing when we are confronted with situations in which the distor-
tions matter. But in other cases, the very features that count as distortions
can also enable easier comprehension and reasoning. And from a biological
perspective, both evolved cognitive systems and ones produced by forms of
learning that are not regimented like the sciences are shaped by processes
that converge on good-enough models rather than ones that are optimal
for purposes of exactitude.
Sometimes idealizations that distort by making simplifying assumptions
are needed to make problems computationally tractable. And even when
a more exacting model is computationally tractable in principle, it may
not be so for every actual mind. Some people can grasp Newton’s mechan-
ics but not Einstein’s; others cannot comprehend mathematical physics of
any sort. It is a good thing that the vast majority of us have more intuitive
models (learned or unlearned) that allow us to predict the trajectories of
falling bodies and to throw footballs. And these are good things for the
mathematical physicist to have, too. Mathematical physics is more apt for
rocket science but is not very apt for avoiding falling objects. Because there
are several epistemological and practical factors that go into the aptness of
a model, different models of the same phenomenon can be apt for different
purposes, and hence it is good to have more.

to form models that are useful to them, and also that some of these models
provide more exacting theoretical understanding. We have to begin with
models that have a great deal of us built into them—affordance models,
ways of knowing how to move about the world and how to manipulate
objects with the particular types of bodies we possess, models that are cen-
