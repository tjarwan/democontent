Semantic and Psychological Typing
This leads quite naturally to the second observation: a distinction between
semantic and psychological typing. There is a perfectly reasonable sense
in which, for purposes of semantics, one might wish to type concepts
according to their semantic properties, even at a maximally fine-grained
level. Many philosophers have taken this approach, some concluding that
it is unlikely that two people share many concepts, because there are
likely to be many fine-grained differences in the semantic properties of
your concept of something versus mine. But the foregoing discussion sug-
gests that we need to think about the identity of concepts as psychological
entities in a different way. I coin a new concept on the basis of thinking
I have encountered a new phenomenon that is not captured by my exist-
ing concepts. I do not yet understand it well, but a new psychological
structure is created—a kind of stub—that has the function of tracking that
sort of phenomenon. I then explore and learn, and different hypotheses
about it are encoded in my concept of it. The “concept” in this psy-
chological sense is a psychological continuant, whose semantic properties
(particularly its sense) can change over time. Alex formed a concept

at some point, probably through early encounters with dogs. To this were
attached various semantic properties, such as that dogs are pets. When
he learns the disconcerting truth that not all dogs are someone’s dog, it is
that concept (in the psychologically individuated sense) that will undergo
revision.
Stepping back a bit, we can see that this is really an application of the
basic insights of cognitive pluralism to theoretical models of concepts—
say, those that individuate concepts semantically and those that do so
psychologically. We speak of these as accounts, views, models, or theories
of concepts out of an assumption that there is something real that they
are all seeking to characterize. But they characterize “it” in different ways,
emphasizing different things that provide distinct insights and forms of
reasoning, but idealized in ways that can generate puzzles when we try
to put them back together.
