Redundancy: Expected and Unexpected Benefits
One type of multiplication of models—redundancy—has particular advan-
tages of its own. One thing it does, of course, is to make an organism more
resilient in the face of injuries that compromise one of the redundant sys-
tems. But redundant systems do not function exactly alike. Redundant per-
ceptual systems like those for extracting information about depth respond
to different cues and hence are useful under different optical conditions.
Redundant cognitive systems may represent their common target differ-
ently, thus conferring different inferences and breakdown patterns. And
when two systems with the same target produce conflicting results, this
provides impetus for deeper empirical and theoretical investigation, both of
the phenomena in the world they aim to track and of the cognitive systems
themselves. Redundant systems can be productive of greater understanding.
Multiple Idealized Models, Epistemic Triangulation, and Compensation
for Forced Error
More generally, given that an organism is using idealized models of particu-
lar domains, it is naturally beneficial to have an abundance of models. On
the one hand, the bracketing idealizations of models imply that multiple
models are needed to cover different aspects of the world. On the other
hand, as we have seen, even a single model of one target domain can be
supplemented by another, idealized in a different way and employing a dif-
ferent representational system. Different models can compensate for one
another’s limitations. The result is a better understanding of reality, not in
the form of a single broader or more accurate model (though sometimes
that results as well) but through a kind of epistemic triangulation of the world
through a number of different vantage points and lenses.
In the extreme case, any given model may be subject to forced errors: mis-
takes that are ensured by the way it processes and represents information.
