How are we able to make sense of conceptual change? Here we seem to
be presented with a dilemma. On the one hand, a semantic approach to con-
cepts inclines us to the view that a concept is constituted by its semantic
properties, broadly construed to include constitutive inferences. If concepts
C
1
and C
2
have different semantic properties, they are different concepts.
On the other hand, we seem to be able to make at least minor adjustments
to a concept. A child may initially suppose that dogs are domestic animals
and that each dog is someone’s pet. This might even be so deeply bound
up with his understanding of dogs as to be a constitutive implication of his
concept
DOG. If this is the case, when he is disabused of this assumption,
his concept
DOG undergoes a certain amount of revision. It does not seem
natural to say that he lost (or stopped using) one concept and acquired (and
began using) another.
The dilemma can also be posed as a problem about whether two people
share the same concept. Four-year-old Alex and his older sister Betty talk
about dogs frequently. Alex’s concept of dogs involves an implicit assump-
tion that dogs are people’s pets. He would be quite baffled the first time
someone told him that a certain dog was nobody’s dog. Betty understands
that there are wild dogs, feral dogs, stray dogs, and the like. Do Alex and
Betty have “the same concept”? On the one hand, we might be inclined
to say no, because the concepts they express using the word ‘dog’ have dif-
ferent semantic properties. On the other hand, they are able to talk with
each other about the same class of animals without difficulty, and if Betty
tries to disabuse Alex of his assumption that dogs must be pets, Alex may
at first react with confusion or even disbelief, but he knows well enough
what class of things she is talking about: the same class of things picked
out by his concept
DOG. (Indeed, it is precisely because he is able to do this
that he is perplexed by her claim, which violates the constitutive rules of
his concept
DOG.)
