Here is how [the intentional stance] works: first you decide to treat the object
whose behavior is to be predicted as a rational agent; then you figure out what be-
liefs that agent ought to have, given its place in the world and its purpose. Then you
figure out what desires it ought to have, on the same considerations, and finally you
predict that this rational agent will act to further its goals in light of its beliefs. A
little practical reasoning from the chosen set of beliefs and desires will in many—but
not all—instances yield a decision about what the agent ought to do; that is what you
predict the agent will do. (61)
Dennett agrees with intentional realists like Fodor that we can predict and
explain people’s actions on the basis of their beliefs and desires with striking
success—and, moreover, that we have no alternative way of doing so with
any hope of similar success. But he does not agree that what we are doing
in such predictions and explanations is positing inner causal mechanisms
involving states or events called “beliefs” and “desires” or reasoning pro-
cesses that operate on them. The intentional stance is a predictive strategy,
and it does require that we ascribe beliefs and desires. But what one is doing
when one ascribes a belief using this strategy is specifying what a being
must be committed to, given its circumstances, if it is to behave rationally. Any
system that can systematically be predicted in this fashion thereby counts
as an “intentional system,” regardless of what underlying mechanisms pro-
duce those patterns of behavior. And if the intentional strategy works with
respect to a particular system, it is getting at something real about that
system—not (necessarily) real occurrent states called “beliefs” (i.e., judg-
ments), “desires,” and “reasoning,” but real (i.e., empirically robust) pat-
terns, patterns that are revealed only when we adopt the intentional stance
(Dennett 1991b). Moreover, that such patterns are revealed only from a
particular interpretive stance does not render the interpretations fictitious
or mere matters of taste: physical and functional properties are likewise
revealed only from distinct interpretive/predictive stances, which Dennett
calls the physical stance and the design stance, respectively.

which a system is able to behave in rational ways, has the consequence
that his view is actually compatible with what he calls Fodor’s “industrial
strength realism,” which treats intentional states and processes as occurrent
states that are central elements of cognitive architecture. But it is also com-
