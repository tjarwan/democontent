Moreover, a being that thought only in general and basic physical terms
would not be very good at interacting with its environment. The task of fig-
uring out what is edible or dangerous would be impossibly cumbersome—
and would not even make sense for an animal that did not also have models
of feeding and predation. And since there are domains that are not special
cases of physics—mathematics, ethics, aesthetics—the organism would not
be able to think in those terms. Which is to say, it would not be able to
think about all the things we can think about. In short, appeals to the ideal
of unification are irrelevant to psychology: unification is an achievement
that presupposes preexisting models, and a model that is “unifying” in the
sense of capturing the causal or compositional basis for other phenomena
is not necessarily a substitute for them in thinking or interacting with the
world. What is important for an organism is the ability to efficiently track
patterns that are salient, not those that are fundamental, and most of the
salient patterns are not fundamental. Being able to understand increasingly
fundamental theories allows us to think about new things, and to think
about them in illuminating ways, but it does not diminish the importance
of thinking about them in other ways.
