For readers who take an interest in philosophical history, I take it that
I am developing an insight that has appeared several times before in the
history of philosophy. Aristotle claimed that a thing is always a “this-such”
(tode ti). I interpret this in cognitive terms: to think of a thing (as a thing, i.e.,
as a particular individual) is to have a concept of it with components that
(1) aim to track it as a particular individual (the “this-ness”) and (2) ascribe
particular attributes to it (the “such-ness”). My claim also seems closely
akin to Husserl’s statement that the core of the sense of a thought about
a thing (what he calls the “noematic Sinn”) includes not only descriptive
components but also what he calls the “pure x,” which I take to mean an
ostensive namelike element that has the function of tracking an individual
(Husserl 1913/1989, §131).
I think that there is a similar story to be told about other kinds of con-
cepts: concepts of kinds, properties, relations, and processes. Or, more
carefully, that at least some concepts of kinds, properties, relations, and pro-
cesses have an analogous feature. The situation seems clearest with kind
concepts. Our minds are disposed not only to think in sortal kinds but also
to form concepts for new sortal kinds quickly when confronted with some-
thing that does not seem to fit into the kinds we already know about. When
we encounter an unfamiliar phenomenon—particularly a new kind of ani-
mal or plant, though those are not the only cases—we quickly form a sort
of stub of a kind concept. This involves an implicit assumption that there
is a kind of thing there to be tracked, though as yet we do not understand
much about it. There is thus a kind of ostensive act in which we cast our
semantic hook, hoping it latches onto a kind, and then over time reel it in,
gaining more concrete understanding of it, which we incorporate into our
concept of it. The initial tacit assumption may, of course, prove mistaken:
what we observed may be a member of a known kind in an unfamiliar state,
or we may have mistaken what are really two kinds for one, or there may
not be a phenomenon robust enough to count as a kind at all. But the abil-
ity to acquire greater understanding about a kind—or more broadly about
any sort of phenomenon—depends on our first being able to fix our osten-
sive hook into it as a target to be tracked by a concept, and to keep tracking
the same target in spite of semantic and epistemic changes.
