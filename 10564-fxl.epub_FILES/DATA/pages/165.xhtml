9.3 Models in Theoretical Cognitive Science
The claim that model-based understanding is a key feature of human cogni-
tive architecture is found most clearly in artificial intelligence and theoreti-
cal cognitive science. Some writers use the term ‘model’—or some variant
of it, such as “scale model” (Waskan 2006) or “graphical model” (Danks
2014)—as their preferred technical term. But there is an equally large tradi-
tion that postulates systematic nonpropositional structures that form the
basis of ordinary reasoning, but using other terminology, such as Minsky
and Papert’s (1972) “micro-worlds,” Newell and Simon’s (1972) “problem-
spaces,” and the semantic networks, frames, and scripts I described in chap-
ter 4. Those projects had a great deal in common with my model-based
account, particularly the ideas that understanding comes in domain-sized
chunks and is stored in a nonpropositional form. Indeed, I count them as
important pioneers of a model-based approach, and my acquaintance with
them from the 1980s probably provided a substantial part of the inspira-
tion for my own ideas. I will not, however, redescribe them here, as chap-
ter 4 contains lengthy descriptions of some of them, particularly Minsky’s
notion of a frame.
