7.5 Social Contexts
One of the better-known projects in AI knowledge representation was Roger
Schank’s script-based simulation of an understanding of dining in a res-
taurant (Schank and Abelson 1977). Like other frame-type models, this
involved special types of representations for types of objects (menus, uten-
sils, servingware, food and drink, checks, tables, chairs) and activities (arriv-
ing at the host or hostess station, seating the customer, taking the order,
bringing the order, bringing the check, paying the check, tipping), as well
as special social roles (customer, host or hostess, waiter or waitress, chef).
The “script” part of the program consisted in a stereotyped sequence (or
flowchart) of events in their typical order (see fig. 4.7 in chap. 4).
Schank’s program modeled a particular type of social context (albeit one
that allows further variations for different types of restaurants), but its gen-
eral features seem to be required for understanding any type of social con-
text. In our understanding of social contexts, people are characteristically
understood through roles, often roles particular to that context. Knowing
that someone is acting as a waiter, a judge, a coach, or a babysitter sets
up expectations about the things he or she is likely to do. Some of these
expectations may be mere associations or prejudices—I might expect wait-
ers to have stage ambitions as a result of living in an area where out-of-work
actors commonly take work waiting tables—but the more important type
are expectations tied to the role. The expectation that a waiter will bring
what I ordered is not a mere association or prejudice but part of my under-
standing of the role of being a waiter. These expectations are generally inde-
pendent of who is serving in that role: any waiter should bring the check at
the end of the meal. And our understanding of what that person will likely
do within the role will generally offer little guidance in predicting how he
