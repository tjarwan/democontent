On closer scrutiny, however, it does make sense to ask questions about
whether one is entitled to all the assumptions implicit in the model one
is using. I recall a stage in learning chess as a child when I had observed
pawns moving two spaces but had not recognized that this is permitted
only on their initial moves. My error was quickly pointed out after I made
what I had supposed to be a clever (but in fact illegal) move; and in ret-
rospect I can see that I had incautiously made what turned out to be the
wrong generalization based on my observations and whatever instruction
I had received in the game at that point. It may well be that this happened
so early in my acquaintance with chess that I had not yet fully formed a
model (even an incorrect one) of the game and was still operating at the
level of memorized rules. But even so, if there had not been early occasion
for the error to be corrected, I might very well have formed a deviant model
of chess. Forming a deviant model is itself still a matter of getting the model
wrong. But we might go on to ask questions about the epistemic merits and
faults of the process through which I formed the assumption that a pawn
can generally move one or two spaces forward. One might debate whether
my internalization of a model with that particular set of deviant rules was
justified—I seem to recall a feeling of outrage at the time that my teacher
had not made it clear that pawns could move two spaces only on the first
move—but the very fact that there is such a question to be asked implies
that epistemic evaluation has a place here.

can aptly be applied. I recall trying to order wine at a fancy restaurant where
such orders are handled not by the waiter or waitress but by a wine specialist
called a sommelier. The model of restaurant dining I was accustomed to was
one in which a single person takes all types of orders for food and drink,
which is indeed an apt model to use in the vast majority of dining establish-
ments I visit. I assumed it would aptly be applied at this particular restaurant

send over the sommelier. It was an understandable mistake; I had no way
