What I wish to emphasize about these three skeptical theses, however,
is this: regardless of whether one favors the milder or the stronger skeptical
reading of Hume, he is clearly claiming that our deeply seated tendency to
think of a world of independent objects, which endure through time and
through changes in how we perceive them and enter into causal relations
with one another (and with us as perceivers), is somehow a product of cer-
tain peculiarities of the mind that he styles “illusions.” He is committed,
at the least, to the view that claims about external objects and causation
outstrip any kind of evidence we might have, and hence any sense that we
have knowledge of objects or causation is illusory. He may be committed to
the further view that they involve errors of imputing to the world features
that are really only features of how we think about it.
We might characterize the stronger view as positing that the imputa-
tion of causation or objects existing independently in the world constitutes
a projective illusion, in which features that are really only features of the
mind are “read onto” the world. Milder versions of such a view had of
course already been suggested by philosophers like Descartes and Locke,
who claimed that secondary qualities like color are mistakenly assumed to
be properties of objects but really are merely ways objects affect us through
our senses. But Descartes and Locke had each assumed that there really is
a world of substances that stand in causal relations with one another and
with human observers. We might be prone to illusions about what proper-
ties substances have in their own right, but these illusions could be cor-
rected through philosophical reasoning, and basic metaphysical categories
like substance and causation reflect the real metaphysical structure of the
world. Hume seems to have rejected this sort of realist assumption, taking
a more comprehensive cognitivist turn. Kant, whose views were at least
equally cognitivist, made it a major part of his project in the Critique of Pure
