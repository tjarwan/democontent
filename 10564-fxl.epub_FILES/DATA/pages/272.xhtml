15.1 What Are Beliefs?
Back in chapter 2, I made a terminological decision to speak of occurrent
states involving endorsement of propositional content as judgments. Such
states have sometimes been called “occurrent beliefs” as well, a terminol-
ogy generally used in contradistinction to “dispositional beliefs.” I further
decided to use the term ‘belief’ broadly, to include both judgments and dis-
positional beliefs, whatever the latter might turn out to be. In subsequent
discussions of intuition and cognitive illusion, I concentrated principally
on the notion of judgment rather than that of belief, because those chap-
ters dealt with the production of occurrent states from models. We can
and will discuss the question of how judgments are warranted; however,
epistemologists do not generally confine their discussions of knowledge to
occurrent states but employ a notion of belief that encompasses both occur-
rent and dispositional beliefs. In this chapter we will thus need to discuss
the relationship in which models stand to beliefs as well as to judgments.
I touched on this topic in chapter 13, in conjunction with a discussion of
intuition. Here I take it up again in the context of accounts of beliefs and
other intentional states.
As a first step, I would like to explore how mental models might shed
further light on the psychological underpinnings of what we describe as
beliefs. To begin, let us reconsider several ways of understanding what we
are doing when we ascribe beliefs.
