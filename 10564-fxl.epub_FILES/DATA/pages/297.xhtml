16.1 Models and Semantic Value
For several decades, philosophers of science have generally accepted the
claim that theoretical terms in a science are constitutively interdefined
with other terms in the theory, and their meanings—at least in a broad
but plausible sense of ‘meaning’ that includes the constitutive inferences
one is disposed to draw simply by understanding the terms—are derived
from the theory as well. But scientific theories are not the only forms of
understanding of which this is true. It is part and parcel of having mastered
the concept
CHESS_BISHOP that one understands that there is such a thing as
the game of chess, that bishops are chess pieces, and that they can move
diagonally within the game. To grasp the concept
WAITER, one must under-
stand that there are dining contexts in which some people have the role
of serving food to others and that waiters take customers’ orders and bring
food and drink. Indeed, it is often through observing such behavior that
one decides that a particular concept is applicable in a given case. If I see
that two people have drawn an 8 x 8 board in the sand and are moving vari-
ous rocks between spaces, I might form the judgment that they are playing
chess, and decide that one piece is a bishop because it is moved diagonally.
In a restaurant, I may conclude that someone is a waiter because I observe
him taking orders and delivering food.
The idea that the meaning of a word or concept is partly determined
by the inferences in which it is used is central to inferentialist semantics.
But not all the inferences we are disposed to draw are plausibly regarded
as bound to the semantic values of concepts. On the one hand, there are
syntactically based inferences. If I make a syllogistic inference based on
the syntactic properties of the propositions, the semantic values of the
concepts involved are irrelevant to the “inferential disposition” that is at
work, and I can make or be disposed to such inferences without it having
any constitutive effect on my concept
WAITER. On the other hand, we make
inferences that are based in mere association. Someone who lives in New
York or Los Angeles may associate waitstaff with out-of-work actors and
actresses and perhaps even believe that all waitstaff have dramatic aspira-
tions. This, however, is not part of the semantics of
WAITER. Rather, it is based
in some additional belief, or perhaps merely in a bias that I would not even
reflectively endorse as a belief. Confronted with evidence that some or even
most waitstaff have no dramatic ambitions would not force me to revise my
concept
WAITER; it would at most disabuse me of a bias. Connections to some
