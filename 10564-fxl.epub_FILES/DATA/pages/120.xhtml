I can, additionally, do other things in imagination. I can imagine things
that never happened taking place there. What would it have been like
if Martin Luther King had visited us? I can certainly picture him there,
imagine how many steps it would take to rise from the sofa to meet him
at the entrance, and so on. What would the room have looked like had
the walls been painted black? How would things have looked different if
a window had been walled off, or if the porch that adjoined the room had
been enclosed and the doorway to it been made into a doorless arch? What
would the room have looked like from the perspective of a camera in a cor-
ner on the ceiling, or from above, if the ceiling were transparent? Imagin-
ing these things is almost as easy as imagining things from perspectives that
I probably actually did occupy, which suggests that there are capacities at
work that are quite different from episodic memory.
Now try a slightly different exercise: imagine a blueprint of the first floor
of your house. For me, this takes a second or two to coalesce, but it hap-
pens quite readily. This is interesting, as what is present in a blueprint is a
view we seldom if ever actually have of an actual physical house. We do not
learn to understand blueprints by actually exploring houses bodily from
above. But it is a form of understanding that comes easily to many of us.
And, at least for some of us, it feels easy and natural to go from a blueprint
perspective to the perspective of being situated in a room and vice versa.
The information that is directly afforded in one is different from that of
the other, but the mind seems quite adept at translating from one to the
other. Give me a blueprint of a house, and I can fairly quickly form a rough
image of what the view would look like from a given position and angle.
The ability to transition back and forth is surely a form of know-how and
is closely connected with ways of being embedded bodily in the world. But
the “blueprint view” is not something we learn by flying above roofless
buildings. Rather, we have some kind of competence for moving back and
forth between an abstract schematic understanding of a physical layout
and a sort of situated geometrical thinking that allows perspective taking.
