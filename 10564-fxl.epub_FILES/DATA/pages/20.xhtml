A particularly influential version of inferentialism, called holism, holds
that the semantic value of every concept is constitutively interrelated with
that of every other concept, and also with every belief and inferential dis-
position, so that a change in any concept, belief, or inferential disposi-
tion ramifies into a change to all the others as well (Quine 1951; Davidson
1967). Holists deny that there can be a principled demarcation between
concepts and beliefs, or between semantics and epistemology. Aristotle’s
discovery that whales are mammals rather than fish might be characterized
as a change of the semantic value of the concept
WHALE; but it might equally
well be characterized as a change in beliefs about whales, or as a change in
the inferences we are disposed to make about things we classify as whales.
But holists, like atomists, still speak of concepts, beliefs, and inferences as
basic units of cognition, even though they hold that their content and their
warrant are conferred globally rather than locally.
Logic and theory of reasoning are concerned primarily with patterns
of reasoning, particularly deductive arguments. Inferences and arguments
are composed out of beliefs and propositions arranged in a particular logi-
cal form, and logic is particularly concerned with argument forms that are
truth preserving—that is, ones in which, if the premises are true, the con-
clusions must be true as well.
