The basic explanation suggested by a model-based approach is that the
pathway from novice to expert involves the formation of mental mod-
els that allow for quick and apt responses that do not require conscious
propositional thinking. The learning pathway often involves starting with
explicit representation of propositions and rules that approximate the fea-
tures of a model—perhaps in some cases, such as scientific theories, there is
even a propositional articulation of a preexisting model available as an aid
to learning—but becoming an expert requires the formation of something
different, a mental model of the system. The expert’s understanding is better
than the novice’s, because the characterizations the novice uses are approx-
imations of an expert’s model, and because the novice may not be fully
competent even at using the approximations. The expert’s performance is
effortless and does not involve conscious stepwise reasoning because her
answer is produced by the fast and nonpropositional processes of model-
based cognition. The expert’s judgment feels certain because it is driven by
the implicit rules of the model, and because she has a history of using the
model effectively.
13.5 Models and Dispositional Beliefs
A model-based account may also help to shed light on the nature of beliefs
that are not judgments. These are often characterized in terms of “disposi-
tions” or “normative commitments,” but neither of these characterizations
is helpful as a step toward a psychological theory. The ways that we have
been talking about the intuitive production of judgments can help us to see
why we are “disposed” to produce or assent to certain types of judgments
and how such dispositions can be normative.
