2 A Standard Philosophical View of Cognitive Architecture
1 . Definitions of “cognitive architecture” (and, more broadly, “cognitive science”)
vary on the question of just what mental, psychological, and neural phenomena fall
within its scope. Some (e.g., Pylyshyn 1991) limit its scope to phenomena involving
semantic content, while others use it as a general term for the basic functional archi-
tecture of mind and brain. These uses differ, for example, on the question of whether
connectionist architectures should be counted as cognitive architectures.
2 . Dennett, of course, is aware of all of this and addresses such topics in his 1977
review of Fodor’s The Language of Thought.
3 . Classic precedents for this approach include Heidegger 1927/1996; Merleau-
Ponty 1945/1962; Gibson 1979. Some more recent important publications include
M. Anderson 1997; Chemero 2009; Clark 1997, 2008; Gallagher 2005; Gallagher and
Zahavi 2008; Lakoff and Johnson 1999; Noë 2004; Shapiro 2004; Silberstein and
Chemero 2011; Thompson 2007; Thompson and Stapleton 2008; Varela, Thomp-
son, and Rosch 1991; Zahavi 2005.

7 Mental Models
1 . My use of the term ‘affordance’ here may be closer to Donald Norman’s (1988)
usage than to Gibson’s. For Gibson, an affordance was a perfectly objective feature
of the environment that also happened to be relevant to the organism. Norman’s
characterization locates affordances more at the interface of organism and
environment.
