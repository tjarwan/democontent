7.8 Core and Folk Systems
At the other end of the spectrum of cognitive sophistication from scien-
tific models, we find early appearing, developmentally canalized forms of
thinking such as the core and folk systems described in chapter 4. These are
each centered on domains that are constituted by their respective internal
ontologies, which are represented and reasoned about in distinctive ways.
2
The core object system, for example, has an internal ontology with a single
generic class of things (“Objects”), properties (location, shape, motion),
relations (being in contact, colliding), and processes (causal launching,
moving in a continuous path) and is characterized by constitutive “rules”
that guide anticipations—for example, that Objects are cohesive and con-
tiguous, move in continuous paths, and go into motion only when acted
on. It seems natural to view at least the core object and agency systems
(and perhaps the core geometric system) as models of broad and generic
domains. They involve both bracketing and distorting idealizations and
employ representational systems with different representational types and
rules of inference.
There are several core systems and several folk systems. They latch onto
different systemic features of the physical, mental, social, and biological
world and provide systematic frameworks for representing facts involving
those features. Two systems can often be applied to the selfsame object:
Socrates can be constituted as an Object, an Agent, a thinker, an organism.
But in spite of this, different systems can constitute their common referent
in incompatible ways. A child’s toy or doll might at one time be constituted
as an Object and at another as an Agent, but nothing could really answer
perfectly to the rules of both the Object and Agency systems, as they have
incompatible rules about how motion is initiated.
Given the extended developmental sequence of the folk theories, it
seems overwhelmingly likely that their acquisition, like those of several
other types of mental models we have discussed, depends on extended
engagement with the physical, biological, and social world, albeit with the
important caveat that their species-typical features suggest that there may
be strong developmental biases. Core systems would seem to need to be
even more strongly developmentally canalized, as there is evidence that
they are present so early in infancy that it seems unlikely that the child
could have inferred principles like cohesion and local causal dynamics (for
Objects) or goal-directed motion (for Agents) from observation and the
severely limited capacities for controlled interaction available to human
infants.
