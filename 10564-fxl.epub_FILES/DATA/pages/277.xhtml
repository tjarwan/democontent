This approach seems particularly helpful in interpreting ascriptions of
beliefs to nonhuman animals. If we suppose that language and language-
like thinking are uniquely human, and attributions of beliefs ascribe disposi-
tions to form language-like occurrent states, it would seem that nonhuman
animals cannot really have beliefs. On the other hand, ascribing beliefs
to a dog does not seem peculiar in the same ways that ascribing beliefs to
thermostats does. We can make some sense of this, however, if we recognize
that many nonhuman species do have some sorts of mental models, even if
they are not able to form models of many of the things we can, and even if
their models are surely different in their details from ours. Describing their
beliefs in a fashion analogous to reporting human speech involves more of
a distortion of the real psychological content of their mental states, because
they do not have dispositions to make judgments in linguistic form. But
in ascribing beliefs to them, we are nonetheless referring to inner states of
the same kind that we refer to when we ascribe dispositional beliefs to one
another: to dispositions grounded in the models they possess.
15.2 Models as Epistemic Units
Cashing out the notion of belief in terms of mental models provides a first,
but relatively weak, connection to epistemology. In this section, I argue
that there are also more robust connections: models are sources of warrant
for beliefs and judgments, models are themselves epistemically evaluable,
and the fact that models are integral units not reducible to beliefs requires
that epistemology accommodate model-sized units.
Models as a Source of Warrant

model endows a person with a space of possible representations that can
be framed in terms of that model. Judgments about particular situations
are formed by applying a model that is used to frame the situation, and
the resulting judgment is drawn from the space of possible representations
generated by the model. (Of course, we can additionally combine represen-
