In this sense, language and language-like thought are “domain general.”
The same linguistic structures and rules are applied to representations
of various content domains and can be used to combine elements refer-
ring to different domains. On this point, advocates of central cognition
and a language of thought had something right: human cognition involves
more than the ability to think about particular domains in domain-
specific ways, and this is true even if we recognize that domain-specific
understanding can take the form of learned models and not just modules
that are developmentally canalized and appear principally in perceptual
preprocessing.
If you were to include within the scope of the word ‘language’ every-
thing that might appear in a text on language or linguistics, it would cut
a wide swath: not only syntax and semantics but pragmatics, phonetics,
phonemics, prosody, dialect, and so on. Of course, much of what would
thus be regarded as “parts of” natural language would not be parts of lan-
guage-like thought, as the list includes acoustical and pragmatic features of
public languages. Because I have characterized “language-like thought” as
a form of thinking with structural units analogous to those of language, it
would have to involve syntax, or at least something structurally analogous
to syntax. And there are specialized uses of the word ‘language’ that require
no more than this, as when we speak of “formalized languages.” So there
are “thin” notions of language that treat it simply as a system of syntactic
structures, (uninterpreted) lexical types and tokens, and perhaps generative
and inferential rules defined over these.
Philosophers have generally assumed that language-like thought would
also have to include more or less the same semantic elements found in nat-
ural language as well. And in one sense this is clearly correct: by and large,
when we think in a language-like form, we are not dealing with a purely
formal language. But just how much of what we call “semantics,” even with
respect to public languages, is really linguistic? The question is not yet clear,
as we have yet to specify the boundaries of what to count as “linguistic.”
But in the present context, where we are asking what the addition of lan-
guage or language-like thinking adds to the cognitive resources of beings
who already possess model-based cognition (or whose ancestors did), we
have some reason to draw the boundaries of the “truly linguistic” more nar-
rowly than we might in other contexts. If, as I have suggested, inferential
semantics is grounded in models that are not themselves language-like, a
good portion of what we call semantics is not “linguistic” in the sense of
having its origins in distinctively linguistic capacities.
