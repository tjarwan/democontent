9 Other Model-Based Approaches
In the preceding chapters, I have attempted to develop, in an intuitive an
easily understandable way, a very broad notion of a mental model. While
I have gone so far as to describe some of the features mental models might
require, I have deliberately kept the discussion at a level of abstraction that
avoids the kinds of technical details that we would need to do things such
as create a computer simulation of model-based understanding and test its
adequacy to account for various bodies of psychological data. Not only am
I not in the right academic discipline to be pursuing that kind of project,
but the differences between different model-based approaches to knowl-
edge representation or AI are tangential to my purpose, which is to outline
a general philosophical thesis about understanding and explore its further
consequences for areas of philosophy such as epistemology and semantics.
Numerous ongoing research programs in psychology, AI, and theoretical
cognitive science employ notions of mental models as a central feature of
their work, and this book is intended more to complement than to compete
with them.
At the same time, the uses of the term ‘model’ in those academic areas
vary considerably. Indeed, if the people writing about models agree on
one thing, it is that the word is seldom characterized explicitly, and to the
extent that it is, widely different uses of it are at work. Readers familiar with
other model-based accounts of cognition may understandably desire some
clarification of how my notion of a model accords with or differs from
others. It is impossible to canvass all the uses in several fields exhaustively,
but in this chapter I locate my account with respect to several influential
projects in psychology, philosophy of science, and theoretical cognitive sci-
ence. (I should, perhaps, warn that this chapter may seem more “academic”
than most of the others and may be of interest primarily to readers already
acquainted with some of the other work surveyed.)
