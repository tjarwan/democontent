7 Mental Models
As a transition from external models to mental models, consider what
happens when you gain a proficient familiarity with a model or modeling
system. At some point, you begin to be able to think about the kinds of
structures found in the modeling system without actually having to per-
ceive or manipulate elements of the external model. Perhaps you have been
learning about molecular structures using the kind of kit for building model
molecules I have described. Now you want to find the answer to a ques-
tion—say, how many distinct types of organic molecules can be built on a
chain of two carbon atoms. The modeling kit would be a useful aid in such
a task; but suppose it is unavailable, or perhaps you are considering the
question in taking a test in which you are not permitted to use it.
You may well find yourself able to imagine the kinds of structures you
became familiar with through the modeling kit quite vividly. (Humans vary
quite a bit in their capacities for imagery, and some seem to lack such a
capacity altogether. If you fall within the latter group, you may find the
ensuing discussion alien to your experience, though it is familiar to many
other people.) Perhaps you imagine yourself handling a chain of two of the
balls that represent carbon atoms in the kit, or perhaps you visualize such
a structure without tactile or proprioceptive imagery. Perhaps you actu-
ally imagine yourself working with the kit, or perhaps you merely imagine
structures (suspended before the mind’s eye, as it were) that you think of as
molecules. If you have learned to use the kit well, you do not simply imag-
ine the pieces as solid geometric forms viewed in something like a mental
snapshot. The affordances they offer as parts of the modeling system are
available to you as well: the ways the balls and links can fit together in
particular ways and hence be used to build larger configurations. Indeed,
