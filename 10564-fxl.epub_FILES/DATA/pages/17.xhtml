2 A Standard Philosophical View of Cognitive Architecture
If one were to ask philosophers what features of the mind would need to be
accounted for in a viable theory of cognitive architecture, beliefs and other
intentional states would surely be at the top of the list. Many researchers in
cognitive science (or most, or all, depending on how narrowly you define
“cognitive science”) would agree.
1
Moreover, this assumption accords well
with common sense. In everyday life, we routinely explain people’s actions
as consequences of what they believe and desire. This style of commonsense
belief-desire explanation has been developed extensively in philosophical
action theory and extended by theoretical cognitive science into explana-
tions of the mechanisms underlying action and other cognitive processes.
But the idea that intentional states, particularly beliefs, play a central
role in human cognitive architecture is not a product only of reflections on
common sense and discussions in philosophy of mind, action theory, and
belief-desire explanations in cognitive science. It is also strongly suggested
by the ways that philosophers have approached a number of “core” areas of
philosophy: truth theory, epistemology, semantics, logic, and theory of rea-
soning. The most influential approaches to each of these topics have been
undertaken in terms of beliefs (or judgments, a distinction I clarify later in
the chapter), the concepts that are taken to be the constituents of beliefs,
and forms of reasoning that operate over beliefs. Together, these strongly
suggest that the basic units of thought are concepts, beliefs (and other
intentional states), and inferences over such states. These three units, more-
over, form a compositional hierarchy: concepts are constituents of beliefs
and other intentional states, and beliefs are constituents of inferences. If
these areas of philosophy deal with things that are psychologically real, their
success would seem to commit us to such entities being important elements
of our cognitive architecture. And to the extent that epistemology, seman-
