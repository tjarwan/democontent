Depending on the state of interaction between organism and environment,
control of the overall dynamics might be located in various places: in some
neural subsystem, in processes adjusting the coupling, in features of the
environment, or in a more distributed system including both an organism
and objects and processes external to it. While such an explanation does
not focus primarily on operation of subsystems like mental models, this is
primarily because it is intended to be a general framework that is applicable
to many types of relationships between organism and environment. The
enactive approach need not deny that neural systems can be functionally
decomposed into subsystems like mental models, or that these can play
a predominant role in the dynamics of some kinds of tasks or be what is
tightly coupled to more specific features of the environment (the system
modeled) in the performance of those tasks. Coupling mechanisms specific
to model-based interaction might be eminently relevant to the mastery
and aptness of models, and the dynamics of neural change in learning an
important tool for an account of model acquisition. Moreover, the capac-
ity for off-line cognition may quite plausibly involve replacing an online
coupling to external objects with a coupling to mental models that act as
their surrogates. (In this respect, I think that a model-based approach to
cognition provides a natural extension of dynamic coupling accounts of
online cognition.)
The Selfsame Object
None of this so far really addresses the key issue Husserl identified as Hume’s
problem: how we are able to constitute something, perceived and conceived
in various ways and over time, as the selfsame object, which endures through
changes over time and is understood as the same thing even as our percep-
tions of and beliefs about it change. This phenomenon—which we may
call object-oriented cognition—is really a special type of triangulation, distinct
from the practical coordination of different models used in tandem.
