and works of other philosophers in my thinking, teaching, and writing, but
at the same time I am aware that these models are not fully adequate. And,
of course, this goes for any number of nonphilosophical topics as well.
This is, perhaps, a kind of expression of the Socratic virtue of knowing
what you do not know (or understand). It strikes me that anyone who has
developed this intellectual virtue is always in something like the position of
the advocate of a mild form of negative theology. The strong form of nega-
tive theology says that our concepts are so unsuited to talking about God
that we can say nothing true about God and perhaps ought not to try. The
milder form says that all our theological claims must be hedged: “This, but
not quite this, and much more as well.” I think that a proper appreciation
of the idealized and perspectival character of understanding based in men-
tal models should lead us to adopt a similar attitude much more broadly.
We make claims using the models we have—what alternative do we have
beyond a principled silence? But we also know that the resources of those
models are limited, some in ways we understand, and no doubt others in
ways that we do not. To the extent that the model is apt, we count as true
the best ways we have of expressing what can be said using its resources.
But we should be reluctant to say that it is the truth, the whole truth, or
nothing but the truth.
