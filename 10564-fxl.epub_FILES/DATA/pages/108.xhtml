6.5 Computer Models
Computer programs can also be used as models of noncomputational pro-
cesses. The evening weather report, for example, might display weather
maps for the next day or the next week. These are generated by programs
that model meteorological phenomena and generate predictive simulations
of how they might evolve: for example, how a storm will track, or how
colliding fronts will produce high winds and precipitation. Researchers in
computational neuroscience, artificial intelligence, and cognitive psychol-
ogy use programs to test hypotheses about the mechanisms underlying
neural and cognitive phenomena. And economists use computers to model
the economic consequences of possibilities such as lowering the prime
lending rate or a change in the oil supply.
To build such a program, one must begin with a set of hypotheses about
what objects, properties, relations, and processes are relevant to the dynam-
ics of the real-world phenomenon to be modeled. One then creates com-
putational stand-ins for these variables, in the form of data structures and
algorithms that are designed to mirror the relevant properties of the content
domain. A model of billiard ball collisions, for example, would need to have
some representation of the dimensions of the table, as well as the locations,
dimensions, and momenta of the balls; and a sophisticated model might
represent further factors such as the coefficients of elasticity for the balls
and the sides of the table, and the coefficient of friction for the table surface.
It would also require algorithms for computing trajectories over some quan-
tized representation of time, for the effects of friction, and laws of collision.
Such a model would be idealized in a number of ways. Real pool tables have
surfaces that are not perfectly regular. A gust of wind might affect the real-
world kinematics of a shot, or someone might interfere with a ball in play.
The program may not have a way of modeling the effects of spin (“Eng-
lish”) or the chaotic effects of many-body gravitational variables (which, I
have heard it claimed, can produce macroscopic effects with a fairly small
number of collisions). It may also not be prepared to handle the differences
in high-impact collisions, where structural features of the balls become
important, as they may significantly deform under impact or indeed lose
structural integrity. These features are also relevant to the scientific models
themselves on which such a computer model is ultimately based.
