Idealization
However realistic the model, it is made in such a way that it models only a
subset of the features of the target system. (If it is exactly like a ’64 Mustang,
it is the real thing, or a working prototype, or a reproduction, not a model.
If we build something isomorphic to methanol using atoms of carbon,
hydrogen, and oxygen, we are building an actual molecule of methanol,
not a model.) We may say that in this sense the models are idealized. They
have elements corresponding to some elements of the system modeled and
permit relations and transformations corresponding to some of those char-
acteristic of the target system while differing in other properties. We can use
such models as a kind of stand-in for the real thing for various purposes.
But just what set of purposes they can effectively be used for depends on
what features are modeled. And hence, to use the model effectively, one
must understand how it is idealized and what it reproduces faithfully. A
good scale model of a train may provide information about the relative
size of the engine and caboose but cannot be used to accurately predict the
real train’s structural integrity in a crash. A classroom model of an organic
molecule may provide information about its shape and what it can bond
with, but not about how the bonds of real molecules are formed or broken.
