ing somewhat like mine can replicate.
Ask yourself: what color are (were) the living room curtains? For me,
both a verbal answer (“red”) and a visual image occur so quickly that I sus-
pect I have more than one way in which I have committed this information
to memory. But if I ask myself about their texture, or how they were hung,
or how many panels there were, I find that the process of “recollection” def-
initely requires a good deal more imagination, and very likely some deal of
reconstruction. For example, in thinking about the texture, I imagine myself
feeling the curtains as well as looking at them. Perhaps some of this is based
on stored episodic memories of times I touched them as a child. (Indeed, in
some sense it must be based on that.) But I can also make myself imagine
handling them in any fashion I can handle curtains—drawing them open
or shut, pulling them outward, lifting them off their hooks (ah—in think-
ing about this, I recall that they were on those hooks that get pulled by
drawcords!), and so on. To some extent, the parameters within which I can
imagine those curtains reflect the actual ways I did interact, or could have
interacted, with them.
Now, in one sense, this seems quite in line with the idea that cognition
is based in capacities for bodily engagement with the world. But it is also in
tension with the most extreme form of that view: that cognition takes place
only through actual bodily engagement with the world, without features of
the world being stored in the mind. Indeed, for me to be able to imagine
interactions that supply some of the same sorts of information as analogous
interactions through the body, there would seem to have to be something
stored in my mind that echoes the affordances I once found in the things
I am remembering.
