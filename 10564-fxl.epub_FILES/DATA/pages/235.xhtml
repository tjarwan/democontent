To get an argument off the ground at all, you have to start by believing
something. Moreover, the justification for the conclusion of an argument is
no better than that of the premises you started with. So there seems to be
something right in Locke’s notion that, for deductive reasoning to accom-
plish anything, it has to be able to start out with judgments that are arrived
at in some other, nondeductive fashion, which are justified (or at least seem
justified) for reasons other than that we have reasoned them out on the
basis of some prior argument.
But how are judgments produced when they are not products of
explicit reasoning processes such as deductive arguments? And why do
some of them have the ring of truth to them that we describe as seem-
ing “intuitively true” or “intuitively obvious”? Not every thought that
spontaneously occurs to us seems intuitively true, after all. Sometimes
an idea pops into our minds, and our immediate reaction is “No, that
doesn’t seem right”—it seems intuitively incorrect or counterintuitive—and
sometimes we have no intuitive sense about whether something is correct
or not. So really there are two phenomena we must account for: (1) the
types of psychological processes that produce judgments without employ-
ing explicit reasoning, and (2) the fact that some judgments thus pro-
duced seem self-evidently true (or at least plausible), others self-evidently
false (or at least dubious), and still others lack any attendant sense of
self-evident truth or falsehood.
The word ‘intuitive’ is used to mark both a type of process and a type of
apparent self-evidence. We call a judgment-producing process “intuitive”
when it does not involve explicit, conscious, stepwise reasoning. When we
call a judgment “intuitive,” we might mean either of two things: (1) it was
intuitively produced, that is, produced by an intuitive process rather than by
explicit reasoning; and (2) it is intuitively recommended, that is, it seems self-
evidently correct. These two features of judgments need not go together.
Not all judgments that are not produced by explicit reasoning come intui-
tively recommended; and explicit reasoning can produce conclusions that,
once considered, are intuitively recommended. But they go together curi-
ously often; and as our concern here is with the intuitive production of
judgments, I will use the expression “intuitive judgment” for judgments
that are both intuitively produced and intuitively recommended.
