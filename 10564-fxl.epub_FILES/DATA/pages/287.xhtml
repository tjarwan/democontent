Up until fairly recently, the vast majority of foundationalists assumed
that the standard for knowledge needed to be certainty. As a result, properly
basic beliefs were generally assumed to be ones that are in some fashion
self-evident, indubitable, or incorrigible. For rationalists, these might be
truths of reason that are self-evident because their denial is either self-con-
tradictory or literally inconceivable. For empiricists, they might be Humean
impressions or positivist sense data. Foundationalist views that additionally
require that properly basic beliefs be in some fashion indubitable are now
called strong foundationalism.
From a contemporary standpoint, it may seem somewhat remarkable
that, for so much of the history of philosophy, the notion of knowledge
was restricted to things that could be demonstrated or things so certain that
they required no demonstration. I suspect that two factors played a role
in this. The first was the specter of skepticism and the perceived need to
respond to skeptical challenges. The second was that many generations of
philosophers inherited a technical notion of “knowledge” (epistēmē, scien-
tia) that in fact had something like Aristotle’s demonstrative standards built
into it. I see little way of understanding why Locke said that his “sensitive
knowledge” might better be called “faith or opinion,” or why Hume viewed
the nondemonstrative character of induction as leading to a skeptical con-
clusion, unless they simply used the word ‘knowledge’ (or the Latin scientia)
in an already-standardized way to mean “things either known directly and
indubitably or else derived from these through demonstration.”
Locke, of course, also characterized properly basic beliefs as “intuitive
knowledge.” My discussion of the nature of intuitions and their capacity to
generate cognitive illusions provides further reason to doubt that a sense of
subjective certainty is any guarantee of truth or warrant. But the market for
additional arguments against strong foundationalism is slim nowadays, as
it fell precipitously from favor in the middle part of the twentieth century,
due in large measure to criticisms from Quine (1951) and Sellars (1956).
