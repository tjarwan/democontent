Again, in one way all of this is both intelligible and useful from a psy-
chological standpoint. To the extent that we are ever to learn when a prin-
ciple is and is not aptly applied, we first have to learn the principle and
try to apply it. And this requires that learning the principle be dissociable
from mastering the conditions for its aptness. This opens the gates to inapt
application to particular cases, as discussed in the previous section. But the
unqualified form of the principle also allows it to be interpreted as a per-
fectly universal and unidealized claim.
Mistaking a generalization for a universal claim can, of course, result in
epistemic illusion: the intuitive force of the general claim may still be pres-
ent, but it has been covertly turned into a different sort of claim. (Whether
it is also an alethetic illusion depends on the nonpsychological facts of
whether the content of the judgment is true.) That “Xs are Ys” is a useful
rule of thumb in some unspecified range of contexts by no means ensures
the truth of “All Xs are Ys.” And when we (perhaps unwittingly) transform
a useful generalization grounded in a model that is often apt into a univer-
sal claim, we may still feel the intuitive force of the generalization, not real-
izing that it has illicitly been transferred to an unsubstantiated universal
claim, and hence fall prey to a cognitive illusion.
But the mind’s tendency to elide from general to universal claims can
also be the beginning of a fruitful kind of dialectical process of inductive
testing. “Xs are Ys” is probably too vague to test at all. “All Xs are Ys” can
be tested, often by first being contested, and most claims of this form turn
out to be false. But the dissonance between the intuitive appeal of the gen-
eral claim and the proven falsity of the universal claim drives the mind
to look for a more adequate exact formulation. This is, in fact, one of the
predominant methods we see at work in philosophical discussion. A thesis
may first be proposed on the basis of seeming to be an apt way of viewing
things based on a small set of paradigm examples. The thesis is proposed as
a universal claim (often in the form of putatively necessary and sufficient
conditions), and either the theorist herself or other philosophers then test
it by considering possible counterexamples. Often the account is modified
to accommodate counterexamples and subjected to a further round of criti-
cism, and so on.
From this perspective, I think we can see some of the familiar weak-
nesses and strengths of this approach to philosophy. On the one hand, it is
hardly a new observation that many philosophical theories were motivated
by what in retrospect might seem a woefully small set of paradigm cases.
