16.7 Possible Implications for Disputes about Concepts and Semantics
What I have proposed in the previous section should be viewed as some-
thing on the order of a schematic outline of a theory of conceptual seman-
tics. It is not quite so concrete as a theory, but it enumerates some of the
components that might need to go into a theory, and explores some basic
suggestions for how they might be related.
The proposal for looking forward involves a reframing of how we think
about philosophical debates in semantics. Debates about semantic theories
are too often framed as either-or debates between accounts that each focus
on different, but arguably equally legitimate, features and concerns: sense,
reference, truth, or inferential commitments; internalism versus exter-
nalism; intension versus extension; atomism versus holism. There have
been some attempts to move beyond this, such as the two-factor accounts
proposed by Field (1977) and Block (1986). My suggestion is to broaden
this approach to recognize the multiple factors that not only are a part of
semantic competence but would have to be present in any organism that
possesses the kinds of cognitive capacities that human beings possess, even
if that means that they cannot all be accommodated in a compact and tidy
account.
Conceptual atomists and direct-reference theorists have something right.
There is an element of a concept whose function is to track individuals,
kinds, properties, relations, or processes (depending on what kind of con-
cept it is): an ostensive element akin to what Husserl called the “pure x.”

ways of thinking about them, so it is crucially important for us to be able
