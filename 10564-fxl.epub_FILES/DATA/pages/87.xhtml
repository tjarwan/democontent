5.6 Two Types of Alethetic Virtue
Because models (a) define a possibility space of representational vehicles
and (b) are idealized, we must speak of two types of alethetic virtues,

applying to representational systems and one to token representational
vehicles. On the one hand, we may evaluate how good a model is for its
task. Classical gravitation is good enough for some types of problems but
is not a good model for objects moving at relativistic velocities. Descartes’s
understanding of gravity as an effect of particles in the plenum pressing
down is not a good model at all. The restaurant script a person has learned
from going to Denny’s may be inadequate to model what he finds when he
goes to a state dinner at Buckingham Palace. I will speak of the degree of
fit between a model and the phenomena it models as its aptness. Aptness is
always judged relative to particular cases or classes of cases.
A model (or its representational system) defines a space of possible rep-
resentations of how things might be. (As a result, many and perhaps all
representations of how things are will be framed within the context of par-
ticular models.) It is to such token representations that we apply the hon-
orifics “true” and “false.” Models and representational systems are apt or
inapt (to particular problems). Individual representations are (or at least are
capable of being) true or false. When we speak of something like a scientific
theory being “true,” what we really mean is that it is apt, or perhaps that
the claim that it is apt is a true claim. Evaluations of truth or falsehood are
generally claims about whether a given representation of how things are is
the best representation available from a given model and for a particular
set of purposes, and furthermore are normally offered without qualification
only when some available representation afforded by the model is adequate.
When none of the canonical ways of representing things supplied by a par-
ticular model is up to the task, we generally qualify truth claims or else
point out the need for a more adequate way of framing the situation. For
example, when two women came to King Solomon, each claiming to be
the mother of a particular baby, there was a single true answer to the ques-
tion of who was really the baby’s mother given their shared understand-
