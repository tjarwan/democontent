in so doing, we are not reducing our model of atomic-level phenomena to

building dispositions for atomic-level behavior into our models of particles
in the form of dispositions, capacities, and powers that need to be specified
in higher-level terms.
This, in turn, leads to a broader issue. Some models are “outward look-
ing” in that they assume something about a broader and more encompass-
ing context. Take, for example, a model of an organism that treats it as
embedded in an environment. Such a model may be designed primarily to
bring out features of the organism but treats it as an open system. Perhaps
some of the salient extrinsic variables will be specified but an open-ended
number left unspecified. Moreover, some of the features of the organism
that are thus highlighted will be inseparable from the broader context:
respiration, nutrition, procreation, and being predator or prey cannot be
understood as features of the organism in isolation. Indeed, if you were to
try to reconstruct these notions in terms not centered on the organism as
the canonical level of description, the natural way to do so would be by
moving outward, to a description of a larger and more encompassing system,
defined at a higher level of organization, rather than by microexplanation.
Reductive microexplanation is thus a strategy ill suited to capturing the
generalizations or explanatory power of such models, because what they
depend on is a larger and more complex, rather than a smaller, system.
