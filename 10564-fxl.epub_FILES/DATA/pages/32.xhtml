A somewhat stronger claim is that such skilled bodily interaction pro-
vides the grounding on which concepts, explicit judgment, and reasoning
are based. In something like its Heideggerian form, this is not a repudiation
of intentional states, concepts, or inferences but a claim that we need a
more fundamental theory to explain how they are psychologically possible
at all. (Later I make a similar claim that mental models play such a ground-
ing role, and that the notion of a mental model can be combined with
ecological enactive views of cognition.)
Finally, many of the works in this tradition include claims to the effect
that some or even all of our intelligent interaction with the world is nothing
but skilled bodily interaction, with no need to postulate things such as con-
cepts, beliefs, inferences, or mental representations. To my mind, the plau-
sibility of such claims depends on their scope. It strikes me as plausible that
much of the online cognition of both humans and nonhuman animals is a
form of direct engagement with the world, requiring neither judgments nor
inferences, and that we too easily read an interpretive framework involving
judgments and inferences onto processes that are in fact accomplished in
other ways. On the other hand, as I said earlier, it seems clear that we do
sometimes engage in judgments and explicit reasoning. Moreover, it seems
necessary to invoke some kind of inner processes for off-line cognition in
which the mind uses some type of “representational” surrogates in lieu of
objects in the environment, though that need not imply that they are the
types of entities postulated by the standard view.
Intrusions from Broader Cognitive Architectures
This brings us to the broader and older concern that the standard view
leaves out a great deal about the mind, and even if the units of cognition
it posits are real and play some role in the explanation of behavior, we
end up with a seriously misleading picture of how the mind works if we
ignore other things. Of course, no one who is even modestly knowledge-
able about neuroscience or cognitive psychology believes that the standard
view is suitable for a story about all the topics one would find in textbooks
on those subjects. Autonomic processes, orienting systems, attention, early
perceptual processing, proprioception, haptics, motor control, arousal,
mood, and emotion are all important elements of our psychology. In many
cases, we have a good idea of what sorts of neural processes are involved in
their operation, and the neuroscientists’ models of those processes are cast
