Social Knowledge Production
Finally, while knowledge and understanding may be attributed in the
strictest sense to individuals, their production is often inescapably social in
character. Things like contemporary scientific theories and systems of laws
are seldom the work of one person working in isolation. Not only does one
person’s work build on another’s, but it is often the case that the formation
of a model requires teams of individuals with different types of expertise
and distinct duties working together to craft something that none of them
could accomplish alone. Even if the final product is something that an
individual is capable of understanding, it may well be that, in the pro-
cess of developing it, none of the members of the team understood the
whole product both comprehensively and in detail, and the process of
coordinating the different components depends essentially on language.
Mental models are indeed essential to the process, but their coordination
takes place not via mechanisms like triangulation that operate within indi-
vidual minds but through carefully developed special means of commu-
nication, crafted toward a particular end, but erected on the foundation
of language.
11.5 Summary
Public language and language-like thinking are cognitive capacities distinct
from model-based cognition. They are comparatively recent additions to
the cognitive tool kit and would have been of little use to animals that did
not have substantial capacities for model formation. They inherit much of
their semantic content from mental models and do not themselves provide
a basis for the same types of reasoning and understanding supplied by men-
tal models. But neither are they simply separate additions to the cognitive
tool kit. They provide distinct forms of thinking and reasoning that com-
