As with other discussions of “analog” models, Waskan emphasizes that
scale models reproduce systems of relationships in their target domains,
and that as a result, transformations on the models track transformations
in their targets. Waskan emphasizes the importance of such models in
“forethought”—in anticipating how the world will behave (on its own or if
intentionally manipulated) through simulation or other model-based rea-
soning processes.
Like Marr’s account of vision, Waskan’s theory is more limited in scope
than mine. It is broader than Marr’s, in that it includes rich possibilities
of off-line cognition and anticipations of possibilities in the context of
online cognition. But it is by its nature restricted to modeling spatial phe-
nomena and hence is an account of only a subset of cognitive capacities.
Like me, Waskan does not deny that there is also language-like cognition,
but I also hold that there are also a variety of nonspatial models that pre-
serve relations within, and allow nonpropositional reasoning about, other
domains of understanding. Moreover, Waskan’s scale models seem likely
to be, for the most part, “models” in Johnson-Laird’s sense, rather than
abiding frameworks for understanding classes of phenomena. However,
the capacity to form and manipulate scale models presupposes a more
general modeling system (or a collection of such systems) suitable to
this purpose.
In the most recent book on mental models in theoretical cognitive sci-
ence, David Danks (2014) argues that understanding and reasoning (or at
least a great deal of it; Danks seems to offer different claims about the scope
of his view in different parts of the book) are underwritten by graphical
models. The notion of a “graphical model” is considerably better defined
than most notions of models, thus rendering his theory more directly avail-
able for testing against bodies of empirical data, an enterprise that occupies
much of the book. But the notion of a graphical model was not originally
developed as a notion of a mental model or as a thesis about cognitive
architecture, and much of the point of Danks’s book is to motivate the
claim that it can nonetheless serve such ends.
In the original sense, graphical models are a type of mathematical model
used to represent statistical relationships between variables.
