find a similar situation with the relationship between modal and nonmodal
logics: whereas it is possible to, say, treat the various logical connectives in
first-order logic as constructions from a single (albeit less intuitive) connec-
tive, it is not possible to construct the modal operators for necessity and
possibility from nonmodal resources. The reasons for this sort of irreduc-
ibility are straightforward, but the implications are significant: whatever
the state of our model(s) of purely positive claims, such as empirical facts
and laws, they cannot provide a sufficient basis for reconstructing the con-
tent of our normative and modal models.
Two of the more familiar philosophical objections to reductionism are
multiple realization and emergence. We speak of multiple realization prin-
cipally when some functionally specified kind—like AND-gate or heart—is
always (or at least often) built out of some physical components, but there
are many physical configurations that could be used to make something
with the same functional description. It may well be that, in each separate
case, the functionally specified system has the functional properties it does
as a result of its physical configuration. But this falls short of a reduction
for two reasons. First, the models used for the realizing system (say, phys-
ics or cellular biology) do not contain the relevant functional concepts.
Given a model containing those concepts and a sufficiently rich physical
or biological description of an individual system, one might be able to
deduce that it was an AND-gate or a heart, but only by way of compar-
ing it with the standards supplied by the functional model. Second, the
physical or biological model does not capture the relevant generalizations
of the functional model: the functional laws do not “pop out” as laws,
and thus, without the functional model, real and salient patterns would
go unrecognized.
