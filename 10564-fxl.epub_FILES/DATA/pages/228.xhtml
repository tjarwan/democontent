Similar things could perhaps be said about most models of psychologi-
cal and social phenomena. We can explain a great deal about perception
and motor control through neuroscience, but perception and action are
fundamentally relationships between an organism and things in its envi-

strong influences on individual behavior, are fundamentally relations to
larger social structures, as well as being tied to individual, cultural, and bio-
logical histories. You cannot reduce these features to synchronic relational
properties of the proper parts of individuals, because the representational
resources of an at-a-time model of the parts simply lack the requisite con-
ceptual machinery.
12.6 Comprehensiveness and Consistency
Let us return in a more general way to the problem of unities of belief and
understanding. Two things we might want in a system of understanding
and the beliefs that can be framed in it are comprehensiveness and consistency.
On the one hand, we want to be able to understand and have knowledge
about as many things as possible. Perhaps there are some things that our
minds are so utterly unsuited to understanding that they fall outside the
scope of possible knowledge or even belief. It is impossible even to know
what these might be, as we would have to have some level of understand-
ing of them even to specify them that far. We can perhaps conceive of such
limitations only by analogy: that just as we understand things that dogs
cannot grasp, there might be some other beings that understand things
we cannot grasp. There is little point in discussing them further, because
they really cannot be discussed in any concrete way. So when I speak of the
desire to have a system of understanding and knowledge be comprehensive,
I mean this in the more limited sense of encompassing everything we could
in principle understand, or at least all the things we do presently understand
through the models we possess.
The cognitive pluralist thesis is that the basic strategy that a model-based
cognitive architecture employs to gain more comprehensive understanding
is through a proliferation of models that confer understanding of differ-
ent things. A basic stock of such models is part of the developmentally
normal cognitive tool kit, but a modeling engine supplies the resources for
greatly expanding the number of models we possess, and hence the num-
