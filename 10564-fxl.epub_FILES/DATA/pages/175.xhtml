A more precise knowledge of fields like biology and medicine can pro-
vide a far more exacting understanding of what classes of things present
biological hazards, the mechanisms through which they operate, and the
protocols that would allow us to deal with them safely. To a limited extent,
having such knowledge may allow us either to repress ill-founded reactions
of disgust or perhaps even to edit the set of stimuli that provoke them. But
such knowledge does not cause the disgust reaction to go away, and argu-
ably this is a very good thing. The capacity for disgust cannot be unlearned
because it is not part of a knowledge base that can be affected deeply by
other things we learn. It is a deeply ingrained mechanism of its own. And it
continues to be a useful mechanism, one that could potentially save us from
sources of contagion—particularly novel ones—that we might not respond
to sufficiently quickly by way of our scientific knowledge.
Or take the case of sex. Human sexual instincts are far more “open”
than the hardwired programs for reproduction found in insects or in the
species of birds that have characteristic mating rituals. But they are an
important motivational component in human reproduction. It is true that
we can understand human reproduction far more accurately in biological
and medical terms. But a being that had only such scientific understanding
of reproduction would have no intrinsic motivation to actually engage in
reproductive acts. And it would probably be quite impaired at things like
identifying suitable mates and their availability to breed, as our ability to
identify these in large measure depends on the operation of special biologi-
cal mechanisms sensitive to cues involving biological changes (pupil dila-
tion, increased blood flow to the lips and genitals) and behavioral patterns
indicative of gestational cycle and interest. (People could just ask each other,
“Would you like to make babies with me?” but I will leave it to readers to
decide for themselves whether that would be an effective sexual overture.)
When it comes to the things that natural selection really cares about, like
avoiding danger and producing offspring, evolution has provided a highly
effective set of tools that it is difficult to improve on, the lack of which
would leave us seriously impaired.
