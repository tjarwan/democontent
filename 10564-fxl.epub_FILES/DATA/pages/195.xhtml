Let us assume, for purposes of argument, that some sort of causal or tele-
ofunctional relation is sufficient for fixing references of names (or name-
like mental representations) and kind terms (or concepts tracking kinds).
(Indeed, I think such theories have at least some grain of truth, which I
expand on in chapter 16, though I think such stories make sense only
within a broader story about mind and world.) This supplies an explana-
tion of semantic competence only to the extent that semantic competence
is confined to reference—that is, to tracking individuals and kinds.
But clearly this is a much thinner and more limited notion of semantics
than what motivated the need to posit things like mental models. Semantic
understanding is not simply a matter of being able to name or track classes
of things; it also involves being able to grasp their characteristic proper-
ties, relations, behaviors, and transformations. Even if we suppose that, in
coining a term or concept for water, I thereby come to track a particular
type of stuff, and even to pick out that kind of stuff in all possible worlds,
it by no means follows that I thereby gain any understanding of that kind
of stuff. Indeed, it implies neither (1) that I gain a grasp of the properties
that kind of stuff really has (which is, in fact, an important part of the stan-
dard development of such accounts) nor even (2) that I have any particular
“folk” understanding of its nature or how it behaves. Whatever forms of
understanding I might have must be reflected in a systematic set of ways of
representing and making inferences about it—the very sort of thing that I
have argued requires a mental model. Not only is no such model ensured
by the kind of causal relation that is supposed to secure reference, but
according to such theories, it would have to be to some extent independent
of reference, as I and my Twin Earth doppelgänger are supposed to have
isomorphic models that differ only with respect to reference. Not only do
causal accounts of reference not supply a way to explain such inferential-
semantic competence, but their standard development ensures that they
are independent of it. If there is any illusion that they obviate the need for
