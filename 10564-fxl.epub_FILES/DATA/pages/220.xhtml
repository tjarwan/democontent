The first paragraph reflects a cognitive constraint, in this case on how evi-
dence is conceived and expressed. The second reflects a pragmatic aspect:
our epistemic access is mediated by measuring instruments, and there is no
“sharp separation between the behavior of atomic objects and the interac-
tion with the measuring instruments which serve to define the conditions
under which the phenomena appear.” This leads to a pluralist conclusion:
Different experimental setups reveal different patterns of behavior, and we
need different models to capture the patterns. If the models were taken
as descriptions that were independent of experimental setups, they would
generate conflicting descriptions and predictions. But in fact, the experi-
mental setups are mutually exclusive, and the results one obtains with
each are stable. I would interpret this as implying something about how
each model is idealized and where it is aptly applied. Treating the mod-
els as unidealized would not only generate conflicting predictions but also
result in an inability to see how we actually have ways of determining with
some surety how matter behaves in the different cases. Conversely, in this
example, properly understanding how the models are idealized, and more
specifically the range of cases in which each can be aptly applied, prevents
the formal incompatibilities of the models from entailing commitments to
contradictory beliefs or predictions about more specific cases.
But there are other cases in the sciences in which multiple models are
used to frame discussions that result in incompatible implications that can-
not be resolved by restricting their apt use to particular experimental con-
texts. A useful example is the ongoing debate within biology about the
proper way to understand species. If we are to believe the developmental
psychologists, folk biology contains an intuitive concept of species that
includes assumptions such as that animals and plants come in species

mies, and diets. The intuitive essentialism of this notion was made explicit
