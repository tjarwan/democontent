the developmentally normal cognitive tool kit. We quite naturally evaluate

intuitions, we probably seldom even consider the possibility of giving up a
mode of moral evaluation. Each of them provides insights that the others
cannot, and though these may be quite unlike the explanation of scientific
data, a similar principle is at work: that, when push comes to shove, we are
willing to pay the price of perplexity or paradox rather than lose a useful
and powerful tool for insight.
This, moreover, may point to a different kind of “necessity”: there may
be some kinds of models that we are in some sense psychologically inca-
pable of giving up. Modes of ethical evaluation and perhaps an assumption
of freedom that is implicit in some of them might fall into this class, as
might assumptions about a world of classical objects in space and time, and
models of causation and teleology. It is not that people—or at least some
people—cannot conceive of alternatives and even endorse them at a theo-
retical level. Some people can and do regard classical objects, causation,
teleology, free will, and ethical properties as illusions or even as nonsense.
But this does not mean that these theoretical commitments prevent them
from forming judgments, or at least candidate judgments, on the basis of
deeply entrenched models that assume them. At best, it may allow them
to reflectively disavow the truth of the judgments or the aptness of the
models. Note, though, that this too reflects a kind of disunity if, in making
or being disposed toward such judgments, one treats them as true and the
models as apt but reflectively disavows them.
