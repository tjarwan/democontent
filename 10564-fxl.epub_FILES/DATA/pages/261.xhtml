273 Cognitive Illusion
From the outside, it might seem puzzling how anyone could seriously have
believed theories such as that all thoughts are images or that everything peo-
ple do is done on the basis of rational calculation involving their beliefs and
desires. The corresponding general claims, like “thoughts are images” or
“people decide what to do on the basis of their beliefs and desires,” might
be plausible and useful within some more restricted scope and might be
tied to models that are indeed apt for understanding something. But the
universal claims do not have good inductive support, and one would be led
to very different conclusions if one started from different paradigm cases.
That anyone could have thought the universal claims plausible suggests
that they were victims of a cognitive illusion that involved losing sight of
the restricted scope of the general claim. This type of interpretation is often
offered as a criticism of a theorist and his reasoning process. But it only
really works as a criticism if we assume that the process of finding good
inductive generalizations works by first assembling all the evidence and
then drawing a conclusion from it. Viewed as a dialectical process, often
involving multiple participants playing the roles of defender and critic,
the process of proposing a universal claim based on a restricted range of
cases and then subjecting it to critique by counterexamples can in principle
work quite well, even if in other cases it ends up being unproductive, fal-
sifying the universal claim but offering nothing constructive in its place.
What is perhaps more abidingly peculiar from an epistemic standpoint is
that professional philosophers who adopt the role of advocating a particu-
lar theory in such dialectic often seem to also feel committed to the theory
being true, even when they are aware of poignant counterexamples. I sus-
pect that the roots of the commitment are really to be found in a com-
mitment to an underlying model being apt for understanding a particular
range of cases.
One important question for further empirical investigation is whether
the formulation of generalizations within the resources of ordinary language
facilitates the production of illusions. It is not that we cannot devise careful
uses of language that include explicit hedges indicating the idealization of
the models underlying generalizations or employ more formal logical tools.
Indeed, whole schools of philosophy have been devoted to “correcting” the
vagueness and ambiguity of ordinary language. The interesting question,
rather, is whether some of the structures of ordinary language, like “Xs are
Ys,” lose information that was present in purely model-based understanding
before it was transmuted into language—for example, whether we are more
prone to forgetting the idealization conditions of a model we are reasoning
with when we express it in language in the form of a general claim.
