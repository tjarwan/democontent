The semantic value of the concept DOG commits us to take “dogs are ani-
mals” to be true and licenses inferences from “x is a dog” to “x is an ani-
mal.” It likewise commits us to take “dogs are vegetables” to be false and
incurs no commitment about the truth of “dogs are Pope Francis’s favor-
ite type of animal.” But the meanings of all three sentences are built up,
in the same way, from the semantic values of their constituents and their
respective syntactic structures. The difference between atomists, on the one
hand, and holists and molecularists, on the other, is not (usually) about
whether sentence-sized units get their meanings compositionally, but
about whether (or in what cases) epistemic and inferential commitments
are a consequence of something that word-sized units have in their own
right, or whether word-sized units have such semantic properties because
of epistemic and inferential commitments.
A more stringent notion of semantic atomism adds the additional condi-
tion that the semantic value of one word-sized unit cannot depend on its
relation even to other word-sized units:
Semantic atomists hold that the meaning of any representation (linguistic, men-
tal, or otherwise) is not determined by the meaning of any other representation.
Historically, Anglo-American philosophers in the eighteenth and nineteenth cen-
turies thought that an idea of an X was about X’s in virtue of this idea’s physically
resembling X’s. Resemblance theories are no longer thought viable, but a number
of contemporary semantic atomists still believe that the basic semantic relation is
between a concept and the things to which it applies, and not one among concepts
themselves. (Lepore 1999, 829)
By this criterion, if BACHELOR includes other concepts (MAN and UNMARRIED), the

BACHELOR is not a semantic atom. The older definitional approach
to semantics claimed that we define many and perhaps most concepts
in terms of simpler atomic concepts; and for such a theory to get off the
ground, it had to assume that there must be some concepts that are atomic.
The question, of course, was what sorts of concepts could really be atomic
in this way. One answer, explored by the British empiricists and some of
the early logical positivists, was that the best candidates would have to
be something like Locke’s simple ideas of sensation—or, in more recent
formulations, sense data or qualia. Other recent atomisms have attempted
to ground semantic atoms in causal and informational relations between a
thinker and an object or class of objects. According to one type of causal
theory, a concept C means X because Cs are reliably caused by Xs (Fodor
