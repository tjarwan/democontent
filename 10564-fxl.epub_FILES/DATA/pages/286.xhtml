Foundationalism
Foundationalism is a very old philosophical view. Variations on it were
endorsed by Aristotle and his medieval followers, by early modern philoso-
phers in both the rationalist and empiricist camps, and by a significant
number of twentieth-century philosophers, including some of the logical
positivists, such as Moritz Schlick (1934/1979). Aristotle is often credited
with first articulating a foundationalist account of knowledge in his Pos-
terior Analytics, in response to a skeptical problem about demonstrative
knowledge, the regress problem. The skeptic argues that whatever is known
must be demonstrated—that is, deduced validly from things that are
already known. But for those things to be known, they must be demon-
strated from other things that are already known, and so on, in an infinite
regress, so that the chain of demonstration cannot get off the ground, and
hence knowledge is impossible. Aristotle seems to endorse demonstration
as a paradigm for knowledge but rejects both the skeptical regress and the
alternative of circular demonstration in favor of the view that there can
be another, nondemonstrative form of knowledge of the first and foun-
dational premises. Thus we have two types of knowledge: things that are
known directly in their own right, and things validly derived from such
foundations. This is the core of a foundationalist view of knowledge: in
contemporary terms, a belief is warranted if and only if it is either (1) prop-
erly basic or (2) derived in the right sort of way (usually meaning by valid
deduction) from properly basic beliefs.

nition. Not only are the units sentence sized (beliefs, judgments, proposi-

starting point of the discussion is a paradigm of knowledge as a product
