9.1 Models in Psychology
The Scottish psychologist Kenneth Craik appears to deserve the credit for
coining the expression “mental model” in the cognitive sciences. (And,
even if someone else used it earlier, advocates of model-based approaches
trace their usage of the expression back to Craik.) In a passage oft quoted in
the mental-modeling literature, Craik wrote:
If the organism carries a “small-scale model” of external reality and of its own pos-
sible actions within its head, it is able to try out various alternatives, conclude which
is the best of them, react to future situations before they arise, utilize the knowledge
of past events in dealing with the present and the future, and in every way react in
a much fuller, safer, and more competent manner to the emergencies which face it.
(Craik 1943, 61)
Craik published this in 1943, considerably before investigations in arti-
ficial intelligence. Indeed, at the time, computing machines were largely
shrouded in the secrecy of the wartime code-breaking project at Bletch-
ley Park, though Craik was familiar with and wrote about control systems
in two posthumously published papers (Craik 1947, 1948) and sought a
physiological and “mechanistic” explanation for mental processes. He was
also writing long before evolutionary and adaptive approaches to the mind
became popular (though, of course, this line of thinking had been pio-
neered by William James [1890]), but Craik framed the problem of thinking
in terms of an organism coping with problems in its environment. Organ-
isms are able to do so intelligently, according to Craik, through “explana-
tions” that allow predictions of what will happen in the world and as a
consequence of various things the organism might do to act on the world.
The abilities to explain and predict require external processes to be “trans-
lated” into words, numbers, or other symbols, which are then used to “rea-
son,” and then “retranslated” into external processes, like building a bridge.
While Craik did to some extent explore the idea that such internal pro-
cesses operate on linguistic or verbal representations, his central explana-
tory strategy was to liken it to the construction and use of external models.
Indeed, as I have done in this book, he paved the way to developing the
idea of a mental model by first discussing external models.
By a model we thus mean any physical or chemical system which has a similar rela-
tion-structure to that of the process it imitates. By “relation-structure” I do not mean
some obscure non-physical entity which attends to the model, but the fact that it is
a physical working model which works in the same way as the process it parallels, in
the aspects under consideration at any moment. (Craik 1943, 51)
