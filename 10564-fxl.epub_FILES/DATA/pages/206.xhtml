A second and stronger vision of unity is based on the implicatory rela-
tions between propositions. Two such implicatory relations are particularly
important. The first is the Laplacean determinist view that a comprehen-
sive set of laws of nature (regarded as propositions) combined with a com-
prehensive description of the state of the universe at a time t should imply
a comprehensive description of the state of the universe at any later time
t + ∂. The second is the reductionist view that there is some lowest-level
description of the world (say, in the vocabulary of fundamental physics)
from which all the legitimate higher-level descriptions of the world (say, in
the vocabularies of chemistry, biology, and psychology) can in principle be
derived. Both determinism and reductionism have sometimes been regarded
as views that must be true. No less a philosopher than Kant claimed that it
is a synthetic a priori truth that we must regard all events as determined by
a law of nature.

And some of the twentieth-century positivists and empiri-
cists regarded intertheoretic reduction as a norm of the logic of science,
eventually leading some philosophers who believed some supposed phe-
nomena (such as intentional states and ethical facts) to be irreducible to
become eliminativists about them. But the questions of whether all events
are causally determined and whether all scientific theories are reducible to
fundamental physics also seem quite plausibly to be higher-order empirical
questions (a view taken, e.g., in Oppenheim and Putnam’s [1958] “Unity
of Science as a Working Hypothesis”). And as empirical hypotheses, both
have fallen on hard times. Reductionism reached its zenith in the early
1960s, but since that time, philosophers of science who have examined the
relationships between theories carefully have repeatedly found that they
fall short of the standard of reduction as axiomatic reconstruction (Horst
2007). Quantum theory employs probabilistic equations and, in some of
its philosophical interpretations, is regarded as truly indeterministic. And I
argued in Laws, Mind, and Free Will that even nonprobabilistic classical laws
do not imply a commitment to determinism, and a commitment to them
is compatible with a commitment to anomic events, including free will and
miracles (Horst 2011).
