First, consider an experiment in which a metal object is dropped in prox-
imity to a magnet. If we apply a gravitational model, we will get a descrip-
tion of the resulting motion of the falling object. If we apply a model of
electromagnetism, we will get a different description. They will be different
descriptions; and if either model is used alone, the resulting prediction will
diverge from the real-world behavior observed, because in fact both gravita-
tion and magnetism are at work. There is nothing here that should be all
that surprising. More than one force is at work in most mechanical situa-
tions, and the resultant behavior of objects is influenced by all the forces
at work. There is good reason to have separate models of the forces, both
because they are distinct and separable causal patterns in the world and
because understanding them separately makes them comprehensible to us.
The bracketing idealizations of the models are both psychologically neces-
sary and empirically justified.
In Laws, Mind, and Free Will, I took some empiricist accounts of laws
to task for treating laws (like those of gravity and electromagnetism) as
though they directly license claims about real-world behavior, such as how
bodies will actually move when dropped. If understood this way, the two
laws would have to be seen as entailing conflicting predictions, neither of
which corresponds to the real-world behavior in cases where multiple forces
are at work. Interpreted as universally quantified claims about how bodies
actually behave, the laws would be false. But the source of the error is not
that the laws are false but that they should be interpreted not as universally
quantified claims about actual behavior but rather as idealized claims about
the different forces that are causal contributors to that behavior. The error
in the interpretation of laws is a philosophical error—resulting, I believe,
from an attempt to force scientific laws into a particular logical mold. It is
not an error that any competent scientist or engineer would be likely to
make, though of course the scientist or engineer might overlook a factor that
