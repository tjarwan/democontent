Johnson-Laird’s development of a notion of mental models also distin-
guishes models from both propositions and images and treats them as “ana-
logical” representations of relational structures. His models, however, are
not representations of systematic relationships stored in long-term memory
but are constructed on the fly and held in working memory in processes of
inference and discourse comprehension. Consider, for example, the prob-
lem of combining the information contained in the following sentences:
The spoon is to the left of the knife.
The plate is to the right of the knife.
The fork is in front of the spoon.
The cup is in front of the knife. (Johnson-Laird 1983, 160)
The information about the overall scene can be represented in a diagram-
matic model of spatial arrangement.
spoon knife plate
fork cup
The diagrammatic model, unlike the sentences, encodes information in
a format that employs relations analogous to the relations between the
objects referred to—in this case, spatial relations. The processes of reason-
ing to further conclusions—say, about the relationship of the spoon to the
plate or the cup, which is not explicitly mentioned in the sentences—could
be approached as inferences over sentences (which would require further
tacit knowledge, such as that the relationship “to the right of” is transi-
tive, and of how to combine relationships along the left/right and front/
back dimensions) or on the basis of applying capacities for spatial reasoning
directly to the analog model (in which such principles are present by dint
of the general structural features of the modeling system). Consider another
similar set of sentences:
The spoon is to the left of the knife.
The plate is to the right of the spoon.
The fork is in front of the spoon.
The cup is in front of the knife. (Johnson-Laird 1983, 161)
