The first tradition emerged from the “evolutionary synthesis” of system-
atics, genetics, and evolution (Dobzhansky 1937; Mayr 1942). Mayr’s for-
mulation of the “biological species concept” was particularly influential:
[A] species consists of a group of populations which replace each other geographi-
cally or ecologically and of which the neighbouring ones intergrade or interbreed
wherever they are in contact or which are potentially capable of doing so (with one
or more of the populations) in those cases where contact is prevented by geographi-
cal or ecological barriers. …
Species are groups of actually or potentially interbreeding natural populations
which are reproductively isolated from other such groups. (Mayr 1942, 120)
The notion of “reproductive isolation” is key to the biological species
concept. One crucial question separating subsequent developments of
the concept was whether merely extrinsic barriers to interbreeding, such as
geographical separation or ecological barriers like mountain ranges, should
count as differentiating species. Dobzhansky’s definition of “isolating
mechanisms” was sufficiently broad to imply such a conclusion, though
the first quote from Mayr implies the opposite conclusion. The answer
one gives to the question has implications for things such as whether
populations that are temporarily isolated thereby count as separate species,
and (more importantly) whether, if they are later reunited with related
populations with which they interbreed, what were separate species can
then remerge. (Perhaps most strikingly, if merely geographical isolation
can be determinative for species separation, various isolated human
populations were arguably until recently distinct species that have been
reunited into a single species through the availability of long-distance
transportation.)
