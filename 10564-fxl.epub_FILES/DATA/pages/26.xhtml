Sellars
Wilfrid Sellars (1956) proposed that we think and talk about mental states
by analogy with public language. He developed this view through a “myth”
about how our ancestors came to talk about mental states in the way we
do, through three stages of the development of language. In the first stage,
people were able to talk about the world around them but did not yet have
a way of reporting what others said. The second stage involved the expan-
sion of language to include ways of reporting speech: “Og said, ‘The fire is
going out,’” or “Og said that the fire is going out.” Then came a recognition
(or at least a hypothesis) that other people’s behavior is a result of things
going on within them that are not publicly observable, and the develop-
ment of a way of speaking about these inner states using a variation on the
linguistic devices used to report speech: “Og believes the fire is going out,”
or “Og thinks that the fire is going out.” Sellars’s point, as I understand
him—the presentation in the form of a myth introduces some interpretive
uncertainty—is not that inner episodes are an interpretive fiction but that
the way we understand and characterize such inner episodes (some of which
might be quite real) is colored by the fact that we treat them like inner speech.
My original reactions to Sellars’s myth were of a skeptical sort. This was
partly because at first I (wrongly) interpreted it as part of an argument
against the reality of intentional states as inner episodes, and partly because
I thought there were clearly other ways we could first have become aware
of, and come to talk about, mental states, namely, through introspection. I
think I now appreciate his point better. It clearly has something right about
it and raises interesting and important questions. We do tend to think of
intentional states in a fashion analogous to how we think about speech.
