3 Central and Modular Cognition
In the previous chapter, I claimed that mainstream theories about think-
ing tend to treat the mind as a single language-like medium with units of
three distinctive sizes, related to one another compositionally: word-sized
concepts, sentence-sized intentional states (particularly beliefs or judg-
ments), and larger discourse-sized units (particularly deductive arguments).
Theories in a number of core areas of philosophy—epistemology, seman-
tics, truth theory, and logic—are at least tacitly committed to this three-
tiered picture, though each area has its rival theories about matters such as
what makes beliefs warranted, how concepts obtain their semantic values,
what relationship between thoughts and the world is needed for beliefs
to be true, and what are the most appropriate logical formalization(s) of
reasoning. In ordinary life, we often explain people’s behavior in terms of
their beliefs, desires, and other intentional states and how they reason on
the basis of these. And influential mainstream projects in cognitive science
have attempted to provide more explicit and exacting theories of such pro-
cesses through formal and computational models.
While philosophers have often managed to ignore the fact that there is
also much more going on in mind and brain than intentional states and
processes, this option is hardly available within the cognitive sciences. Even
if we were to stipulate that only mental states involving concepts, inten-
tionality, and reasoning are to be called “cognitive,” these still make up
only a part of what goes on within some larger architecture that is needed
to account for things such as sensation, perception, motor control, proprio-
ception, and other topics studied in the cognitive sciences. And this raises
the question of whether, even in this narrow sense of the cognitive, a cogni-
tive architecture can really be specified in a fashion that is independent of
the rest. Alternatively, if we use the word ‘cognitive’ more broadly, can the
architecture for what we might call “the space of meaning and reasons” be
specified independently of a broader cognitive architecture?
