The second tradition, by contrast, emphasizes common descent. The
most influential version of such an approach is the cladistic approach,
based in the notion of a clade, which consists of an ancestor and all its
descendants, living and extinct. (Depending on the details of the specific
cladistic theory, an “ancestor” might be an individual organism, a popula-
tion, or a species.) Cladistic classifications are treelike structures; but unlike
the Linnaean system, they are intended to represent real and evolution-
arily salient relations of lineage and divergence. (Linnaeus, like Aristotle,
assumed species to be unchanging and eternal.) Cladistic classification
produces representations of a hierarchy of taxonomic levels in a treelike
structure, in which a species is a lineage between two branching points. Of
course, most such branchings occurred in the remote past, and so the data
for cladistic analysis must often be drawn from morphological “characters”
reconstructible from fossils, though molecular features of DNA can also be
used for modern specimens and for fossils from which reliable DNA infor-
mation can be obtained. The use of characters as the basis for constructing
cladograms is what allows the cladistic methodology to be extended to the
remote past (something quite important for a classification based on lin-
eage), but there is a great deal of dispute over the choice of relevant and
reliable characters that are diagnostic of lineage and relatedness. (To take a
toy example, using striped fur as a character would result in a biologically
unrealistic analysis of the relatedness of lions, tigers, and zebras.) While the
goal of cladistics analysis is a reconstruction of lineage, using characters as a
basis for taxonomy runs the risk of the resulting system being a classification
system that fails to represent a deeper evolutionary reality.

cant units is often noted in the theoretical literature on species. But atti-
