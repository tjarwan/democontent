Medin and Ortony (1989) suggest that essentialism is a “placeholder” notion: one
can believe that a category possesses an essence without knowing what the essence
is. For example, a child might believe that there exist deep, non-visible differences
between males and females, but have no idea just what those differences are. The
essence placeholder would imply that categories permit rich inductive inferences,
capture underlying structure (in the form of causal and other non-obvious proper-
ties), have innate potential, and have sharp and immutable boundaries. (Gelman
2004, 404)
Essentialism is an implicit feature of how children think about the world.
(The question of how psychological essentialism is affected by further
learning into adulthood is a topic of greater debate, though it seems clear
enough that essentialist biases continue to operate in some fashion.) But
it is also expressed in generalizations: “lions are carnivores,” “women
are nurturers,” “Germans are strict.” The surface form of the generaliza-
tion admits many fine-grained interpretations; but when it expresses an
essentialist assumption, it imputes a trait as something internal to the
nature of the kind. As the examples show, the “kinds” to which such
thinking might be applied are not necessarily restricted to scientific kinds
like species.
Essentialist generalizations can be produced as a result of experience but
can also be transmitted socially through language. Essentialist thinking is
a powerful inductive tool. Making essentialist generalizations explicit can
also be a powerful tool (see the next section) and also makes them available
for social transmission. Of course, essentialist thinking about subcategories
of human beings is notoriously present in social bias and prejudice, and so
the benefits of a powerful tool for learning and categorization clearly come
with serious cost and risk. This, however, helps to illustrate some of the
types of errors to which essentialist thinking is prone. Even if some kinds
of things are aptly thought about in some contexts as having essences (or
essential properties), we may think about other kinds of things in essential-
ist terms inaptly. And even if there are some kinds that can aptly be imputed
essential properties, we may mistake the wrong properties as essential or
mistake whatever “essentialness” amounts to for universality. If species have
essences, being quadrapedal may be essential to dogs, but not all dogs have
four legs. Female mammals may possess biological features that make them
more likely to manifest nurturing traits, but the expression of these traits
depends on other variables, and there are men and women at both ends of
the range of nurturing behavior.
