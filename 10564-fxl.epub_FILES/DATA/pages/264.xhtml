Of course, the discussion of disunity also included the observation that
while there are clear cases in which a proper understanding of how par-
ticular models are idealized prevents their committing us to contradictory
beliefs (e.g., in pure mathematical models and cases like quantum com-
plementarity), there are probably many more cases where models frame
matters in alternative ways that seem to be contradictory without the pos-
sibility of any decisive experimental technique for deciding when each is
aptly applied. Nonetheless there can be other ways in which they differ
with respect to aptness. For example, consequentialist, deontological, and
virtue-theoretic models may differ in their assessments of goodness in vari-
ous cases, with no decisive way to arbitrate between them, and yet still
differ with respect to what insights they afford that might prove useful in
moral deliberation and assessment. The norm of consistency indeed gives
us reason for dialectical engagement in such cases, but it is not the only
norm to which we are beholden. Even if our concern is only with theoreti-
cal understanding, there may be other regulative principles as well—such
as having good ways of understanding as many things as possible—which
may pull in opposite directions, and the extent to which any regulative
principle can be fulfilled completely by a given mind depends on empirical
facts about that mind’s cognitive architecture. It is an epistemic illusion to
assume that a given mind must be capable of fulfilling a regulative prin-
ciple, much less fulfilling all of them simultaneously.
