Are Models Epistemically Evaluable?
Are models themselves epistemically evaluable? Given the standard char-
acterization of knowledge as warranted true belief, models cannot count
as knowledge, because models are not beliefs. (And as the terms ‘true’ and
‘false’ are applicable only to beliefs and to other units of the same size, such
as propositions and sentences, models cannot be true or false, either.) It
is true, of course, that we sometimes speak of some types of models, such
as scientific theories, as “true” and as “knowledge,” but this does not so
much present a challenge as it bespeaks an ordinary language usage that
is broader than technical usage, or a residual assumption that theories are
propositional in character, or perhaps really that we can make statements
about the aptness of models, and such statements can be true or false, war-
ranted or unwarranted.
While I have spoken (albeit briefly) to the reasons for using separate
terms to express the alethetic virtues that are appropriately attributed to
judgments (and other sentence-sized units) and to models, I have yet to
discuss the question of whether models are also units that can be evalu-
ated epistemically for warrant or something like it, apart from questions
of whether the beliefs constitutively associated with them are individually
warranted. We have at least two distinct sorts of questions here. First, are
models, as models, subject to any sort of epistemological evaluation at all?
Second, if they are, are the features that count as epistemic virtues for mod-
els sufficiently like those that count as epistemic virtues for beliefs or judg-
ments that we should use the same terminology for them—for example,
should we speak of models as things that can be warranted?
