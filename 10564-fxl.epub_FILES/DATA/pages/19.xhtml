A third and more recent theory of knowledge is reliabilism. Reliabilists
hold that a belief is warranted if it is produced by a reliable mechanism.
(A paradigm case would be the production of beliefs through reliable per-
ceptual mechanisms under standard perceiving conditions.) It is perhaps
less clear whether this introduces other types of cognitive phenomena, in
addition to beliefs, that might need to be included in a cognitive architec-
ture for knowledge. On the one hand, we might take the basic relata of a
reliabilist theory to be beliefs and the states of affairs that produce them.
On the other hand, we might take the mechanisms of belief production to
be among the relata, in which case we are then faced with the question
of whether they should be counted as part of cognitive architecture, or as
something noncognitive or even extramental that is nonetheless relevant
to warrant.
We should note, however, that with some beliefs, both their truth and
their warrant may not depend on relations to extramental states of affairs.
These are beliefs that are true because of the meanings of the concepts and
the relationships between them. Propositions like “dogs are animals” and
“triangles are three angled” seem to be true by definition, and we can know
them to be true simply by analysis, based in our mastery of conceptual
semantics.
Semantics is concerned with meaning, and both concepts and beliefs
have semantic properties. Moreover, concepts are constituents of beliefs,
and the semantic properties of a belief (or the proposition believed) are
generally understood to be a function of those of its constituent concepts
plus the logical (or grammatical, or syntactic) form of the proposition (with
a few important caveats, such as that reference may also depend on con-
