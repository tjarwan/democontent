Computationalism
In the last section, I claimed that merely possessing an internal “lan-
guage”—in the thin sense of a system with syntactic structure and some
set of syntactically based techniques for producing symbol strings from
other symbol strings—would not confer semantic understanding. To this,
advocates of the computational theory of mind might point out that Tur-
ing showed us how syntactic operations can be made to track semantic
relations, and this was the first (and perhaps the only) proposal for how
there could be meaning-respecting mental processes that do not require
a magical semantic interpreter. After all, some of the early explorations of
model-based views of semantic understanding—Minsky’s frames and other
projects in AI that took place around the same time—were implemented
in digital computers, and whatever semantic competence (or simulation of
semantic competence) they demonstrated was ultimately based in syntacti-
cally driven computations.
However, note that the sort of “syntax” involved in such systems is not
the syntax of the natural language in which the input and output sentences
are couched. Such “syntactic” processes are machine language operations
operating over binary digits that are not themselves representational at all.
We must therefore be careful to distinguish two senses in which computa-
tional processes might be called “linguistic” or “language-like.” One thing
this might mean is that the computer has been programmed to support
representations in a form something like a natural language and to perform
operations on these—that it has representational types that are at least struc-
turally analogous to sentence types or judgment forms. This is an important
goal of artificial intelligence, but it is a feature absent from most of what
the majority of computers do. We do speak of “the machine language” of a
particular computer and call the rules of the operating system “syntactic.”
But what we mean by this is not that the symbolic units are structurally
analogous to sentences or judgment types. The syntactic forms of machine
languages have little in common with those of natural languages, and all
that is really meant in calling them “syntactic” is that they are nonsemantic.
So when we speak of computers essentially being devices that manipulate
symbols according to syntactic rules, what we really mean is that programs
have their own distinctive forms of symbols and syntax, and this ultimately
drives the performance of the computer. This is quite generally true of com-
puters—indeed, it is close to being a definition of digital computation—but
it does not require the use of anything analogous to a natural language.
Program languages, particularly those that operate at the most fundamen-
tal level of bitwise operations, bear little resemblance to natural languages.
