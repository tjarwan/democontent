The implication of this seems to be that, in seeing an object, we are not
simply experiencing something like a photograph (or a binocular interpola-
tion of photographs) of the object from a given angle, or even seeing a suc-
cession of photographs taken from different vantages. We do indeed always
see an object from an angle; but we also have some kind of mental model of
the object that either consists in, or is generative of, understanding of what
it looks like from multiple perspectives. And this is not simply a feature of
how we see individual objects. We gain templates for the understanding of
new objects of familiar kinds: if you are handed a new pair of dice at the
casino, you do not need to examine them to see what is on the sides (unless
you have reasons for suspicion), because your mind already fills in default
information that each side will have one to six pips. On the other hand,
some aspects of what one will find are specified only at a generic level: pick-
ing up a book, you expect to see text, but the content of the text requires
empirical examination, as does the question of whether there will also be
pictures.
The question Minsky essentially poses is, What kind of information
structure could have these features? His answer is that it is a particular type
of frame structure. In his own example, an observer is looking at a cube
from multiple angles. Minsky develops the data structure diagrammatically,
using a system of nodes and links ( fig. 4.3 ).
