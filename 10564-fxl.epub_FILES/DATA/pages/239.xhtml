violates these rules triggers a particular type of cognitive dissonance with
the model, and this is experienced as its being counterintuitive. A judg-
ment that expresses the content of the rules is quickly and unconsciously
confirmed as being an implication of the model, is experienced as being
intuitively recommended, and can be produced by some mechanism that
translates the implicit rules of the model into propositional form or con-
firmed by a mechanism that compares the propositional expression with
the rules of the model. Judgments like “agents have goals” seem like neces-
sary truths, perhaps even platitudes, because to think or talk about agency
at all, one must be using a model that imputes goals, beliefs, or desires.
Indeed, there are clear senses in which they are necessary truths. “Agents
have goals” is something like an implicit axiom of models of agency, and
hence is true-in-agency-models in much the way the parallel postulate is
true-in-Euclidean-geometry. To use agency models at all, one must treat
things constituted as agents as things that act to achieve their goals, and in
order for the constitution of something as an agent to be apt, the imputa-
tion of goals to it must be apt as well.
Similar things can be said about the intuitive representation of objects in
space. To represent something as being in space, one must represent it in a
fashion that assumes that there is space on all sides of it. (Of course, “space”
is said in a proprietary sense here, corresponding to a geometrically defined
space, not as “empty space.” If someone says, “There is no space to the left of
the cabinet,” because the left side of the cabinet is flush against a wall, they
mean there is no empty space, not that the wall exists outside space.) This
seems to be a basic feature of the model (or a set of models) we use to repre-
sent objects in space. As a consequence, the question “Is there (geometric)
space to the left of it?” seems absurd to ask, as the answer is a straightfor-
ward consequence of basic principles of the model and hence, from the
standpoint of someone working within the model, should go without say-
