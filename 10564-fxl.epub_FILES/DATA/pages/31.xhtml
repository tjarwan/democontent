Embodied Cognition
Advocates of ecological, embodied, and enactive approaches to cognition
have likewise positioned their views in opposition to those based around
representational intentional states.
3
The core thesis of these views is that
cognition is not simply something that takes place within the mind or
brain but a kind of skilled practical engagement with the world. Among
the sources of this relatively new approach to the mind are the twentieth-
century philosopher Martin Heidegger’s (1927/1996) claim that intentional
states are grounded in and dependent on deeper capacities for skilled bodily
activity, and the ecological psychologist J. J. Gibson’s (1977) claim that
organisms perceive and respond directly to affordances (structures in the
world that provide something to the organism or allow it to do something),
rather than inferring and representing them on the basis of brute sensory
data. Cognition is viewed as a dynamic process that encompasses not only
the mind or brain, or even the whole embodied organism, but a dynami-
cally coupled system of interactions between organism and environment
(Silberstein and Chemero 2011; Thompson 2007).
Beyond the provocative thesis that cognition, and even “the mind,”
is not just “in the head,” writers who embrace ecological, embodied, and
enactive theories of cognition make several additional claims of different
degrees of strength. The mildest of these is that there are a variety of pro-
cesses that do not look much like judgments and inferences that none-
theless play essential roles in intelligent interaction with the world and
indeed in sense making. This does not directly threaten commitments to
a cognitive architecture that includes concepts, judgments, and inferences,
