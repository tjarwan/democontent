described by Newtonian mechanics. As Catherine Elgin notes:
We understand rules and reasons, actions and passions, objectives and obstacles,
techniques and tools, forms, functions, and fictions, as well as facts. We also un-
derstand pictures, words, equations, and patterns. Ordinarily these are not isolated
accomplishments; they coalesce into an understanding of a subject, discipline, or
field of study. (Elgin 1996, 123)
The idea that a content domain might itself be a fundamental unit for
understanding was developed most influentially in philosophy of science,
beginning in the 1960s, as the idea that scientific theories are not simply
combinations of statements or beliefs that can be specified independently of
one another; rather, the concepts and law claims that make up a theory are
constitutively interdefined, and it is the entire theory that is confirmed or
disconfirmed as a unit by empirical evidence. Theories are integral seman-
tic and epistemic units and indeed are in some sense more fundamental
semantic and epistemic units than the concepts or claims they employ,
as the concepts gain their meaning and the claims their warrant from the
larger unit of the theory, not the other way around. As a consequence, we
do not fully understand concepts, claims, beliefs, judgments, or the special
forms of reasoning distinctive of theories if we do not take this other type of
unit into account. And if that is the case, theory-sized units turn out to be
eminently relevant to the concerns of epistemology, semantics, and truth
theory as well.
Just how broad an implication we should draw from this depends on
whether such features are unique to scientific theories, or whether they are
a more general characteristic of understanding. In chapter 4, I present a
case for the more general thesis that understanding is organized in mental
models of content domains, and this thesis is further developed, under the
name of cognitive pluralism, in chapter 5.
