11 The Complementarity of Models and Language
In previous chapters, I have explored the hypothesis that human under-
standing is grounded in domain-centered mental models. Model-based
cognition, however, is clearly not the only component of human cognitive
architecture. Nor is it something unique to human beings. As I have char-
acterized models in a fashion that is independent of how they are acquired,
there may be any number of comparatively simple animals with “nativis-
tic” (i.e., species-typical, developmentally canalized) cognitive mechanisms
that encode systematic features of domains in a fashion that would allow
them to count as models. Even the capacity to acquire models through
learning—the possession of a modeling engine—is clearly evidenced in a
number of biological taxa. One sort of question this presents is how mod-
els and the modeling engine are related to other components of cogni-
tion and intelligence. Fully exploring such a question would require a very
wide-ranging investigation, extending beyond philosophy and psychology
into cognitive ethology and evolutionary psychology. I will approach it
in a far more modest fashion, and principally to make a case that while
there are distinct factors that contribute to the cognitive architectures of
humans and nonhuman animals, some of which may have been historical
precursors of others, they often play complementary roles in cognition and
accomplish more in combination than the sum of what they contribute
individually. This basic orientation—that distinct cognitive systems can be
complementary—will then serve as the basis for a discussion of the rela-
tionship between model-based understanding and the most distinctive fea-
tures of human minds: public language and language-like thinking.
