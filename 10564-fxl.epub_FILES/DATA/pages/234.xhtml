The founders of non-Euclidean geometries, however, recognized that the
parallel postulate could be denied without self-contradiction, and various
alternatives to it would result in distinct alternative geometries. Euclid’s
parallel postulate may seem as though it must be true, but the reason for
that is to be found not in the definitions and axioms of the system or the
principle of noncontradiction but in a separate set of “spatial intuitions”
to which the human mind is strongly biased. This discovery caused a crisis
in mathematics, spawning the formalist movement, which sought to find
ways of reconstructing mathematical systems in terms not dependent on
mathematical intuitions.
In contemporary philosophy, appeals to “intuitions” are often used
to justify important claims. In metaphysics, claims about what is neces-
sary and what is possible—things that go beyond what could be settled
by empirical experimentation—are often explicitly grounded in “modal
intuitions”: for example, that there could be a being that was a molecule-
for-molecule duplicate of you but lacked conscious experience (Chalmers
1996). In ethics, not only claims that some particular action is good or evil,
but also fundamental claims such as that human beings have natural rights,
are justified by appeals to intuitions. However, unlike mathematical intu-
itions, which tend to be about things that everyone, or at least the experts,
agree on, appeals to intuitions in contemporary metaphysics and ethics are
often precisely about matters that people have different intuitions about.
For some, what seems intuitively obvious is that a molecular duplicate of
a person would have to have all the same mental states, and Jeremy Ben-
tham famously quipped that the notion of natural and inalienable rights is
“nonsense on stilts.”
Intuitions, in the sense of judgments that are not reached on the basis
of explicit reasoning from something else yet strike us as clearly true (and
hence not requiring further justification), are by no means the sole prov-
ince of mathematicians and philosophers. We think intuitively all the time
in daily life. Indeed, if you were to monitor the judgments you make on an
average day, you would probably find that most of them were not reached
through any kind of argumentation or reasoning (or at least not any you
could detect). Much of the time, intuitive judgments are nonetheless quite
dependable. In fact, when you think about it, any reasoning you do engage
in has to start with some set of assumptions that serve as the premises from
which you derive the conclusion. The conclusion is derived through rea-
soning from the premises, but on what basis did you believe the premises?
