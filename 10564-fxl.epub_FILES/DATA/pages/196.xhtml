Moreover, while causal accounts do not make reference a consequence
of mental models, they do not make it a consequence of language either.
Generally, what is supposed to secure the reference of a concept is some
kind of causal relationship with things outside the mind, generally through
perception. According to causal theorists, what makes a concept mean
“cow” is not the fact that it can be embedded in particular syntactic struc-
tures or manipulated through formal inference techniques, but the fact that
its tokening is caused in the right sorts of ways by cows. And what assures
this is not the language-like structure of Fodor’s central cognition, but the
encapsulated processes of his perceptual modules.
11.4 What Language Adds
What sorts of cognitive benefits does possessing public language and lan-
guage-like thinking confer that could not be had through model-based cog-
nition alone? In this section, I discuss a number of these benefits, with
attention to how they complement model-based cognition, both in the
sense of adding things that cannot be had using models alone, and in the
sense of how the two types of thinking interact to add up to something
more than the sum of the parts.
Communication and the Acquisition of Models through Language
The most obvious advantage afforded by public language is that it allows
for communication in ways that models do not. It is possible to build inter-
subjectively available external models for some things we think about, and
sometimes these are essential for instruction, communication, and joint
attention; but external model building alone would be an extremely cum-
bersome form of communication. But we should also make an important
distinction here between linguistic communication of propositions about
domains that speaker and hearer both already understand in similar ways
and the use of language in the process of teaching one another the use of
new models.
