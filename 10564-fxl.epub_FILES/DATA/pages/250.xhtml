What features of the mind might produce cognitive illusions? Kant
explored one way they might be produced, which I discuss and expand
on later in the chapter, but we have no reason to think that there should
be only one way. An account of understanding based in idealized models
suggests several ways that judgments can be generated from models and
ways that such judgments can go awry when they lie outside the model’s
idealization conditions. And the suggestion, developed in chapter 13, that
at least some kinds of model-based judgments carry a sense of intuitive cor-
rectness, sometimes even of necessity, can help account for their seeming to
be knowledge, even a priori knowledge of necessary truths. There may be
more types of cognitive illusion than I am able to explore here, but I will
describe three types:
1. Illusions resulting from the inapt application of a model to a particular
situation
2. Illusions resulting from treating the principles internal to a model as
unrestricted truths not bounded by the idealization conditions of the
model
3. Illusions resulting from attempts to unify understanding rooted in vari-
ous models into a single system by unrestricted application of a second-
order model of how more local knowledge fits together
Kant’s dialectical illusions are of the third type, though I will argue that
they are not the only examples of that type. It is this third type that is by
far the most philosophically interesting, as arguably a number of familiar
and controversial philosophical theses are in fact illusions of this type. In
this chapter, I will speak more generally of cognitive illusion from an epis-
temological standpoint, and the chapter will conclude with considerations
of a possible fourth type of cognitive illusion—what we might call projective
illusions.
