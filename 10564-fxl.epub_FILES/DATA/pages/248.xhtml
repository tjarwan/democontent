13.6 Models, Intuition, and Cognitive Illusion
If expert understanding is often associated with intuition, there is also
another and almost opposite set of cases equally associated with it: various
sorts of errors that nonetheless seem intuitively correct. Much of the two-
systems literature in psychology is in fact concerned with how people—
including experts—make mistakes in reasoning, even when upon reflection
they would understand the kinds of methods they employ to be fallacious.
Sometimes such errors may be simple mental slips. But there also seem to
be ways that we are systematically drawn to think about matters in inapt
ways. In the next chapter, I make a case that the mind is subject to a variety
of types of cognitive illusions, in which judgments present themselves as
intuitively recommended, sometimes even as necessary, yet either they are
erroneous, or at least we are inclined to greatly overestimate the degree of
confidence we can place in them. (In Kantian terms, they are illusions of
knowledge, whether or not they are accidentally true.)
