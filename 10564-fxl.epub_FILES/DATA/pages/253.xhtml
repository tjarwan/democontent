Frank Keil (1992) had proposed that humans have an innate system for
teleological explanations linked to biological thinking. Kelemen’s evidence
that young children show a bias toward applying teleological reasoning to
things they understand to be nonliving seems to belie this hypothesis, and
so Kelemen hypothesizes that a system for teleological reasoning may be
developed on the basis of an earlier-appearing system for understanding
intentional agents.
Experiences that suggest objects exist in the world to fulfill the purposes of agents
might subsequently contribute to the tendency to over-generate purpose-based te-
leological explanations when faced with explanatory gaps. In other words, later on,
in the absence of other explanations, children might draw on their privileged knowl-
edge of intentions and artifacts to conclude that, like artifacts, natural objects exist
in the world because some agent put them there for a purpose. (Kelemen 1999, 466)
If we assume, with Kelemen, that the application of a teleological model to
things other than organisms and artifacts is inapt, promiscuous teleology is
a case of a bias to overgeneralize the applicability of a model in inapt ways,
and subsequent learning about science that is ubiquitous in Western edu-
cation narrows the range of cases in which teleological framing is applied.
This range narrowing may well be a by-product of particular types of educa-
tion and enculturation, without which we should expect to find animistic
tendencies in adult populations. This seems to be the view taken by Guth-
rie (2002), who additionally ascribes “animistic” thinking to nonhuman
animals and describes it as “illusion”: “The attribution of characteristics of
animacy to the nonliving and to plants appears widespread among com-
plex organisms. Because illusion is an inevitable concomitant of percep-
tion, such attribution may be universal” (53).
