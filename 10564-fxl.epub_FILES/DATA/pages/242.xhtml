likens concepts to note cards in an internal database and sees ontological
categories as templates for such note cards that specify both category-gen-
eral assumptions (e.g., animals are mortal and the offspring of same-species
parents) and blank “slots” for other information to be filled in on a species-
level basis, such as characteristic physiognomy, diet, habitat, and so on.
When confronted with a new type of animal, we form a new database entry
on the basis of the
ANIMAL category. This leads us to assume that the species
will have some characteristic physiognomy, diet, and so on, which guides us
in a search for what information needs to be acquired to fill in the blanks.
As we fill them in, we have an increasingly richly specified set of assump-
tions about that species, drawn from the “entry” for the species, the onto-
logical category, and any levels (e.g.,
MAMMAL) lying in between.
The information thus “encoded” need not ever have been a part of
an explicit judgment. At some point, I pieced together a set of assump-
tions about the internal organs—hearts, kidneys, and so on—I expect to
typically find in some superordinate class of animals, and these are either
“written into” my model of dogs or else linked with it in a fashion I can
readily exploit. Others may be built into my mental model of dogs through
experience: I observe what dogs eat, and encode the assumption that they
eat meat into the model even if I never think a thought with the content
“dogs are carnivores.” Unless prompted, I may never form a judgment on
questions such as whether dogs have kidneys, are carnivores, are mortal, or
are the offspring of other dogs. But once I am prompted, the answer can
be “read off” the mental model. The judgment is intuitively produced by
being (somehow) read off what is already present in the model in a non-
propositional form, and is intuitively recommended because it is a product
of what is already present in the model.
Boyer develops his theory of concepts in Religion Explained to suggest
that concepts of supernatural beings that figure in religious belief are pro-
duced by starting with a familiar ontological category (or one of its subor-
dinate concepts) and then violating one of the rules—say, a concept of an
immortal dog or a dog that can transform into a human being. These con-
cepts are “counterintuitive” because they violate rules of the ontological
category—say, that animals are mortal, are each members of a single species
kind, or have a (constant) physiognomy. Boyer suggests that supernatural
concepts are special because they (a) violate rules at the category (rather
than the species) level and (b) violate just one rule. But ideas that violate
rules at the species level (e.g., a six-legged dog or a dog without a heart) or
more than one rule (a six-legged dog without a heart) would also be coun-
terintuitive for the same general reason.
