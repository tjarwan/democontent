A model of human anatomy, by contrast, is generally useful not as a kind
of production system for various anatomical configurations but as a model
of the standard human anatomy. It may be that some such models have
parts that can be modified, removed, or rearranged to model nonstandard
configurations, but to the best of my knowledge, this is not its standard or
preferred use. It can, however, be used to model specific conditions and
procedures, such as the anatomical consequences of removing a bone or
organ or different approaches to surgical intervention.
Where we draw the boundaries around the term ‘model’ here is in
part a matter of deciding what terminology will provide the most useful
set of theoretical resources. I am inclined to use the term in fashion that
treats “models” as offering a space of representational possibilities of states
of affairs. But this usage is extremely broad, allowing models of varying
degrees of generality and abstraction. Model A may provide the representa-
tional space for B, in which case B may be said to be a representation within
the representational system afforded by A. But B may nevertheless itself be
a model if it defines a space of representations of its own. Even a model of a
particular object may do this: a scale model of a car or train may be capable
of being manipulated to represent various states—say, doors open or doors
closed, hooked to the engine or decoupled—and may allow other sorts of
representations as well, such as one of the ratio between the length and
width of the vehicle. Or a model of a 1961 Lincoln limo (the type of car
in which President Kennedy was riding when he was assassinated) might
be used in a representation of the assassination, or as a modeling system
for conspiracy theorists attempting to prove that the shots could not have
come from a single gun.
