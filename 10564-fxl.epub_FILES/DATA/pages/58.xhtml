The Core Geometric System
The fourth core system is devoted to an understanding of the geometric
properties of the environment, “the distance, angle, and sense relations
among extended surfaces in the surrounding layout” (Spelke and Kinzler
2007, 91). It does not represent nongeometric properties such as color or
odor (91). The evidence for such a system is drawn from studies of how chil-
dren, adults, and nonhuman animals orient themselves spatially. Adults
use at least two orienting systems, one guided by surface geometry and
the other by landmarks. The landmark-based orientation system develops
later, whereas the geometric orientation system is evidenced very early. The
Geometric System, moreover, seems to undergo an important type of gen-
eralization as it becomes dissociated from the need for a fixed origin and
angle of orientation.
The existence of such a system may seem either obvious or unnecessary.
The geometrical character of the physical world seems so patently obvious
that it may seem unclear why we should need a special system to under-
stand it. Yet this very obviousness may simply be a product of how deeply
ingrained our geometric assumptions in fact are. (As Kant suggested, we
may actually be unable to perceive space except as structured by a highly
specific geometry.) We can see this most readily by considering artifacts
that have some features of human perception while lacking others. A cam-
era receives visual information about the environment but does not inter-
pret that information as being a product of a three-dimensional world. To
