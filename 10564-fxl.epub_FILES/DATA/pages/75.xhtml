5 Cognitive Pluralism
In chapter 4, we saw evidence that understanding is organized into units
that are domain specific, relatively self-contained, and possess distinctive
representational systems and inference patterns. This characterization has
much in common with the way cognitive “modules” are typically described,
but is intended to describe a much more general feature of cognition. The
organization of understanding into domain-centered units that can operate
semiautonomously on their own principles is found in nativistic and highly
encapsulated perceptual Fodorian modules, in nonperceptual cognitive sys-
tems (such as core knowledge systems) that are canalized early in develop-
ment and might be products of natural selection, in the organization of
acquired understanding in semantic networks and frames, and in scientific
models, our most exacting and regimented form of understanding. This
would seem to be a feature of the architecture of understanding that is
independent of whether a particular mode of understanding is “nativistic”
or learned, neutrally localized or distributed, species typical or dependent
on individual learning history or acculturation. Indeed, I will claim that it
is a basic design principle for the architecture of understanding.
We need a nomenclature both for this view of cognitive architecture
and for the domain-specific units of understanding that it posits. I will
refer to the view I am espousing here as cognitive pluralism and will use the
expression ‘mental model’ as a generic term for the domain-sized units of
understanding we have observed in modules, core knowledge systems, folk
theories, scientific theories, and Minskian frames. Settling on this terminol-
ogy for domain-sized units of understanding was in some measure a matter
of choice, as there were several obvious candidates, none of them perfect,
and each having preexisting usages: ‘model’, ‘schema’, ‘frame’, ‘framework’,
‘theory’. Short of inventing entirely new terminology, there was really no
way of finding a term that would not risk some degree of confusion with
