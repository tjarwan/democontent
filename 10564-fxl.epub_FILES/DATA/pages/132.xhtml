Animal minds (including those of human animals) have evolved so that
certain interest-relative properties of the world stand out, and this extends
even to how we conceptualize objects and the world itself. For example,
we “naturally” understand up and down to be privileged directions—a use-
ful and efficient way of framing spatial geometry for organisms that dwell
on the surface of a planet, but one that long proved an impediment to a
satisfactory cosmology. The privileged place of cohesive solids in our matu-
rationally normal ways of thinking about a world of objects is likewise a
useful framing, though one that makes contemporary physics counterintui-
tive. The color vision systems of various species no doubt attune them to
affordances that are important to their mode of life, but color is not a good
framework for understanding scientific optics.
One thing we mean in calling science “objective” is that, to achieve sci-
entific understanding, we often have to peel away layers of “subjectivity”
that involve understanding things in interest-relevant ways that are pecu-
liar to us as individuals or as a species. This, however, does not mean that
there is an end point of “complete objectivity.” The explanatory interests
of the sciences are still interests, and ones that occur to members of one
particular species, and even so only within particular historical and cultural
contexts. Our ways of conceiving things scientifically, even our mathemati-
cal understanding, are still drawn from the repertoire of ways that minds
like ours are capable of thinking, and are also conditioned by the ways we
have of investigating and acting on the world, even if these are greatly
extended and transformed through technology.
The “objectivity” of science also does not imply a single theoretical per-
spective. Scientists use a number of distinct models: relativistic and quan-
tum models, wave and particle models, evolutionary and molecular-genetic
models. Like different types of maps, scientific models can be incommen-
surable with one another, have incompatible representational systems and
background assumptions, and license contradictory predictions. This is one
of the principal phenomena described as “disunity of science,” and we will
examine in later chapters what cognitive pluralism might have to say about
the causes of such disunity and the prospects for unification.
