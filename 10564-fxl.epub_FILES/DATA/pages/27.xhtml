Introspective evidence clearly indicates that some thought takes the form
of language-like propositional thinking and reasoning, because sometimes
we think in a natural language, and sometimes we apply argumentative
forms such as deductive syllogisms in explicit reasoning. But this evidence
extends no farther than conscious judgments couched in language and
explicit application of inferential methods. Moreover, we are probably
prone to conflate cases in which thinking is done in a language with cases
in which the actual thinking and reasoning are accomplished in some pre-
linguistic form and then rendered in linguistic form, even if only to oneself.
Perhaps this is merely “translation” from one “language” (Fodor’s “language
of thought”) to another (such as English), or perhaps it is a very fast and
automatic conversion of something that is not language-like in form into
language. With reasoning, the situation is perhaps even worse, as numer-
ous psychological studies indicate that when people produce “reasons” for
their statements or actions, what they produce are really rationalizations
confabulated after the fact that do not reflect the actual processes that led to
what they said or did.
We thus seem to be faced with the possibility of four distinct kinds of
situations, all of which can be reported in the same intentional idiom:
1. Cases in which thinking and reasoning take place in an actual natural
language, albeit in an internalized form.
2. Cases in which thinking and reasoning are real events involving lan-
guage-like inner states and processes that have forms analogous to
explicit argumentation but are not in a natural language like English.
3. Cases in which ascriptions of intentional states refer to real events that
play a causal role in the production of behavior but are not themselves
structurally language-like.
4. Cases in which intentional descriptions pick out real patterns—for
example, patterns of normative commitment that are also predictive of
behavior—but do not refer to occurrent states or episodes.
