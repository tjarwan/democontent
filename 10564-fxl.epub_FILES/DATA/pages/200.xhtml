Linguistic Creativity
Language also affords its own forms of creativity. Precisely because gram-
matical sentence formation is relatively unconstrained by model-based
semantics and new sentences can be constructed more or less at will, we can
construct sentences that express things that we might never have thought
of on the basis of model-based thinking. Of course, the vast majority of ran-
domly constructed sentences have little cognitive value. Some are merely
amusing (e.g., puns, at least if you consider puns amusing), others evocative
(poetry). But sometimes they may spur new ways of thinking that prove to
afford genuinely useful new ways of understanding the world. The initial
creative act may not have been a product of insight but might nonetheless
generate new insight.
Higher-Order Cognition
Language also seems to provide the basis for several forms of higher-order
cognition, in which we think thoughts about thoughts or thought struc-
tures. Perhaps some basic forms of higher-order cognition are nonlinguis-
tic, such as simply being aware that I am having a particular sensation,
thought, or experience (though this might better correspond to what is
sometimes called “higher-order perception”; cf. Armstrong 1968, 1984;
Lycan 1996). The analytic power of higher-order cognition, however, seems
to reside in the ability to attend to features of thoughts, and particularly
to either linguistically formulated thoughts or linguistic representations of
those thoughts. We can raise questions about the clarity of a term or con-
