3.1 The Mind in Philosophy, Psychology, and Neuroscience
While philosophy may have been the first discipline to study the mind,
it has been joined by a number of scientific disciplines, sometimes col-
lectively referred to as the cognitive sciences. Not only has each of these
disciplines taken on a life of its own, with professional societies, degree
programs, conferences, journals, and monographs, but the overarching
disciplines like psychology and neuroscience have each branched into a
number of subdisciplines, to such an extent that keeping up with the lit-
erature in any one of them could be a full-time occupation. Some of the
topics studied in these disciplines would be familiar to philosophers and
touch in different ways on topics of central philosophical interest, such as
concepts and reasoning. Others lie far afield from philosophy, such as the
more biological aspects of neurophysiology and neuroanatomy. And others
lie somewhere in between, dealing with topics like perception and emotion
that philosophers have long been aware of, and many philosophers have
written about, but which have tended to remain separate from discussions
of epistemology, semantics, truth, and logic.
It is useful to do at least a brief survey of the sorts of topics that one finds
in textbooks in psychology and neuroscience and compare these with those
discussed by philosophers. In compiling these lists, I have followed the sim-
ple procedure of looking at the tables of contents of some contemporary
textbooks in neuroscience and in cognitive and developmental psychology,
doing some minor rearranging, and omitting some things like neurophysi-
ology and neurochemistry that lie far afield from mainstream philosophical
interests.
