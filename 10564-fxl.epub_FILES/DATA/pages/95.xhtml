6.2 Maps
Scale models, as scale models, share the physical dimensionality and geo-
metric proportions of their targets. One can also have scale models of physi-
cal environments (e.g., people who reenact historical battles with model
soldiers often build scale models of the terrain of the battle they are repris-
ing) or even the whole Earth (a globe). More often, however, we use a dif-
ferent sort of model to represent geographical information: the map. Or
perhaps better, as we shall see, we use various types of maps. For while maps
share as a class the property of being two-dimensional representations of
broadly geographical features, they come in a bewildering number of vari-
eties. But we can bring some order to the variety in terms of just two vari-
ables: what features a map represents, and how it represents them.
Representational Systems and Uses
Consider the maps in figures 6.1 through 6.4, all of which represent approx-
imately the same geographic area in and around Boston. The first map is
a map of roads and light rail lines, drawn to scale. The reproductions here
are grayscale, but in its original form, the map uses particular color conven-
tions to represent land, water, roads, and each of the MBTA train lines. It
also employs labels in plain text for minor roads, and special conventional
symbols for state and federal highways. Such a map affords the ability to
measure distances (at least roughly) between points in Boston, either by a
particular road route or as the crow flies. If the map indicates which roads
are one-way, it also allows the user to plot driving routes. (Having lived in
Boston, I can vouch for the fact that a street map of that city without such
indications is largely useless for driving there.) And it allows one to see,
very easily, what surface points are accessible by rail, and to plan rail routes
involving a change of train.
The second map (fig. 6.2) is much like the first in reproducing the rough
spatial features of the city and the rail lines but does not include streets
other than major highways. It does, however, note rail stations with labels,
whereas the first map indicates only a few major stops and does not label
these. Such a map is intended for use in rail navigation by a person who
already knows where the stations are, or the name of the station that is
her destination, but not how to get there. Precisely because the map omits
other information, it is easier to use if one does not need this other infor-
mation. On the other hand, it is less useful if one needs, say, to determine
which station is closest to a known street intersection.
