Hume’s Problem
The great phenomenologist Edmund Husserl identified a central issue
in philosophy as “Hume’s problem.” Hume was a British empiricist who
viewed perception and thinking as a bundle of independent impressions
and ideas, held together by association. In particular, in his early work A
Treatise of Human Nature (1738), Hume denied that we have a notion of
substance that can be used to knit together the various sensory impressions
we have of the world—red, round, soft—as properties of a single object.
Husserl noted that such an account of perception and thinking does not
provide sufficient resources to explain why we do not simply think of vari-
ous impressions, ideas, or properties but think of a world of objects that
persist over time.
At least two distinct issues are at work here. One, which Husserl became
interested in through his investigation of time consciousness, is how we
are capable of reidentifying something as the selfsame object over successive
perceptions or thoughts. Even if we are capable of constituting something
as an object at time t
1

we are able to additionally link these two thoughts as thoughts about the
same object? To put it crudely, how is it that we do not simply think “an
X” at t

2

2
of “the self-
same X we thought about at t

”? The second and more fundamental issue
is how we are able to think, even in a single thought at a single moment,
not merely of this property and that property being presented but of a single
thing that has both properties. We are clearly able to do so, but a psychology
like Hume’s, whose resources are limited to the presentation of individual
impressions and ideas, cannot explain how we are able to do so.
The Binding Problem
A related problem occurs in neuroscience. Philosophers since Aristotle tradi-
tionally assumed that, in addition to the various sensory modalities—sight,
hearing, touch, smell, taste—there must be an additional faculty, the sensus
communis, or “common sense,” where the deliverances of these modalities
are fused together into a polymodal representation of an object. Scientific
study of the brain has revealed various neuroanatomical areas correspond-
ing to the various sensory modalities, but not of a sensus communis where
“it all comes together.” Indeed, our contemporary understanding of the
brain only makes things more complicated: not only is there no brain area
where vision comes together with all the other modalities in a single repre-
sentation, but there are distinct areas for different aspects of vision: color,
form, and motion.
