These examples, however, share two features that might lead to some
unnecessarily narrow assumptions about mental models. The first is that
they are all taken from the middle ground of understanding—types of cog-
nition that are acquired in mid- to late childhood or adulthood, as opposed
to those found in infants and young children, yet not among the more
rarefied forms of thinking that require very specialized theoretical learning,
like higher mathematics or scientific theories. The second, related to the
first, is that they are all cases in which the acquisition of a mental model is
achieved through some invisible learning process on the basis of bodily and
social engagement with the domain modeled, or with an external model of
it, or with existing social and linguistic ways of thinking and talking about
it. The types of models we have discussed may indeed be acquired in differ-
ent ways from one another—for example, some may be formed largely as
a result of individual bodily and perceptual engagement, and others from
supervised learning—but none of the examples was taken from the forms of
thinking found in very young children that seem to be present too early to
be a result of learning at all, and none from the higher reaches of abstract
theoretical understanding.
This might have suggested to the reader that mental modeling is some-
thing found in the broad swath of everyday commonsense understanding,
but absent from infant cognition and higher theory. If so, this would be a
misleading suggestion. Indeed, the reader may recall that a number of the
main examples of domain-centered understanding described in chapter 4
included several forms of thinking that arise in early development and also
scientific theories. In that context, the point of the examples was to show
that domain specificity was by no means unique to Fodorian modules, and
we would need to move beyond the bifurcation between “modular” and
“central” cognition to find an appropriate way to characterize understand-
ing. While it was this line of thinking that led up to an alternative model-
based view, we have not yet talked about the natural conclusion: that these
forms of understanding are based in mental models as well.
