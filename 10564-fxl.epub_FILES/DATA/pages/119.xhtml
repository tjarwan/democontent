of the doorways, the types of stones in the mantel, the flooring, and so on.
I should note that I fully expect the results of such an exercise to be quite
fallible. Some might be fallible in the ways one would expect specifically
for episodic memories. I might reconstruct the room as being larger than
it actually was because many of my memories might have been from the
perspective of the much smaller body I had as a child. But others might
be fallible in ways that we expect more of a reconstruction of details from
something more abstract. But a reconstruction from what? The answer that
seems to suggest itself here is something like an abstract, schematic model,
perhaps analogous to the information in the data used by an architectural
design program. This answer particularly suggests itself when the imagina-
tive task results in thoughts that are vague or blurry. My mind imposes
some kind of casing around the windows; but if I am to be truly honest, I
have little idea what the contours of that casing were. I remember a few of
the details of the masonry around the fireplace in detail, the rough width
of the grouting between stones, and some vague textures; but beyond that,
my memory of the hearth is something like a blank template waiting to
be filled in. I remember that several of the stairs to the second floor were
visible from the living room, and the rest disappeared behind the wall and
ceiling, but I could not tell you how many without reconstruction that goes
beyond remembering or exploring what is implicit in memory.
Yet all of this still suggests that the basis of my knowledge of my child-
hood home comes not in the form of episodic or propositional memories
alone but in the form of some kind of mental model (or models) of it, and

aptly—through an imaginary recapitulation of the operations I would have
performed to collect the same information if I were bodily present in the
