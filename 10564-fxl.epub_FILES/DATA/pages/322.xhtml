Inferentialists also have something right. The semantic nature of a con-
cept is in large measure determined by its relationships to other concepts
and its constitutive implications. In my view, these are to be found not in
the language-like CLs but in the CMs embedded in models. Feature-based
accounts also have something right. There must be something like feature-
aggregating mechanisms that constitute classes as categories for perception,
and these are part of the network of systems that is a concept. It may be
that different feature-based accounts—say, those involving descriptions,
prototypes, and exemplars—may each have something right at a more fine-
grained level as well. There is no reason to assume that there is just one
way that the mind detects and groups features and uses them as a basis
for categorization. Intensionalist approaches have something right: CMs
define a projective class of things constituted by the properties imputed by
a model. But extensionalists also have something right: concepts, or ele-
ments thereof, aim at tracking some class of things that may not be charac-
terized, or may be mischaracterized, by the CMs.
On this view, particular semantic theories are best viewed as idealized
models of particular factors of semantic understanding. It may be dialecti-
cally useful for a time to set them up as direct competitors, each of which
attempts to explain everything about semantics, because this is an efficient
way of revealing the limitations of each. The moral to draw when such limi-
tations are revealed, however, is not always that a model gets nothing right,
but that there are things the model is not suited to addressing at all, which
might be illuminated by other models. The approach I suggest is to look for
the kinds of psychological mechanisms that might be needed to underwrite
different aspects of concepts and inference, and for how these might inter-
act with one another, even when their interaction does not appear to be
logically tidy. Logical tidiness is a commendable ideal; however, our minds
were designed not to be logically tidy but to do things like track objects and
kinds, make inferences and predictions about them, and learn from our
mistakes. The result of such an investigation may well be that “concepts”
and “semantics” turn out to be more complicated affairs than we might
have supposed. But that is the road forward.
