In some cases, we may be unable to form a representation of the candi-
date content within the model at all. Our spatial intuitions may be unable
to accommodate a representation of a three-dimensional space that curves
back on itself like the two-dimensional surface of a sphere. We can con-
ceive of such things through other means—say, mathematically—but they
seem abidingly counterintuitive because they are dissonant with a deeply
ingrained model of spatial geometry that is more or less Euclidean. Likewise
there is a way in which ways of thinking about things for which I do not
already have a model are bound to seem counterintuitive—that is, they
seem counterintuitive at least until I acquire and master the model. The
pronouncements of contemporary science often seem baffling, nonsensi-
cal, or even plainly impossible at first. They lose something of this air once
we master the scientific model in which they are couched, and may even
seem routine and obvious to the practicing scientist when she is using the
requisite models.
The examples of agential and spatial representation both involve models
that are deeply canalized and difficult to disengage. But similar points can
be made about the intuitiveness of judgments that state basic principles
of models whose use is more voluntary. Within the game of chess, it goes
without saying that each piece in play must occupy a single square on the
board. Indeed, within the model of the game, there is no such thing as a
location halfway between spaces. Of course, we are perfectly capable, using
other means, of seeing pieces as straddling two spaces. We can refuse to
interpret them in terms of the chess model at all, or we can wonder which
space an awkwardly positioned piece is “in” for purposes of the game, or we
can interpret it as being in a particular space for purposes of the game. To
not interpret it as being in a particular space is to not interpret it as being
in play in a game of chess at all.

forward diagonal or can move a third space, capturing the opponent’s pawn.
