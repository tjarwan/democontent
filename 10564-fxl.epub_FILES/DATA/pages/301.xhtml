Let us further distinguish between several claims sometimes associated
with the label atomism.
A1: Semantic independence of concepts: The semantic value of concepts is
fixed at the level of concepts and does not depend on cognitive units other
than concepts (e.g., beliefs, inferential dispositions, mental models).
A2: Atomic and nonatomic concepts: At least some concepts are atomic, in
the sense that their semantic value does not depend on their relations
to other concepts.
A3: No nonatomic concepts: All concepts are atomic in the sense defined in
A2.
The first thesis, the semantic independence of concepts, can be held
independently of the other two. Someone could hold a network theory
of conceptual meaning while denying that a concept’s semantic value is
determined by any other type of cognitive unit like a belief, inferential dis-
position, or mental model. A1 is a denial of inferentialist semantics and
hence is a standard assumption of noninferentialist accounts of semantics,
but the distinguishing features of such accounts are found in how they
go beyond A1 to make claims about how the semantic values of concepts
are fixed. Historically, the predominant account of conceptual semantics
was a definitional view, which viewed most concepts as constructs out of
a relatively small base of atomic concepts that were variously thought to
either be innate ideas or acquired through sensory experience. Given that
we often learn concepts through verbal definitions, this view may strike
many as the most intuitively plausible. However, beginning in the 1970s,
philosophers began to question the definitional view following its critique
by Saul Kripke (1980) and Hilary Putnam (1975). Kripke and Putnam sug-
gested that at least some concepts behave more like proper names, referring
to the objects in their extension not by dint of picking out their properties
but by a kind of direct reference grounded in causal relationships. Jerry
Fodor (1998) and Ruth Millikan (2000) are among the more prominent phi-
losophers who have developed semantic accounts that deny that concepts
have an internal structure, and embrace the somewhat counterintuitive
view (A3) that all concepts are atomic. There are, of course, potential prob-
lems for this theory that do not affect A1 and A2. But as the issues between
