A good deal of scholarly debate has addressed just what sort of skepticism
Hume wished to endorse on all three of these topics. Hume’s views on
causation have drawn by far the most scholarly interest, but the general
form of the interpretive problem can be generalized to the other topics as
well. The Treatise was a work of Hume’s youth, and he seems to have either
backed away from or at least remained silent on some of its more controver-
sial points in his later work, the Enquiry. Hume’s skepticism about causation
can be interpreted in two basic ways. On a milder interpretation, Hume is a
skeptic only in the sense that impressions do not give us a basis for knowl-
edge of real causal relations in the world, because we have no impressions of
causation. This is compatible with the possibility that there is such a rela-
tion, which our mental habit of imputing such a relation fortuitously gets
right, at least in cases where we attribute it correctly. According to the stron-
ger interpretation, causation is not a real relation in the world at all but a
projection of our own associative interpretation onto the world, painting it
from a palette originating in the mind. This stronger interpretation is sug-
gested by Hume’s use of the word ‘illusion’ in his earlier work, the Treatise.
But as we have seen, Kant came to use the word ‘illusion’ in a fashion that
implied not falsity of belief but only a mistaken sense of knowing what in
fact one cannot know. It is possible to read Hume as presaging Kant’s usage,
albeit without the careful distinction Kant made to clarify it.
With respect to how we come to think of a single enduring object on the
basis of many impressions of its different perceivable qualities, it is clearer
that a change has occurred between the two works. In the Treatise, Hume
endorses a “bundle theory” of perceptions, according to which each “per-
ception” (i.e., impression or idea) is completely distinct and autonomous.
Since all our perceptions are different from each other, and from everything else in
the universe, they are also distinct and separable, and may be considered as separate-
ly existent, and may exist separately, and have no need of anything else to support
their existence. (Hume 1738, pt. 4, sec. 5)
There is neither something in which impressions reside (a “mind”) nor
any notion of an “object” or “substance” that is a bearer of the proper-
ties they represent. We have ideas only as copies of previous impressions,
and since we have no impressions of “object” or “substance” in addition
to impressions of things like redness and roundness, we can have no idea
of “object,” either. The common assumption that there are independently
existing objects that persist through changing perceptions therefore cannot
be grounded in Hume’s psychology. So again we are faced with two possible
