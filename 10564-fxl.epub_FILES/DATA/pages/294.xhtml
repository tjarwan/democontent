This is not so uncommon a situation as one might suppose. Consider
the concept
HEALTH. Medicine employs the concept of health but provides
no positive account of it. Instead medicine examines various dimensions
along which an organism can approach or deviate from an unarticulated
ideal standard: various forms of disease, deformity, injury, and dysfunction.
I will call this kind of concept—one of which there is not a single defini-
tive positive account, but various relevant accounts of how an ideal may
be approached or deviated from in different ways—a standard. H
EALTH is a
concept of this type, and so, I suggest, are
KNOWLEDGE and WARRANT. I suspect
that, upon analysis, other important philosophical concepts like
GOODNESS
might turn out to be standards as well.
This analysis involves putting the interpretive tools of cognitive plu-
ralism to the task of interpreting philosophical theories. Philosophical
theories are themselves models, and as such they have particular content
domains that they represent in a particular fashion. They are idealized, and
unlikely to capture everything that might really be important about their
target domains, and hence a single theory may not aptly be applied in
all cases. Indeed, the kind of situation we find in protracted philosophical
debate, in which proponents of “rival” theories each have a set of core cases
that seem to be aptly treated by their theory but not by others, can be diag-
nostic of a situation in which we have multiple models with overlapping
domains, none of which is apt for all the relevant cases.
