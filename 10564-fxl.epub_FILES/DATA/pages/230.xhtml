care more about having good-enough ways of understanding and dealing
with a variety of situations than about how well all that understanding
and all those beliefs hang together as a whole. We need understanding, and
hence the models that supply it. Consistency is something we want—and
some care about it more than others—but it is not something we need.
But in what sense do we need understanding? In one sense, this might
simply mean we have a strong internal drive for it. And it is easy to see why
such a drive would be biologically adaptive: the more about our environ-
ment we understand, the more we can deal with in adaptive ways. What
Hawking and Mlodinow seem to have in mind, however, is something
slightly different: we “need” multiple scientific models if we are to explain
all the data. Most human beings have gotten on quite well without any
understanding of the phenomena that make up the subject matter of con-
temporary physics—we do not need it in a biological sense—but one of the
effects of human cognitive evolution is that our potential range of inter-
ests has expanded well beyond “interests” in the biological sense. But the
“need” here is still a kind of pragmatic need: we need a model M if we are to
understand (and be able to intervene on) X.
