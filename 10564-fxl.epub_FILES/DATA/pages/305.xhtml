16.4 Concepts without Models
I will explore three reasons to think that concepts can exist without being
embedded in mental models. First, in psychology, concepts are often treated
primarily as units of categorization. (And while it is often an assumption
left implicit, what is really central to the discussion is often categorization
in perception.) Categorization can take place without a mental model; and so
if concepts can be (just) tools for categorization, they need not be embed-
ded in models. Second, in some nonhuman animal species, we might have
reason to be skeptical about the presence of the kinds of models that are
relevant to inferential semantics, but still have reason to think that the ani-
mals have concepts (particularly in the sense of things used in perceptual
categorization). And third, concept learning does not seem to require the
mastery of an associated model. Indeed, the process of learning a model
often involves early stages in which we learn to recognize categories of
objects that will be relevant to the model without our yet having mastered
the model.
