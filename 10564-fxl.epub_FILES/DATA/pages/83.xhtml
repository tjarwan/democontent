A representational system may include some of these elements without
others. For example, it is likely that some organisms can track particular
objects in perception but cannot reidentify them as the selfsame object
over repeated encounters, thus lacking the kind of resource needed to track
individuals, as opposed to kind-instances. A representational system may
also include some analogue of nonsemantic machinery such as logical con-
nectives and modal operators. (By “nonsemantic” I mean that they do not
have the functions of tracking sense or reference, though of course they
contribute to the semantic properties of complex representations.)
Let us take some examples with different levels of cognitive sophistication.
Scientific models: A scientific model, such as a theory of gravitation, may be
viewed as a representational system, in the sense of being a system that
provides resources for representing and reasoning about particular types
of states of affairs. A particular gravitational model will have some par-
ticular domain, such as bodies with mass, and will model its objects in
some particular way—for example, as point masses or as extended bodies
with distributed mass. It will track particular properties like mass and per-
haps shape and size, but not others, like charge or color. It will employ
a particular geometry (e.g., Euclidean or Lorentzian) defining a space of
possible locations. And it will possess dynamic laws whose function is to
track state changes due to gravitation over time. Other scientific models
will have different internal ontologies (e.g., living organisms), different
properties and relations (e.g., kinship relations), and different dynamic
principles (e.g., principles of selection).
Commonsense models: A frame-type model of a social situation, such as
behavior at a restaurant, will have an ontology consisting of things like
persons in roles (customer, waiter, host), menus, dishes, utensils, furni-
ture, bills, and the like. Its stock of properties, relations, and transfor-
mations will include things like being seated at a table, ordering, and
paying the bill. The “dynamic principles” will take the form of some-
thing like Schank’s scripts, which track characteristic temporal patterns
of states and transitions.
