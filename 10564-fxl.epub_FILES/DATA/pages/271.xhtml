15 Cognitive Pluralism and Epistemology
The model-based cognitive architecture of cognitive pluralism was devel-
oped as an alternative to the three-tiered standard view of the mind, which
acknowledges concepts, beliefs, and inferences but accords mental models
no fundamental role in understanding. While the standard view is assumed
in many accounts of the nature of cognition, it also figures prominently
in other areas of philosophy: epistemology, semantics, truth theory, and
logic. Indeed, I have suggested that one reason it may recommend itself to
philosophers as a view of cognitive architecture stems precisely from the
fact that it is assumed in these other areas of philosophy. To the extent that
the standard view has served philosophers well by providing a framework
for successful theories in their pursuits, that in itself provides some reason
to suppose that it supplies an adequate categorization of the basic units
of thought. We have now considered the alternative proposal that mental
models are fundamental units of understanding, along with several applica-
tions of this proposal. In these final chapters, I will consider the implica-
tions of cognitive pluralism for theories of knowledge, meaning, and (to a
more limited extent) truth.
This chapter will consider the implications of a model-based cognitive
pluralism for epistemology. Most contemporary epistemologists treat it
as a framing assumption for their discipline that knowledge is warranted
true belief. There are some interesting dissenters from this characteriza-
tion of knowledge (e.g., Sayre 1997), and some philosophers have made a
plausible case that it marks more of a departure from classical epistemol-
ogy than their colleagues suppose, and that, say, Plato’s use of the Greek
word epistēmē might better be translated “understanding” (e.g., Kvanvig
2003; Grimm 2010). There are worthy questions to be pursued here, but for
the purposes of this chapter, I will be engaging what is currently the
mainstream view, and hence assuming that knowledge is warranted
true belief.
