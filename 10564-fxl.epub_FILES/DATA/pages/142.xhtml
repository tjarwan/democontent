I can also coordinate information from the different models: if the
knight is also a heavy statue, I know I will need to use different techniques
to move it than I would if the knight were a small piece of wood or a human
in livery. If I know that I actually have the means to move some pieces
but not others—say, I can pick up the fifty-pound pawn statue but not the
three-thousand-pound knight, or I know that one wooden piece is glued to
the board—it might affect my chess tactics. If I know that a particular actor
in the human chess game has to leave soon, I might deliberately sacrifice
the piece he is playing. I can even think thoughts that seem to cross the
lines of models: I might, for example, wonder how the players in a game of
human chess feel when, as pieces, they are captured.
I will speak of thinking about the same thing—the same object, event,
process, phenomenon, and so on—through different models as triangula-
tion. That we engage in triangulation through different models seems indis-
putable. How we do so is a much more difficult matter. I will in fact suggest
that there must be more than one way of doing so, and I will outline several
in the course of this section. This, however, will not go so far as to try to
describe the mechanisms through which the various types of triangulation
take place. That is a difficult empirical question. There are some candidate
answers in some cases, but I think that in others what I am outlining are
some important and fundamental open questions for further research in
the cognitive sciences.
Three Great Problems
The problem of triangulation has been touched on several times, and in
various forms, in philosophy and the cognitive sciences. In fact, there are at
least three famous and significant problems that seem to be special cases of
it: what Husserl called “Hume’s problem,” the binding problem, and what
Dreyfus calls “the frame problem.”
