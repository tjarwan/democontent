255 Models and Intuition
Saying that intuitive judgments (or a significant class of them) are ones
that can be “read off” and “pop out” from the rules of mental models would
seem to require three things:
1. The content of the judgments is implied by the rules of the model, either
in themselves or as applied to particular cases.
2. There is some mechanism connected with the model that is capable
of producing, on the basis of the model, judgments that track the
implication.
3. There is some quick and unconscious process that compares judgments
with the principles of the model and confers on the judgment the kind
of confidence and sense of self-evidence that is the subjective mark of
intuitively recommended judgments or the cognitive dissonance associ-
ated with counterintuitive judgments.
There are ways these three things can come apart. It is not clear that the
feeling of self-evident correctness that comes with judgments arising from
models one has mastered is different from the feeling that can come from
wish fulfillment or bias, and even experts can make mistakes about which
they feel a great deal of subjective certainty. Conversely, model-bound
mechanisms may produce candidate judgments without a feeling of subjec-
tive certainty. The rules of a model may imply things that there may be no
process for detecting, or which can only be discovered through laborious
explicit reasoning. (For example, all the theorems of Euclidean geometry
are implied by the axioms, but it takes laborious work to prove them, and
hence some might initially strike us as unintuitive or even counterintui-
tive.) And there may be spurious judgment-producing processes that fail
to track the rules of a model dependably, whether because they regularly
produce intuitions that are not underwritten by the rules of the model or
because they sometimes produce errors of execution.
13.3 Model Relativity of Intuitiveness

judgments are characterized relative to particular models. Indeed, a judg-
