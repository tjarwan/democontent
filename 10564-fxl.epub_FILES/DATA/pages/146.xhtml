Perceptual Binding
The perceptual binding problem is probably the case that has received the
most scientific attention. A proposal like Crick and Koch’s phase binding
hypothesis provides an explanation of how information represented in
distinct areas of the brain—say, the visual areas for color and form—gets
coupled. Integration of information from different sensory modalities may
additionally use association areas of the cortex that take inputs from mul-
tiple sources. However, these do not seem to be a sensus communis for pan-
modal representations. Rather, the integration of information from various
modalities into a single percept seems to be a matter of how different areas
are coordinated and act in concert rather than being accomplished by an addi-
tional area. There is no more an anatomical sensus communis than a string
quartet ensemble is an instrument over and above the violins, viola, and
cello. Moreover, the explanation invokes nothing like language-like repre-
sentations, but neural systems in a kind of dynamic resonance pattern. And
whereas a language-like representation supports the combined representa-
tion of two types of information in a single medium, phase binding treats
the situation as involving the coordination of separate representations in
different representational systems and representational media.
Sensorimotor Engagement
Perception is not normally a passive process like a camera recording an
image. It usually takes place as part of engagement with the world, which is
often purposeful and involves motor processes through which an organism
acts on the world, even in the process of exploring it perceptually. Senso-
rimotor engagement involves more than merely binding together informa-
tion from separate perceptual streams. It involves coordinating perceptual,
motor, and orienting systems in a fashion that is propelled by the drives
and interests of the organism and constrained by its physiological and cog-
nitive abilities. Such processes, even when they are primarily “perceptual,”
also evolve as dynamic processes over time, adjusting to explore for new
information in light of present states.
