5.5 Models and Idealization
Models are abstract and idealized. A model is (generally, and perhaps univer-
sally) a model of some particular subset of the features, objects, processes,
and events that make up the world. It abstracts away from most of the
features that make up the world to attend to a few. This confers understand-
ing of particular invariants, generally in accordance with some pragmatic
goal, whether explicit or implicit. Such abstraction is necessary given facts
about both perception and cognition. On the one hand, an organism must
gain information about the world through processes of transduction, and
transducers are sensitive only to particular types of information. On the
other hand, cognitive processing capacities are limited and thus unsuited
to grasping everything at once. Moreover, it is by taking the particular and
organizing it under more general patterns that we become sensitive to the
various types of order in a complex and messy world.
Idealization is a special type of abstraction. I will use the term ‘idealiza-
tion’ for types of abstraction that result in models that can mischaracterize
how the world actually is in ways that can matter. Of course, what matters
is relative to particular pragmatic goals. Treating objects as point masses,
as we do in some physical models, may matter for some problems but not
for others. Elsewhere (Horst 2007), I have distinguished several types of
idealization:
• Bracketing idealizations: These idealize by ignoring features that are pres-
ent in the real world. For example, a gravitational model brackets other
forces like charge and wind resistance, which most bodies with mass are
also subject to. Hence bodies generally do not move exactly as described
by gravitational models. A restaurant script brackets many other things
that may in fact happen in restaurants—a customer choking, or the fire
alarm going off.
• Distorting idealizations: These idealize by modeling their subject domains
in ways that are not fully accurate, such as treating bodies as point
masses, or employing classical gravitational models that do not reflect
the curvature of space or temporal frame relativity.
