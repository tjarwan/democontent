16 Cognitive Pluralism and Semantics
Many sorts of things are said to “be meaningful” or “have semantic con-
tent.” Some of these are mental: concepts, beliefs and other dispositional
intentional states, judgments and other occurrent intentional states. Oth-
ers are linguistic: words, phrases, sentences, speech acts, utterances, inscrip-
tions. Some would say that logic has its own stock of meaningful units,
which are neither truly mental nor truly linguistic: bound variables, con-
stants, predicate letters, Fregean propositions, well-formed formulas. And
these meaningful entities come in a variety of sizes as well: word-sized units
(lexical units in a language, concepts, predicate letters), sentence-sized
units (sentences, utterances, speech acts, beliefs, judgments, propositions,
well-formed formulas), and larger units such as arguments.
In one sense, we thus have a number of different types of “semantic
units,” differing in compositional size and in whether they are mental, lin-
guistic, or abstractly logical. But we might also ask whether one of these
types of units is fundamental. Discussions of this topic generally list either
two or three candidate answers. Perhaps most common is a two-way divi-
sion between the options of atomism and holism:
Semantic atomism locates meaning at the level of individual words (the meaning
of each word is in it); semantic holism locates meaning at a broader level, ranging
from an indefinite network of linked units to an entire language. (Engler 2011, 266)
The tradition of formal semantics has been resolutely atomistic, in the sense that the
assignment of a semantic interpretant to one element (say, a proper name) is taken
to be intelligible independently of the assignment of semantic interpretants to any
other elements (for instance, predicates or other proper names). One does not need
to know anything about what other dots represent, or what blue wavy lines repre-
sent, in order to understand that a particular dot stands for Cleveland on a map.
