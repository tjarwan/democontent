2. Dispositionalism: S believes that P just in case S is disposed to readily
endorse P when appropriately prompted. The immediacy and con-
fidence with which you assent to “dogs have kidneys” or “117 + 8 =
125” is a sign of, perhaps even constitutive of, the fact that you already
believed these propositions. This may be true so far as it goes, but it does
not tell us much about the psychology of belief. Attributing dispositions
does not describe a mechanism so much as it points to a need to specify
one.
3. The intentional stance: Belief attribution involves adopting a stance of
interpreting a system in terms of beliefs and desires—namely, those that
the system ought to have if it is to be rational. Sometimes this inten-
tional stance allows us to pick out real patterns in the world; but because
the attribution is normatively rational rather than factual, it does not
require the attribution of individual occurrent states corresponding
to those beliefs and desires. When the stance works for predicting the
behavior of a system S, S counts as a genuine believer, regardless of the
psychologically real causes of its behavior. This, however, underplays
both the fact that at least sometimes the causes are actual conscious
judgments and the important possibility that there are also inner states
that share much in common with judgments even though they are not
conscious. The stance-stance may describe what we are doing in attribut-
ing intentional states in many cases, but like dispositionalism, it tells us
nothing about the underlying psychology. Moreover, assumptions about
S’s rationality and S’s having the information it needs about its envi-
ronment present problems in using the intentional strategy to explain
behavior that is a result of irrational processes (e.g., faulty reasoning) or
misinformation.
