What we have arrived at here are the elements for explanations of
various types of disunities of belief and understanding in terms of the fea-
tures of our cognitive architecture. If understanding comes in the form
of models, and each model employs a particular representational system
that is optimized for particular ends, it is quite possible for two or more
models to carve up the world in incompatible ways, to be formally incom-
patible, and to license incompatible inferences. Two models can each
be eminently apt for their own purposes and yet not play well together.
Given the assumption of a model-based cognitive architecture, we should
expect to find such disunities, and the thesis about cognitive architecture
supplies a kind of diagnosis of their source. In some ways, it is a defla-
tionary diagnosis: conflicts in belief or understanding need not imply that
“the world is disunified” (whatever that might mean) but may be artifacts
of the cognitive architecture through which our minds understand the
world.
Yet in another way, this is also a disconcerting insight. It may be that
the disunities we find today are things we can bootstrap our way beyond,
by finding a better set of models that do not conflict in the same ways, and
perhaps even finding a single super model of everything that is self-consis-
tent and allows all the legitimate insights provided by our existing models.
But it could also turn out that this is impossible—that either features of our
cognitive architecture itself or their combination with the particular set of
things we encounter and care about in the world requires multiple models
to provide maximal epistemic and practical traction on the world, and in
some cases these models may, in combination, produce inconsistencies and
paradoxes. If this is the case, certain disunities of belief and understanding
may be principled and abiding for minds like ours.
