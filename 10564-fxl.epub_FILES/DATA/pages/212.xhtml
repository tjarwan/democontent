While pure mathematics is special in several ways, we can understand
some of the basic issues most clearly by considering it first. Consider two
geometric systems: a two-dimensional Euclidean geometry and Riemann’s
geometry of the surface of a sphere. Each can be described by a system of
axioms and theorems, and indeed they share a substantial number of axi-
oms, differing chiefly with respect to Euclid’s parallel postulate. In Euclid-
ean space, a line stretches indefinitely in two directions, and for any point
P not on a line L, there is exactly one line through P that is parallel to
L—that is, the two lines do not intersect. On the surface of a sphere, the
equivalent of a line is a geodesic, like the earth’s equator. Start at any point
and continue in any direction, and eventually you will return to the point
of origin. There are no parallel lines on the surface of a sphere. (Cartogra-
phers do speak of “parallels,” but they are not really lines as defined in the
system, nor are they parallel in the relevant mathematical sense. To follow
a cartographic “parallel” around a sphere, you would have to orient your
path slightly to the left or the right. And two geodesics—say, the equator
and the Greenwich meridian—intersect at two opposite points.) The paral-
lel postulate is true in Euclid’s geometry but false in Riemann’s. And there
are other differences as well: for example, the sum of the interior angles
of a triangle on the surface of a sphere will always exceed 180 degrees;
the Pythagorean theorem is false in a spherical geometry, as you can
have a triangle with three right angles; and so on. If you reasoned from
premises taken from the two geometries, it would be easy to generate a
contradiction. Neither geometric system can be derived from the other,
and there is no self-consistent third system from which all the axioms
of both can be derived, because to produce the contrary axioms and
theorems, the third system would have to already contain contradictory
assumptions within it.
