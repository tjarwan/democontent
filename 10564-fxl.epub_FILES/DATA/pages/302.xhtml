From the cognitive pluralist’s standpoint, the problem with semantic
atomism is that it has no way of accounting for the constitutive connec-
tions within a model between concepts, beliefs, inferential dispositions,
and capacities for perception and action. A change in my understanding
of how a chess bishop is permitted to move is a change in my concept of
a chess bishop as well. This is similar to familiar holist arguments against
atomism, with the important difference that the cognitive pluralist holds
that there is a principled difference between the situations one finds within
a model and outside or between models.
Additionally, the cognitive pluralist is not required to hold either that
the atomist story has nothing right or that there cannot be concepts about
which it tells the best story. What the cognitive pluralist needs to insist on
is that, in the case of model-based understanding, the semantics of a concept
must be constitutively linked to those of other concepts, and to the rules,
representational system, and inferential commitments of the model. It is
compatible with this idea that there are also cases where we have concepts
that are not grounded in this way in models. Indeed, I think that, in cer-
tain learning contexts, concepts can be acquired (at least initially) without
being associated with a well-formed model. When we encounter a new kind
of thing without yet having any concrete understanding of it, the concept
may well take a form very like a conceptual atom. And when we learn a
concept through a verbal definition, it may have constitutive dependencies
on the other concepts through which it is defined but not yet be embed-
ded in a model. I tend to expect that these are primarily characteristics
of learning processes that normally lead toward embeddedness in one or
more models, but this is an empirical hypothesis that would need careful
exploration.
Holism
Conceptual holism is the thesis that all our concepts are constitutively
interdefined, such that a change to any one of them would amount to a
change in all of them. Moreover, holist theses are generally not confined
to semantics. The holism found in Quine (1951) and Davidson (1967), for
example, posits constitutive interconnections between all of a person’s con-
cepts, beliefs, and inferential commitments.
