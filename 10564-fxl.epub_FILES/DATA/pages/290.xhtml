One possibility is that a belief should be counted as properly basic just in
case it is warranted, but not in a fashion derived from logical inference. In
this interpretation, judgments produced by the apt application of models
might indeed count as properly basic. But if this is all that “properly basic”
means, there might be any number of noninferential sources of warrant,
and all that groups them into a class is a feature they lack: they are not
processes of logical inference. This might not be such a problem if they
also shared some feature that rendered them in some recognizable sense
foundational. But it is not clear how the metaphor of a “foundation” should
be understood once one has abandoned stronger forms of foundational-
ism. Perhaps it should be understood epistemically: foundational beliefs
are those about which there are no further epistemic questions to ask. Or
perhaps it should be understood psychologically, in terms of the origins
of beliefs and judgments: the foundational ones are the first epistemically
evaluable ones in a process, or perhaps ones without which no other beliefs
or judgments could be warranted.
It seems plausible to me that beliefs based in models may fit some such
psychological criteria: without models we would have no beliefs at all, and
these are the beliefs on which any other epistemic processes operate. How-
ever, most models are learned, and in learning a model, we often engage
in some sort of process of confirming that it is a good model, at least for
particular epistemic and practical ends. The process of confirmation may
be unconscious, implicit, and nonargumentative (though in cases like sci-
entific models, a good deal of explicit reasoning often goes into building
and confirming them), but models are not really “epistemic foundations”
in the sense of being “givens” whose epistemic credentials can be taken
for granted. As a consequence, the production of judgments from models
does not seem to be an epistemological foundation, either. There are fur-
ther epistemological questions about judgments produced by models: ques-
tions about whether the model is a good model of its domain, whether it is
aptly applied in this circumstance, and whether the judgment produced is
the best judgment among the options available from the model. There are
likewise further epistemological questions about models themselves and
the beliefs expressing their systematic commitments: about how they are
idealized, and about what sorts of cases the models are apt for and hence
