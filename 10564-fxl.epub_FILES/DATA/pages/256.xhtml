14.2 Illusions of Unrestricted Assertion
The examples in the last section involved applications of models to par-
ticular cases. But when we move from practice to theory, we do not merely
use models; we attempt to make their principles explicit and assert them.
In science, it is well understood that any theory or model has boundary
conditions governing where it may aptly be applied. Fully understanding an
area of science requires understanding the boundary conditions as well as
the theories. But particularly in the process of learning, understanding the
model and understanding its boundary conditions are often distinct cogni-
tive achievements. In science education, a model may be introduced using
problems taken from its core area of applicability, and the student may
learn only later (or not at all) what cases it can and cannot aptly be applied
to. Reports of scientific models in the popular press often lack any discus-
sion of boundary conditions. And indeed, someone who does not really
understand the science may make remarkable mistakes of scope, such as
thinking that everything about an organism must be an adaptation.
Outside science, the scope of a model’s aptness and the ways it is idealized
are seldom explored as carefully as the boundary conditions of a scientific
model. The form of generalizations in a language—“dogs are four-legged,”
“people act on the basis of their beliefs and desires”—leaves the precise
implications of such claims quite vague, and we often do not really know
how broadly or exactly they apply. General claims are a blunt instrument
from a logical standpoint. And this has its advantages: few generalizations
could accurately be expressed as, say, universally quantified claims; and if
we are often in the position of knowing that they are applicable in a broad
range of cases, but not exactly which cases, it is useful to have a manner of
expressing them that leaves such questions open. (We arguably could not
do this if our forms of judgment were limited to the logical forms available
in quantified first-order predicate calculus.) We may implicitly know that
the generalizations are idealized, but not know exactly how they are ideal-
ized. The risk, of course, is that we forget that we are dealing with idealized
claims and mistake them for perfectly universal claims. We can apparently
