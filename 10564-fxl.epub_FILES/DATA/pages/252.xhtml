had to work with. Of course, one can also apply a model inaptly even if one
already has the wherewithal to know that it is not aptly applied there.
If we assume that (1) models can give rise to intuitions about what will
or even must be the case, (2) models are idealized in ways that render them
apt only under certain conditions, and (3) we do not always know where a
model is aptly applied and may even be unaware that there are restrictions
on its apt application, then we should expect that there would be cognitive
illusions that arise when a model is applied inaptly but we are unaware of
the inaptness. And such unawareness can arise in several ways: as a simple
mistake (we know the conditions for apt application but forget that a par-
ticular case lies outside them), out of ignorance that a particular case is of
a type that lies outside the conditions, or out of a deeper naïveté about the
idealized character of models in general or about the particular model we
are using.
Discussions of “cognitive illusions” in psychology are principally con-
cerned with inapt applications of models. Consider Deborah Kelemen’s
notion of “promiscuous teleology.” Teleological thinking is a common fea-
ture of maturationally normal human minds. It is also found in very young
children, perhaps predating causal explanation. While scientifically tutored
adults tend to reserve teleological explanations for artifacts and (perhaps
with some qualms and hedging) for organisms, Kelemen’s research points
to a developmental pattern in which children at first apply teleological
models much more broadly, and in preference to causal models.
