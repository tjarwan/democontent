understanding of falling bodies, which, while less accurate and projectable
than general relativity or even classical mechanics, is better for catching
falling vases or avoiding falling off cliffs. And even in scientific contexts,
we may need multiple models for phenomena like collisions at different
energy levels, partly because the particles themselves behave differently,
and partly because the most useful ways we have of observing, conceiving,
and acting on particle collisions differ with context.
So we have a first reason for a plurality of models: the world demands it.
There are many real patterns in the world, and the most efficient way—per-
haps the only way—to model them is one by one. (There are often ways of
combining them, such as vector algebra, but this presupposes already pos-
sessing models of the individual forces.)
An Objection: Most Patterns Are Not Fundamental
Now, in some contexts, one might object that “many real patterns” can
turn out to all be consequences of a single, more fundamental phenom-
enon. What we now call gravitation was once thought to consist of two dis-
tinct sets of principles: one for celestial bodies circling in the heavens, and
another for falling bodies beneath the sphere of the moon. Electromagne-
tism and strong and weak forces were all accommodated within quantum
theory, and many physicists expect there to be a unified field theory that
will fold gravity into a single theory with them. This is a legitimate question
in philosophy of science, one that I return to in part 3. But it will not do as
an argument about what is psychologically possible.
Could God have made creatures ab initio that thought about the world
solely in terms of the best theories we now possess, or a unified field theory,
if there is such a thing? Perhaps. It’s never a good gamble to bet against
what God could do. Could an organism that thought in this way, and only
in this way, be produced by natural selection? Probably not. Organisms that
attained this sort of very general understanding would probably have to do
so the way we have: by learning models of many more specific phenomena
