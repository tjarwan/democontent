One basic difference between my cognitive pluralism and Kant’s philos-
ophy is that my theory locates much of understanding as arising from ide-
alized domain-specific models rather than from sentence-sized judgments
and arguments composed of such judgments. We have seen that there are
ways that the idealization of models can result in a kind of epistemic dis-
unity. I have speculated that sometimes there are incompatibilities between
apt models that are principled and enduring, but I have also endorsed
attempts to reconcile incompatibilities as a regulative principle. And it is
clear that attending to problems that are signaled by having incompatible
ways of understanding things often produces intellectual advances, such as
corrections to models we have previously used, discoveries of better mod-
els (sometimes ones that cover the domains of two preexisting models),
and second-order ways of conceiving the relationships between models,
such as seeing one as a limiting case of another. Sorting out such problems
differs from sorting out inconsistencies between propositions or trying to
unify propositions into a more comprehensive system without attending
to models. By the same token, the ways we try to unify models can lead to
types of illusion that are not apparent if we attend only to propositions and
arguments.
The most basic such illusion is perhaps to assume that all the things that
we justifiably believe on the basis of apt models should be consistent with
one another. Consistency is, after all, a norm of logic. And it is an extremely
powerful norm, as it gives rise to a strong intuitive assumption that two
inconsistent claims cannot both be true. But we now have several reasons
to look past this compelling intuitive assumption.
To begin with, the norm of consistency is a norm for sentence-sized
units: propositions, judgments, beliefs, claims, assertions, sentences. The
introduction of models as a different type of cognitive unit—one that is in
some ways prior to sentence-sized units, as it is models that define a space of
possible propositional representations—complicates matters. What “consis-
tency” means when comparing two models is different from what it means
in comparing two propositions. Euclidean and non-Euclidean geometries
are inconsistent with each other, but this does not mean that at least one
of them is false. Indeed, it makes no sense to speak of a pure mathematical
system being “false,” or of a pure mathematical claim being either true-
full-stop or false-full-stop. Mathematical systems simply are the systems
they are, and the truth of a pure mathematical claim like the Pythagorean
