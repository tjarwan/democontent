225 Disunities of Knowledge, Science, and Understanding
The question of whether there are such principled and abiding disuni-
ties, I think, cannot be settled decisively in advance or by purely theoretical
considerations. We cannot know a priori how many independent principles
are at work in the world, and so any intuition that there should be just one
must reflect a cognitive bias. Nor will it do to appeal to an intuition that
“the world must be comprehensive and self-consistent,” because the ques-
tions about unifying belief and understanding depend not solely on how
the world is but on how we are able to understand it, and hence involve
empirical questions about human cognition. On the other hand, the mere
fact that our present ways of understanding the world are disunified in vari-
ous ways does not entail that we cannot achieve better ways of understand-
ing it that are better unified. There is no way of ruling out in advance a
new insight that would change everything, because we could only evaluate
its prospects by knowing what it was, and by definition future insights are
unknown. Whether disunities of belief and understanding are principled
and abiding is ultimately an open-ended empirical question.
So here is a second important point. A model-based architecture for
understanding can (and does) result in de facto disunities of belief and
understanding but does not itself answer the question of whether such dis-
unities are a kind of principled limitation on knowledge or understanding.
It does give us reason to be suspicious of any intuitions we may have to the
effect that there should ultimately be a single, all-encompassing model of
everything, or that we should be able to find ways to reconcile all our con-
flicting beliefs. There is no reason to assume that the evolutionary forces
that shaped our cognitive architecture would have done so in a fashion
that ensures such a capacity. (And even if one believes that there was a
certain amount of special intervention by God in the design of human
minds, there is little reason to think that the design would have included
the capacity to have a fully unified understanding. Perhaps the sense of
mystery engendered by paradoxes that cannot be resolved in this mortal
life is precisely what God was after.)
The only kind of evidence we have on such a question would have to
come from looking at cases. And here we find things that pull us in two
contrary directions. The histories of philosophy of science are filled with
examples of surprising unifications—say, that the things we see in the sky
are not made of a different sort of matter operating on different princi-
ples, and the orbits of the planets and the behavior of falling bodies are
explained by a single principle of gravitation. But they are also filled with
