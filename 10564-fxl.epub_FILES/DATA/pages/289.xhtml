Some versions of modest foundationalism, however, have in fact explored
ways that the theory can be supplemented with other sorts of resources,
and at least one of these suggests a way that a modest foundationalist might
attempt to incorporate insights about mental models. One long-standing
problem for epistemology was that of how sensation and perception can
warrant beliefs. Alvin Goldman (1979) proposed a reliabilist account of
this: perceptual beliefs can be properly basic by dint of being produced by
perceptual mechanisms that reliably produce true beliefs. While reliabilism
has gone on to be regarded as an epistemological theory supplying its own
account of warrant, Goldman’s account is often also classified as a founda-
tionalist account, augmented with a reliabilist story about how a certain
class of judgments come to be properly basic. This, in turn, suggests a way
that a modest foundationalist might embrace models as playing a role in
warranting beliefs and judgments. If, like perceptual mechanisms, models
reliably generate true judgments when aptly applied, at least some judg-
ments produced from models might count as properly basic.
I prefer to leave it to the foundationalists to decide what should be
counted as a variant on foundationalism, or a supplement to it, and what
needs to be viewed as a nonfoundationalist account. Clearly the core com-
mitments of foundationalism leave open questions about the noninferen-
tial sources of warrant, and I believe that some of these are derived from
model-based understanding. Just how model-based understanding can be
combined with a foundationalist account is constrained by the fact that
foundationalists group all noninferential knowledge together in the cat-
egory of properly basic beliefs. So whether beliefs that derive their war-
rant from models can be accommodated within a basically foundationalist
framework will depend on how one understands what it means for a belief
to be properly basic. Strong foundationalism, of course, has extremely high
standards for proper basicality, and judgments produced from model-based
mechanisms do not seem to meet those standards. I have explored how
