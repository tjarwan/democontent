Language, or at least a language-like medium for thinking, also seems
to be a prerequisite for particular forms of reasoning. Most obviously, it
is needed for explicit argumentation in which the syntactic form of the
reasoning is crucial. A language with names is a medium particularly well
suited to representing and exploiting an open-ended wealth of informa-

examine, test, and refine. I discuss these topics further in chapter 11.
How One Model Tests Another
Finally, the use of multiple models can in various ways help us refine the
application and assess the aptness of each. In particular, different and espe-
cially contradictory results that emerge from the application of two models
(discussed in the following section) may raise questions about the aptness
of one or both in a particular context and may raise more abstract questions
about the class of cases in which a model is aptly applied. In some cases, it
may also lead to alterations in one or both models. These situations may
often call for a kind of metacognition—for thinking about models rather
than simply through them—that may not be available to nonhuman ani-
mals. However, we probably also have reason to think that there are ways,
not involving metacognition, that a conflict between models can lead to
changes in one or both models.
8.5 Dissonance
Using more than one model can help us to understand a more complex real-
ity than either model could allow us to understand alone. But at the same
time, sometimes the different models we use do not play well together, and
generate puzzles and paradoxes. Sometimes they lead to contrary expecta-
tions and predictions. Sometimes they are actually incompatible with one
another. We can, however, understand some of these problems by looking
at the features of models as models.
