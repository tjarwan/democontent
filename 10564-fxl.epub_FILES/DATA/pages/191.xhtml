Consider an example. Suppose I am told (in language), “Napoleon was
short and vain.” Understanding this sentence allows me to make a variety
of inferences: that Napoleon was short, that he was vain, that he was less
than seven feet tall, that he would be likely to react badly to criticism, that
there was at least one person who was both short and vain, and so on. How
much of this understanding is a result of either linguistic competence or a
capacity for language-like thinking? Of course, the mere ability to parse the
sentence and make any sense of it at all requires competence in English.
Moreover, inference from a sentence of the form “x is P and Q” to “x is P” or
to “there is something that is P and Q” might well be based on understand-
ing how the language works, and likewise for any syntactically based infer-
ences I might make using it as a premise. But my understanding of what
heights are counted as short when dealing with human beings, or what
vanity is, or how a vain person is likely to respond to criticism is based not
in truly linguistic competence but in having models of the relevant content
domains. A person can have perfectly good linguistic competence and lack
the knowledge of a particular content domain that he or she would need to
make a semantically based inference.
The lexical items in a public language or a system of language-like
thought do indeed somehow tap into the separate forms of understand-
ing afforded by models: linguistic utterances and language-like thoughts
can express what I understand through models. And if someone is learn-
ing a language, part of what she has to learn is what lexical items tap
into which models. Someone learning English as a second language, for
example, may already have a mental model of vanity but not know that it
is expressed in English by the word ‘vanity’. But if the problem is that she
is a competent English speaker but has somehow never formed an under-
standing of vanity, this problem cannot be corrected by further learning of
the language. Rather, she needs to become familiar with the phenomenon
of vanity and form a mental model that tracks its salient characteristics.
Someone whose social cognition was impaired in ways that rendered her
incapable of understanding vanity might be able to learn what sorts of
things people say about people labeled “vain” and mimic them, but this
would not amount to an understanding of vanity. She would merely be
faking it when the conversation strays onto that topic. Her deficit in social
cognition, however, need not imply any impairment of her competence
in language.
