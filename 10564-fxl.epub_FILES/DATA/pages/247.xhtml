If beliefs are dispositions that are grounded in stable features of models
plus mechanisms that produce or confirm judgments in a noninferential
manner based on their content when prompted, we have a characteriza-
tion of beliefs that grounds a form of psychological realism but is not com-
mitted to the thesis that dispositional beliefs are sentence-like entities in
the mind.
Moreover, we can also explain the fact that imputations of belief are
also imputations of normative commitments: beliefs of the type I have
described are implied by the implicit rules of their models of origin, and
hence one is normatively committed to them when one is using the model.
Of course, different models have different commitments, and those of one
model may conflict with those of another. This would seem to imply that
we have conflicting dispositional beliefs in such cases. But this is actually
potentially a point in favor of the account. Numerous psychological stud-
ies have shown that the judgments we are disposed to make are highly
subject to context and priming. So it will not do to simply characterize
beliefs as dispositions to judgments without further qualification. More-
over, contexts do not simply magically produce changes in disposition.
We need some way of connecting different contexts with the different
judgments they evoke. One plausible way of doing this is to suppose that
context and priming activate particular models, and as a result, one is
biased toward producing the judgments that are commitments of those
models and not others.
Not all dispositions to form judgments work in this way. The timid per-
son is disposed to judge himself in danger. In a sixty-degree room, I am in
some sense disposed to judge that it is a bit chilly. These dispositions are
probably not based in mental models. But by the same token, it is not clear
that we should think of them as involving beliefs, either. The timid person
has a low trigger point for judgments of danger, as well as for various types
of vigilance, but it strikes me as more appropriate to say that he is always on
the lookout for danger and ready to impute it than that he always believes
himself in danger. Nonetheless it may be that cases exist in which we would
attribute beliefs that cannot be explained on the basis of the implicit com-
mitments of models, and so the hypothesis should be taken as an explana-
tion for at least one class of belief attributions.
