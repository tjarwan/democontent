In addition, there are times when we humans learn a concept without
having the sort of understanding that allows us to reason with it. We may
have concepts we can use only to identify things as members of a category.
And even in the process of acquiring a model—say, when we learn to play
chess—there is an early stage at which we are able to do things like iden-
tify the various classes of pieces but do not yet know how to use or think
about them as chess pieces. At this stage, I have a concept that I use the
word ‘knight’ to express. It is not yet the concept

There are, of course, issues of terminological choice here: Should we talk
about cognitive units that are used only for categorization as “concepts” or
find some other term for them? And, particularly in the case of much sim-
pler animal minds, should we use the word ‘concept’ at all, or some other
term, such as ‘protoconcept’? The choices we make here might make a big
difference in avoiding misunderstandings in the context of a comprehen-
sive account of concepts, but my ambitions here are far more modest. And
because I will be arguing that there is a sense in which “the same concept”
can endure as a psychological continuant over changes in its properties, I
will not make any such terminological distinctions yet.
