Again, for the most part, these verbal differences do not strike me as con-
trary to my claim that scientific understanding, including the understand-
ing embodied in laws and theories, ultimately depends on mental models.
Writers who distinguish models from theories often do so to emphasize
ways in which scientific reasoning depends on models (Magnani and Nerses-
sian 2002; Magnani, Nersessian, and Thagard 1999). If we need models as
“mediators” (Morgan and Morrison 1999) between laws or theories (under-
stood as “explicitly formulated principles” [Giere 2004, 744]) and specific
contexts, then the understanding of the theory requires such models. I have
no wish to claim that science does not also include equations, propositional
representations, and symbolic operations over them. My claim is that the
ability to work with equations and other symbolic representations in a
purely formal way does not itself amount to understanding of a scientific
domain, and such understanding is supplied by mental models.
Another use of the term ‘model’ in philosophy of science, however,
is distinctly different from mine. This usage is taken over from logic and
model theory. Model theory is used in the examination of formal systems
in mathematics and logic. A mathematical or logical system is a set of axi-
oms and their inferential consequences under the derivation rules of the
system. Such systems are formal in the sense that they do not have interpre-
tations of the terms assigned. A model of such a system is an assignment of
interpretations to the terms of the system. In logic, this might be an assign-
ment of a domain of objects to the referring terms and of relations to the
two-value predicates. In mathematics, the integers under addition would
count as a model of a group-theoretic structure with a particular type of
operation and properties like transitivity. A “model” in this model-theoretic
sense can serve a number of purposes. It can be used as a heuristic tool
for understanding the abstract structure through a more familiar particular
mathematical system. But it can also be used to test the structure for proper-
ties like consistency and closure.
