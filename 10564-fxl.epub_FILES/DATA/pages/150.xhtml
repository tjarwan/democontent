The ability to reidentify specific individuals seems to require a particular
kind of cognitive resource: concepts that are namelike rather than generic.
Kind concepts (
DOG) and property concepts (MALE, SPOTTED) are by nature
things that can be applied to multiple individuals. Namelike concepts, by
contrast, have the function of picking out and tracking just one thing. This
strikes me as likely to be a special kind of concept, one that is not required
for having ways of tracking kinds and variable properties, and one that has
probably appeared comparatively recently in evolutionary history, though
perhaps independently in several widely separated taxa.
Information Passing
While thinking within a model may often take place more or less autono-
mously, we are also often able to pass information between models. In this
way, at least some model-based reasoning is not entirely encapsulated in
the way that Fodor described modules.
2
My mental model of a social situ-
ation may supply general anticipations about what to expect, but I may
need different types of information, not supplied by that model, to achieve
a more fine-grained understanding of the situation and to make better
predictions. Some aspects of how a waiter will behave—at least while “on
script”—may be supplied by my model of restaurant etiquette. But oth-
ers might require me to know something about his individual psychology:
whether he is passive-aggressive, what his cultural background is, whether
he is an experienced waiter or in training, what other roles he plays in the
restaurant or outside it, what sort of mood he is in. It requires different sorts
of models to understand these things, but if I do grasp them, they can affect
how I run the restaurant model in this particular case.
The analogy that recommends itself to me here is that of parameter pass-
ing in computer programming. Contemporary programming is generally
highly modularized, with distinct subprograms used for different opera-
tions. The different code modules, however, are not simply different opera-
tions that run in parallel; often one calls on another and passes and receives
values of variables called parameters. One module M1 might, for example,
need the area of a triangle but only have information about the length of
the sides. There is a formula for computing area on the basis of the lengths
of the sides, called Heron’s formula.
