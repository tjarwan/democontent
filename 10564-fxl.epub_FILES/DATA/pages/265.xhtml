A fourth, characteristic of Newton’s approach to
mechanics, is to attempt to view different models as expressing indepen-
dent factors that can be recombined (in Newtonian mechanics, through
vector algebra) to get a global picture. There is also a family of strategies,
which I hesitate to call “unifying,” that we might label “contextual” or
“relativistic.” Their basic approach is to say that different models allow us
to say true things, but in a fashion hedged by contexts. I pick these examples
out because they are familiar and important, but I do not wish to suggest
that this list is exhaustive. Indeed, I regard the list of unifying strategies
as open-ended, as there may be unifying strategies that have not yet been
recognized.
Each of these strategies has arguably been successfully applied to resolve
particular problem cases. What is striking from the standpoint of intellec-
tual history, however, is the confidence with which a particular strategy
has often been endorsed, along with a picture of how things would look
if it could successfully be applied, well in advance of a serious attempt to
apply it. This is, to my mind, particularly striking in the case of reductionist
strategies. Pre-Socratic philosophers seem to have been remarkably confi-
dent that everything must be composed of one of the “prime elements”
of earth, air, fire, water, or aether, though they differed over which one.
Pythagoreans shared a similar confidence that everything was ultimately
numbers. Early modern materialists and some of the twentieth-century log-
ical empiricists had a similar confidence that the special sciences could be
reduced to basic physics, and early proponents who had little scientific evi-
dence to go on (like Hobbes) were no less confident than those who came
after. Indeed, the intuitive appeal of reductionism seems to endure even in
the face of strong arguments from philosophers of science that true inter-
theoretic reductions are rare (Horst 2007). Hobbes and Descartes also
seemed to have had a further ambition of seeing physical properties as ulti-
mately geometric in nature. And many contemporary theoretical physicists
seem to regard the prospect of a unified field theory as something that must
be true.

answer is a variant on Kant’s general story about the drive toward unifica-
