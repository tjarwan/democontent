While Fodor’s notion of a module is widely used in philosophy and the
cognitive sciences, it has also received a great deal of criticism, much of
which can in some measure be traced to the looseness of his characteriza-
tion. Recall that Fodor does not tell us how his various criteria for modular-
ity are supposed to fit together—for example, whether they are supposed to
jointly supply necessary and sufficient conditions. Some things he says—
such as that some of the features are not all-or-nothing affairs but admit of
degrees—might suggest a weaker interpretation; but his conclusion that the
number of modular systems is quite small, limited more or less to process-
ing of perceptual inputs, motor outputs, and language comprehension, sug-
gests that he intends the criteria to be fairly stringent, and he clearly resists
the tendency of some other writers to speak of “modules” wherever a func-
tional decomposition of cognitive abilities occurs (cf. Fodor 2001, 56–57).
Criticisms have therefore come on a number of fronts. Some critics, such
as Jesse Prinz, claim that Fodor’s criteria are too stringent jointly, and in
some cases even individually, to be applied even to the few things that
Fodor considers to be modular. On the other hand, there are any number of
cognitive abilities that share some but not all of Fodor’s criteria.
Fodor’s criteria for modularity do not carve out interesting divisions in the mind.

erties on Fodor’s list. At best, these systems have components that satisfy some of
