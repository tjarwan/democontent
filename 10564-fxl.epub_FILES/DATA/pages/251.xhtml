with the question of how light would behave if shined through two slits
in a partition, Newton would confidently have predicted a result based in
his particle theory. What the particle model would predict if applied to the
situation is fairly straightforward: particles, moving in straight lines, should
result in two bands of light corresponding to the two slits. Indeed, between
the evidence from other types of experiments and the fact that the idea that
light travels in straight lines appeals to common sense (no doubt rooted in
some folk model), Newton might well have assumed that light would have
to go through the slits in straight lines and produce two bands of light on
the opposite wall. But this is not what actually happens. Instead the setup
produces a diffusion pattern of the sort one might expect if light behaved
like a wave.
At the least, the experiment shows that Newton’s corpuscular model
cannot aptly be applied in cases like the slit experiment. Just what it shows
beyond this is complicated by the fact that Newton had in fact devised
experiments of his own whose results seem compatible with a particle
model but not a wave model. And for this reason, classical optics was beset
with a puzzle, as some experimental results suggested one model and oth-
ers another, incompatible model. Without a model that made sense of all
the experiments, no one was really in a good position to claim decisively
that light is either a wave or a particle phenomenon. There were two mod-
els, each of which could aptly be applied to predict results in certain types
of situations but not in others. Moreover, each model produced intuitive
anticipations of what should happen in each context—indeed, what must
happen if light behaves as the model characterizes it. Someone presented
for the first time with experimental results contrary to the intuitive antici-
pations of the model he was using might well be astonished by them (as
many of us are the first time we encounter Young’s experiment) and feel a
need to replicate it for himself. Indeed, even having known of the two-slit
experiment for a long time, I still sometimes find the results counterintui-
tive, in spite of knowing that they are well confirmed.
