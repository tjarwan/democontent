It would be simply naive to assume that contemporary cognitive scien-
tists who favor a cognitive architecture involving language-like intentional
representations do so merely because explaining people’s actions in terms
of their beliefs and desires succeeds so frequently in ordinary life. Cogni-
tive science is a highly competitive field, in which researchers are exploring
alternative cognitive architectures, procedures, and implementations. In
fact, it is common to distinguish “cognitive science” from “artificial intel-
ligence” on the basis of whether the research is aimed at an empirically
plausible account of human cognition (cognitive science) or the produc-
tion of intelligent machine behavior through whatever means (artificial
intelligence). Theoretical cognitive science is engaged with experimental
evidence from cognitive psychology and holds itself accountable to explain-
ing such evidence. And researchers in competing labs can be avid in their
attempts to show that their approach can accommodate the full range of
data in ways that their competitors’ cannot. Of course, it is part of any
scientific research project that researchers will doggedly try to find ways
to make their own approach work in the face of apparent obstacles. But
when researchers advocate a cognitive architecture that is committed to
language-like propositional representations, they do so generally because
that approach has demonstrated some success at producing explanations
of a number of psychological phenomena that have been demonstrated
in a laboratory, and in a fashion that warrants some hope of a realistic
interpretation.

This, however, cuts both ways. Theoretical cognitive science is also
home to a number of theories of cognitive architecture in which language-
like propositional representations play less of a role or none at all; and a
number of aspects of cognition are arguably better explained by alternative
cognitive architectures, such as connectionist architectures, Bayesian nets,
or models, a form of which I am recommending in this book.
