The Plausibility of Cognitive Pluralism
10 The Plausibility of Cognitive Pluralism
The most distinctive claim of cognitive pluralism is that we understand the
world through many mental models of different content domains. The claim
that cognition involves the use of a number of special-purpose systems that
detect, track, and allow interaction with different parts and aspects of the
world, taken by itself, may not be quite a consensus view; but many previ-
ous authors have claimed that the minds of nonhuman animals are com-
posed largely of a grab bag of useful cognitive tricks, and argued that this is
a good strategy—and perhaps the only biologically plausible strategy—for
evolving intelligent animals. What is more controversial is the idea that
a further proliferation of special-purpose ways of understanding various
aspects of the world is still a good strategy once we have animals like human
beings, who possess capacities for language and explicit reasoning. Really
there are two separate sorts of issues here. The first arises from the pecu-
liar disconnect we often see between discussions of human minds and the
minds of nonhuman animals. Often, even people who think that animal
minds are composed largely of a bundle of closed instincts and good tricks
and acknowledge that human beings are products of evolution nevertheless
talk about human minds as though we had been designed from scratch to
be general-purpose reasoners. This strikes me as psychologically naive and
scientifically unrealistic. Second, even if we acknowledge that our capaci-
ties for reasoning, language, and language-like thinking are things added to
an older suite of more specialized abilities, there are quite reasonable ques-
tions about whether the newer and more sophisticated forms of thinking
and reasoning also take the form of many separate areas of understanding
about distinct domains, and whether this would still be a good design strat-
egy for minds that are no longer restricted to developmentally canalized
capacities that are products of natural selection.
