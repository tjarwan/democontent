Coherentism
Coherentists locate the warrant for a belief in its being part of a maximally
large set of beliefs that are mutually coherent. “Coherence,” however, is a
rather vague notion. In a minimal construal, coherence requires consistency:
a coherent set of beliefs must be a set of beliefs that are mutually consistent.
But the word ‘coherence’ also seems to suggest something more than this,
ranging from relationships of mutual support to mutual entailment.
Cognitive pluralism helps to explain the mutually supportive relation-
ships between beliefs associated with a particular model but creates prob-
lems for global consistency. The system of meanings, beliefs, rules, and
implicational connections involved in a mental model is generally not only
mutually consistent but mutually supporting and in some cases mutually
implicating. Within a model, there should be consistency and fairly strong
forms of coherence. But the same need not apply between models. Because
each model is idealized in a way that is fitted to particular domains, prob-
lems, and interests, two models can be mutually contradictory both in their
assumptions and in what they imply when applied to particular cases.
As a result, we should distinguish between two kinds of coherentist
claims. A claim that requires coherence within a model is quite consonant
with cognitive pluralism. A model that is internally inconsistent or gen-
erates inconsistencies when applied to various particular cases is clearly
problematic. But if models are idealized, and two models are idealized in
different ways, they can yield implications that are inconsistent. Thus a
more global coherentist thesis—one that requires consistency between all
beliefs for any to be warranted or treats inconsistency of a belief with
any other belief as incompatible with its being warranted—is problematic.
Indeed, if the idea, explored in chapter 12, that some such inconsisten-
cies might prove to be principled and abiding is correct, then cognitive
pluralism may prove to be incompatible with global consistency. (Of
course, there is also a more general concern that, as we all have some
number of inconsistent beliefs, a globally coherentist epistemology would
imply the strongly skeptical conclusion that none of us knows anything
at all.)
