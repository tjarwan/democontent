14.4 Projective Illusion
I conclude this chapter by discussing a phenomenon that some philoso-
phers have regarded as a type of cognitive illusion, but whose status is
more complicated. I introduced the notion of cognitive illusion with refer-
ence to Kant, but in fact Hume had already spoken of the mind producing
“illusions”—a fact of which Kant was no doubt aware, as at least one of the
things Hume described as “illusion” (causation as a real relation outside the
mind) had played an important role in waking Kant from his “dogmatic
slumbers.”
In the Treatise, Hume expresses skepticism about several assumptions
shared by common sense and previous philosophies. The best known of
these is his skepticism about causation as a real relation existing indepen-
dently of the mind. According to Hume’s theory of mind, our only source
of knowledge about the world is through sensory impressions, and there
is no impression of causation. Rather, what goes on in the mind when we
think of an event as involving causation is that we have one impression,
followed by another, which the mind associates in a particular way—a kind
of associative link to which we give the name “causation”—which disposes
us to form expectations of the second type of impression on subsequent
occasions when we experience the first. This general view of the psychology
of causation attribution is preserved in Hume’s later Enquiry, albeit in more
guarded terms. In the Treatise, however, he actually goes so far as to describe
