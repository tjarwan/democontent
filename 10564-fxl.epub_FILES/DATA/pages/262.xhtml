14.3 Illusions of Unification
One of the many fine things about Kant’s epistemology is his attention
to the fact that the mind does not rest content with “knowledge,” in the
sense of knowing individual things, but has a natural drive to unify what it
knows in an ever-more-comprehensive way. Kant regards this drive as both
natural (in the sense of being a part of our psychology) and a good thing,
but he devotes a substantial portion of the first Critique (the section titled
“Transcendental Dialectic”) to showing that this drive can also lead the
mind into certain cognitive illusions. We might sum up Kant’s overall story
in the following points, which I have formatted so that some of his more
particular hypotheses are represented in the second level of the outline.
1. The mind has a drive to unite the things it knows.
2. The mind has particular types of techniques for uniting knowledge.
a. These techniques are the three syllogistic forms.
3. The mind pursues its drive to unite knowledge by trying to apply its
techniques for uniting knowledge to their ideal limit (a regulative
principle).
a. This takes place through the attempt to ground conditioned premises
through construction of prosyllogisms.
4. The mind projects what this ideal limit would look like and what would
have to be the case if this limit were attained.
a. There are three of these, the Ideas of Pure Reason: God, soul, and
world.
5. The mind can mistake these projected ideal completions for things that
it knows in the form of things that must be true unconditionally (dialec-
tical illusion).
a. The Ideas of Pure Reason cannot be known because the sphere
of knowledge is limited to things that could be objects of sensory
experience.
I endorse the general points in the first level of the outline, though I do
not agree in detail with the more specific Kantian views in the second level.
Kant’s general insights here are brilliant. But I regard his attempt to char-

Kant’s technical sense) solely in terms of the forms of judgment and argu-

account, and perhaps something that he himself would have developed dif-
