Models of chess and restaurant etiquette differ from scientific models
in an important way: one has the “right” model of chess or dining sim-
ply by dint of having one that corresponds with the models that others
are using. There is something of the same in understanding a scientific
theory—say, having a correct understanding of classical mechanics—but a
further question arises as to whether that model is an adequate model of
the phenomena it is applied to, and this latter question is at issue in sci-
entific confirmation. But scientific models are by no means unique in this
respect. The models I use to navigate my house or the town I live in have
to track features of those environments if they are to enable me to navigate
successfully, even though their assumptions and commitments are seldom
explicitly expressed. The statistical heuristics we employ in everyday life
are apt (or not) for particular contexts, and we can be in better or worse
positions to assess their aptness. (Hence the tendency of researchers deal-
ing with heuristics and biases to describe inapt strategies as “irrational,”
particularly when the person who employs them is in a position to know
they are inapt.) Our models of social categories such as race and gender are
often woefully inadequate to representing their targets and are produced
and reinforced through mechanisms that are notoriously unreliable for
tracking the facts. The processes of consciousness-raising that can disabuse
us of such social biases are not only morally edifying but also put us in a
better epistemic position with respect to our social understanding.
We probably need far more discussion of the epistemic evaluation of
models than I am in a position to give here, and different types of models are
likely to require different types of evaluation. Epistemic evaluation seems
appropriate with respect to ways a model is produced, how (or if) it can be
scrutinized and adjusted, and how we can learn to distinguish the cases in
which it is apt from those in which it is not and use it only in contexts in
which it is apt. Developmentally canalized models like the core systems
are produced primarily through evolution and development, but these can
perhaps be regarded as prior mechanisms that can be evaluated for their
