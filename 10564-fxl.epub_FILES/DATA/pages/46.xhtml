In short, neural localists like Gall had it right about input systems, but
neural globalists like Flourens had it right about thinking. The mind—at
least the human mind—has modular elements, primarily at the sensory
periphery. But all the things we are accustomed to regard as “think-
ing” are nonmodular. And given Fodor’s roots in the computationalist
tradition, the natural term for such processes is “central.” A computer
may use peripheral units like a keyboard, scanner, mouse, and printer,
and even a separate graphics card, each of which contains its own spe-
cial hardware circuitry; but most of the computation is done by a single
“central processing unit” using programs and data stored in a common
memory space.
The book ends on a note of epistemological pessimism. Fodor points
out that whereas the cognitive sciences have been producing impressive
explanations of processes at the sensory and motor peripheries of the
brain, we understand almost nothing about how thinking takes place.
We do not know of neural localizations for various forms of thought
like concepts or judgments the way we do for, say, detection of color
and form. And computational models of thinking founder on the frame
problem—the problem of setting algorithmic (or even heuristic) bound-
aries around what a program should treat as the relevant information in
performing and monitoring a task. This, Fodor points out, has an eerie
resonance with isotropy and Quineanism, and he suggests (plausibly) that
isotropic and Quinean processes may not be susceptible to modeling by
traditional computational approaches in artificial intelligence, and (a bit
more speculatively) there may be no other way to gain epistemic traction
on thinking, either.
