As with other types of models, scientific theories can be thought of as
models of real-world processes at two levels. First, the theory itself—say, the
theory of special relativity—can be thought of as a model of very general
invariants found in nature, and of space-time as a whole. But it can also
be used as a framework for modeling more specific problems, such as how
several bodies will behave with respect to one another.
But there are also several ways in which scientific models differ from
some of the other types of models we have explored. Contrary to what
some previous generations of philosophers supposed, scientific under-
standing is not simply a result of inductive associations from naive percep-
tion. Scientific concepts and principles often differ significantly from those
of common sense and have to be constructed through difficult processes
of experiment design and theory building. While the resulting models are
ultimately the outcome of a long process of bootstrapping up from com-
monplace modes of conceiving and interacting with the world, the boot-
strapping process requires the development of some highly specialized and
regimented forms of thinking, including new forms of mathematics and
regimens for experiment and confirmation. Many scientific models thus
employ rarefied mathematical forms of representation that confer a form
of exactitude not found in most other models, and scientific reasoning and
experimentation are regimented in ways designed to minimize error far
beyond those employed in everyday life.
We also frequently speak of science being “objective.” This is a tricky
word, one that upon examination has many different meanings. But one
thing that it can mean, which really does seem to apply to scientific under-
standing, involves the minimization of individual and collective idiosyn-
cracies and biases. If we assume that cognition arose first and foremost as a
way of allowing organisms to interact more adaptively with their environ-
ments, it is natural to conclude that the baseline “default settings” for any
mind are optimized for things that are biologically salient to the organ-
ism: paradigmatically, things relevant to survival, nutrition, maturation,
and reproduction. From a biological standpoint, it is actually good that an
organism’s mind is attuned to things like what is dangerous, what is edible,
and, in the case of social organisms, who is aggressive or cooperative. But
