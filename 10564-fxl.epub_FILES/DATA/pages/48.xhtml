In subsequent chapters, I make a case that a subset of these features can be

The other main objection to Fodorian modularity has come from advo-
cates of the massive modularity thesis (e.g., Carruthers 2006a, 2006b; Tooby
and Cosmides 2005). Fodor claims that while the mind has modules, their
number is comparatively small, largely limited to input and output proces-
sors and a language parser. Advocates of massive modularity argue that the
number of mental modules is actually much larger—perhaps in the hun-
dreds or thousands—and, perhaps more importantly, they are not limited
to “peripheral” processes of perception and motor control.
The massive modularity thesis is generally developed in tandem with
views from evolutionary psychology. We seem to have little problem think-
ing of animal minds as including a grab bag of useful instincts that are prod-
ucts of millions of years of natural selection—that is, of seeing the cognitive
abilities of nonhuman animals as distinctly biological traits. Indeed, phi-
losophers have often doubted that any nonhuman animals possess reason-
ing and other features of centralized cognition. But when we think about
human minds, we tend to have the opposite bias. In spite of believing that
we too are products of natural selection, and indeed that the genetic dis-
tance between us and other primates is slight, we seem to have a hard time
believing that we too possess many psychological traits that are biological
adaptations, and try to attribute as much as possible about our minds to
reasoning.
From a biological standpoint, these biases seem misguided. It is true that
the tiny genetic differences between humans and chimpanzees support
enormous differences in intellectual capacity: the capacity for language,
a greatly expanded ability to create and use tools, more complex social
cognition, and most of what philosophers have lumped under the head-
ings of “thought” and “reason.” And we are right to be interested in these
things, as they are important components of who we are, both as individu-
als and as a species. But biological mutation does not operate by wiping
clean the slate of millions of years’ accumulation of adaptive traits and
designing a new type of mind from scratch. New traits may be built on the
old and transform them in many ways; but many of the old traits are sure
