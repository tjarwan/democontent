8.4 Triangulation
The thesis that we understand the world through using many domain-
specific models that have proprietary rules, representational systems, and
internal ontologies may explain important things about understanding, but
it also presents problems. We may use many individual models to under-
stand different things about the world, but we do not only use models one
at a time and in isolation from one another. In bodily interaction with the
world, we employ a number of distinct perceptual, motor, and cognitive
systems, not merely simultaneously but cooperatively and in an integrated
manner. Even in off-line thinking, such as imagination, reasoning, and
theorizing, we can think about an object, phenomenon, event, or general
type of problem from a variety of angles, framed in terms of different mod-
els. And this raises questions about (1) how we are able to coordinate them
adaptively at all; (2) how we are able to recognize one and the same object,
phenomenon, and so on, though we are thinking about it through multiple
models; and (3) how we are able to combine insights from different models
through reasoning, so that the resulting understanding we have as a whole
is more than simply the sum of what we would get from several distinct
model-based processes going on simultaneously.
If our only way of thinking about the world were through individual
models, it would seem that at least the second and third questions might
prove impossible, and even the first would require some explanation. If
we assume that models (or at least those that constitute objects at all) each
have their own implicit internal ontologies, positing special types of things,
properties, relations, processes, and transformations, there is a danger that
they would end up being separate gears spinning on their own axes without
engaging one another. Suppose I am playing chess. I recognize a piece as
a knight within the chess model. I also recognize it as a particular kind of
physical objectâ€”a small wooden piece I can pick up, a large concrete statue
that would need to be moved by heavy equipment, or perhaps a human
being in costume if it is a game of human chess. But if model-based cog-
nition were the whole story, I would not recognize a single thing as being
both a knight and a concrete statue. Rather, I would have two separate
representations: one of a chess knight, and another of a concrete statue.
