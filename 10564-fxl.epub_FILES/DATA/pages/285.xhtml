Reliabilism
While reliabilism is a relatively new addition to the list of philosophical
accounts of epistemic warrant, I will address it first, because the character-
ization I have given of the warrant conferred by apt models seems in fact
to be a reliabilist view. The reliabilist holds that a belief is warranted if and
only if it is produced by a reliable mechanism for producing true beliefs in
that type of context. Alvin Plantinga, for example, writes that a belief is
warranted if and only if it meets the following conditions:
(1) It has been produced in me by cognitive faculties that are working properly … in
a cognitive environment that is appropriate for my kinds of cognitive faculties, (2)
the segment of design plan governing the production of that belief is aimed at the
production of true beliefs, and (3) there is a high statistical probability that a belief
produced under those conditions will be true. (Plantinga 1993, 46)
Saying that a mechanism for belief production is “reliable” in producing
true beliefs does not really tell us much about the mechanism, even if the
reliability of the mechanism is cashed out in terms of probability. This is
not necessarily a bad characteristic for the reliabilist theory to have, as there
may be many reliable mechanisms that are reliable in different ways and
for different reasons, and hence a highly flexible account of warrant may
actually be required.
One of the core applications of reliabilist accounts has been to the pro-
duction of beliefs through perception, where the causes of the belief are
not themselves beliefs. It seems clear enough that perceptual processes are
in some broad sense “mechanisms,” and the details of how they work and
why they are reliable can be left to the cognitive scientists. But to extend the
theory further, we would need some way of clarifying what sort of “faculty”
or “mechanism” might be involved in nonperceptual belief formation.
