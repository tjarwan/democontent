I began with examples of models of mathematical systems because they
most clearly illustrate ways that the features of models can provide the
basis for disunities of belief and understanding. But there are also impor-
tant issues that we cannot illuminate using mathematical examples. Pure
mathematics is special in that it is not accountable to anything outside the
mathematical system. There are, of course, important questions about what
physical systems each geometry can provide apt representations of, but a
discovery that space is non-Euclidean does not amount to a falsification
of Euclidean geometry. It is simply the system it is. And the fact that two
geometries produce seemingly contradictory pronouncements on things
like the parallel postulate and the Pythagorean theorem does not result in
any deep paradoxes. Mathematically speaking, the parallel postulate and
the Pythagorean theorem are not simply true-full-stop or false-full-stop;
they are true-in-Euclidean-geometry and false-in-spherical-geometry. There
is no further question about their truth, except for corresponding ques-
tions about whether they are true in additional types of geometric systems.
Someone who mixes together incompatible commitments of two systems
in an inference is simply making a blunder.
A different set of issues arises when we apply multiple models to real-
world situations. It is here that issues about the ways the models are ideal-
ized and whether they are aptly applied arise. What makes a model good
for its purpose is that it tracks patterns in the world sufficiently well for that
purpose, and in a fashion that allows us to think, reason, and plan about
situations in the world, and it does so by having a representational system
and a set of implicit rules that do a good-enough job of tracking such pat-
terns. The fact that any given model represents only some things, and in
elegant and comprehensible ways, is what allows it to provide insight into
particular real patterns. Two different models may track different patterns
and may need to represent them in different ways to be elegant and com-
prehensible. But the very features that make each model apt for its given
purpose may present problems when we try to use two models together
to understand a real-world situation to which they both apply. This is a
general and schematic story about a source of disunities of belief and under-
standing. In the ensuing sections, I explore its application to a number of
cases.
