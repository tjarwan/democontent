We now need to explore the epistemological implications of the relation-
ship between models and judgments. The basic idea that I will develop here
is a kind of reliabilist thesis: that models are reliable mechanisms for the
production of knowledge in the contexts in which they are aptly applied.
If this is the case, then at least part of what warrants many judgments is
something about the models used to frame them, in which case mental
models are of fundamental importance to epistemology. I will also explore
(in the next section) the further question of whether models themselves are
subject to epistemological evaluation in a fashion that goes beyond their
aptness and reliability—for example, whether we should speak of models,
as well as judgments and beliefs, as being warranted.
We should distinguish two types of judgments and their different rela-
tionships to models. One type of judgment makes explicit one or more
features or commitments of a model. “A body at rest will remain at rest
unless acted on by an external force” and “bodies exert an attractive force
described by the inverse square law” express basic commitments of New-
tonian mechanics. “Bishops may move diagonally” expresses a basic com-
mitment of a model of chess. Insofar as one is using the model, one is
normatively committed to such judgments. And to the extent that the
model is apt, the judgments are true. In many cases, such judgments also
seem intuitively obvious and even necessary to someone conversant with
the model. (Indeed, they are “necessary” in the sense of being constitutive
commitments of the model, much as the parallel postulate is a constitu-
tive commitment of Euclidean geometry and thus necessarily true-within-
Euclidean-geometry.) At least when one is using the model naively—that is,
not attending to the fact that it is idealized and might not be aptly applied
in all cases—such judgments seem self-evident. Indeed, I suspect that the
vast majority of judgments whose truth seems self-evident do so because
they express principles of deeply entrenched models that we can access
through intuitive processes.
