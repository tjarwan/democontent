A mental model, in turn, is something in the mind with a structure that
“parallels” that of the external system we are thinking about:
My hypothesis then is that thought models, or parallels, reality—that its essential
feature is not “the mind,” “the self,” “sense-data,” nor propositions but symbolism,
and this symbolism is largely of the same kind as that which is familiar to us in me-
chanical devices which aid thought and calculation. (Craik 1943, 57)
Just what this nonpropositional symbolism might amount to is unclear, and
we should probably resist the temptation to read later associations that the
word ‘symbol’ has from within later computational approaches to thought.
Johnson-Laird has suggested possible connections to Wittgenstein’s picto-
rial or isomorphism view of thought and Peirce’s iconic symbols, which
represent by dint of structural similarity. Craik’s interest, however, seems
to have been not with semantics (more specifically, with what makes some-
thing represent something else) but with adaptive cognition: if structural
similarities between a model and the system modeled are important, it is
because this allows the model to function as a mental surrogate for the
target system in off-line cognition through simulation or nonpropositional
reasoning. Craik’s suggestion that mental models include representations
not only of features of the world but also of our own actions is also highly
suggestive of the idea that models of this sort might bridge the gap between
theoretical and practical reasoning and provide a basis for an account of
understanding.
Craik never worked out this theory in greater detail, as he died in a bicy-
cling accident on VE Day in 1945, two years after the publication of The
Nature of Explanation. His general idea of a mental model, which seems
altogether consonant with the views I have developed, received little atten-
tion at the time, as he was writing in the heyday of psychological behavior-
ism. The language of “mental models” lay dormant between Craik’s death
and the reintroduction of the terminology in books published in 1983 by
Gentner and Stevens and by Johnson-Laird. Contemporary advocates of
model-based views commonly cite both Gentner and Stevens and Johnson-
Laird as pioneers of model-based approaches, but really they developed
very different ideas of what sort of thing a mental model might be.
Although Gentner and Stevens’s (1983) anthology was titled Mental
Models, the word ‘model’ appears in Gentner and Gentner’s own article in
that volume, “Flowing Water or Teeming Crowds: Mental Models of Elec-
tricity,” only in the title and the first sentence, which mentions “analogical
models” (Gentner and Gentner 1983, 99). The preferred terminology in
