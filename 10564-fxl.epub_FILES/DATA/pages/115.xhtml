Whatever the principles and mechanisms are by which you do this, they

the learned manual skill of attaching such a group using the external model.
A cognitive scientist studying the ability you have gained might propose
theories about how you do it at various levels of abstraction: for example,
in terms of rules that describe how pieces may be combined, or in terms of a
neural architecture that could implement those rules. Some theories might
hypothesize, for instance, that you are, at some unconscious or infracon-
scious level, following rules that must themselves be represented. I suspect
that this hypothesis is false; but even if it is true, it is certainly not appar-
ent to you from your experience of thinking about molecules. (Unless, of
course, you are doing something more than the kind of modeling I have
described, such as using a list of verbal rules like “carbon atoms can form
four bonds” as a kind of mnemonic aid to supplement a modeling process
that has not been fully internalized.) For purposes of developing the notion
of a mental model intuitively, let us set aside for the moment questions
about how it is achieved, and think of it as a real and interesting phenom-
enon for which there might be alternative explanations.
7.1 Two Observations
Now that we have reached this stage in an intuitive development of the

things about the kind of situation I have described.
