Of course, as I have described the examples, the identification of the
Agent and the Object as “the same thing” is supplied from the perspective
of the adult observer, and in the examples described, the stimulus is per-
ceived first through one core system and then through the other. It is less
clear whether (a) a single stimulus can simultaneously trigger both systems,
and (b) if it can do so, whether the child can bind the two representa-
tions together as representations of the selfsame object and regard one and
the same thing as being both Agent and Object. Bloom seems to think
not and claims that we naturally think of human beings as consisting of
two things—a body (an Object) and a soul (an Agent). I am skeptical of
Bloom’s conclusion. Adults, at least, seem to be quite capable of triangulat-
ing a single object through multiple cognitive lenses, even if the two ways
of regarding it are logically incompatible. Of course, it is possible that we
do this through some mechanism other than the core systems. And even if
adults can triangulate through the core systems, it is also possible that the
ability is acquired only long after the core systems are in place. While the
specific question about how the core systems can be combined is an empiri-
cal question requiring more research, there is nonetheless an important
point here about some forms of cognition: that we understand the selfsame
object through more than one way of thinking about it, and incompatibili-
ties between the two ways of thinking about it do not seem to present an
obstacle to the psychological possibility of doing so.
4.2 Folk Theories
A second and slightly older hypothesis in cognitive and developmental psy-
chology is the claim that ordinary human understanding involves implicit
theories about the natural, biological, mental, and social world, acquired
over the course of early to middle childhood and persisting through adult-
hood. These implicit theories are commonly referred to by names such as
“folk physics,” “folk biology,” and “folk psychology.” They consist of what-
ever species-typical assumptions there may be about matters such as how
objects move when thrown or dropped, what features we can expect of
any animal species (e.g., that individual animals of a species are offspring
of parents of the same species, have a species-typical physiology and diet,
etc.), and how we interpret people’s actions as consequences of their beliefs
and desires.
