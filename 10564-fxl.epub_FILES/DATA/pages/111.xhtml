There is, as well, a potential problem here, as the kinds of models we
have examined have features that would be extremely problematic if we
supposed that they must be found in mental models as well. First, using a
scale model, blueprint, or map as a model requires us to make it an object
of attention. But if we have understanding in everyday life through mental
models of things like restaurant scenarios or core geometry, these “mod-
els” are not characteristically objects of our attention. They are not things
we can perceive with our senses at all. We can, perhaps, think about them
as theorists. But whatever the mechanisms through which we understand
the world, we normally do not think about those mechanisms. It might be
more appropriate to say that we think through them, in a sense analogous to
how we see through a lens. And our ability to think through them does not
depend on our having previously attended to them. Indeed, it is the other
way around: it is only by reflecting on their use that we can attend to them
as models at all.
Second, a model is a model of something. And with our examples of
external models, using them as models requires us to take them as models
of something. A child can play with the elements of a kit for modeling
molecules; but for the child, this is not an exercise in modeling molecules
but something more like playing with a Tinkertoy set. To use it as a model
of molecules, one must also have an understanding of the target domain
and understand that the thing one is interacting with is to be used as a
model of that domain. But if this feature is taken to be a necessary feature
of anything counting as a model, we cannot explain our understanding of
the world just in terms of mental models. To use such a model—to use it as
a model—we would need some independent form of understanding of the
domain to be modeled. We could have mental models in the sense of, as it
were, saying to oneself, “I shall use this as a model of X,” in cases where
we already had some other kind of understanding of X. But if understand-
ing is supposed to be “models all the way down,” then there is no way for
this to get off the ground. In Symbols, Computation, and Intentionality (Horst
1996), I raised a similar concern about attempts to use the notion of “men-
tal representation” to do any work in explaining the semantics of human
thought. And so I, of all people, ought to be careful here not to make the
same mistake that I accused others of making. So if we are to use the word
‘model’ here as a term for general features of the mind that are at work
broadly in cognition, we need to explicate how the word is being used in a
recognizable way that avoids this potential problem of incoherence.
