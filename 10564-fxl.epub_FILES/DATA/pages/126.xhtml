The typology of objects within one context might likewise be orthogo-
nal to typologies from other contexts. The judge’s robe might be a piece
of clothing also sold as an academic gown or a preacher’s robe. The same
production model of fork might be used as a salad fork for one course and a
dessert fork for another, and my dessert fork might be made by Mikasa and
my dining partner’s by Lenox. In a pinch, I can use various chess pieces to
play checkers; and if I do so, their different shapes and the ways they could
legally be moved as chess pieces are irrelevant to their typing as checkers
pieces. The same can be said for activities performed. A quarterback and a
wide receiver might go through the same motions during a game that they
go through just tossing the ball around at a barbeque, but only in the game
do those motions count as a forward pass.
We understand many social contexts with special roles, local ontologies,
and activities. Each requires a separate mental model, though some models
may be related to, and variants on or special cases of, others. There are dif-
ferent types of dining contexts and different types of restaurants; and the
experienced diner knows, say, that at this restaurant there is a special person
called the sommelier who attends to the wine, and the check is not brought
until requested. Social contexts vary greatly from culture to culture, and a
great deal of cultural literacy involves understanding the particular social
contexts of that culture. Moreover, cultures can vary greatly in how strictly
ritualized their practices are, and in how much of their daily life is defined
by roles and contexts. In medieval Europe or Confucian China, it would
seem that a very great deal of how a person was expected to behave—and
probably much of how people really did behave—was defined by roles of
family relationship, guild, and social hierarchy.
Sometimes a person’s roles can conflict and present contrary expecta-
tions. In the play Antigone, the title character is caught between the con-
flicting expectations of her role as a pious sister (who should see that her
brothers’ bodies are properly buried) and as a citizen (who should obey the
Archon’s decree that they should be left where they fell in their rebellion
against the state), and this kind of situation forms the basis for much classi-
cal tragedy. To understand it as tragedy, one must understand the conflict-
ing roles, which can make a story from one culture difficult for someone
from another culture with different roles to understand. Sometimes such
conflicts are themselves the material for further rules: a judge should recuse
herself from a case in which she is an interested party, such as a trial involv-
ing a member of her family.
