11.1 Cognitive Complementarity
Before turning to the distinctive features of human cognition, let us con-
sider briefly some of the components of cognition and intelligence that we
share with many other species. Evolved special-purpose mechanisms and
the ability to learn through conditioning provide an ecumenical baseline
for most animal cognition, and both can be found in some form even in
animals as simple as flatworms. There are other strategies that are far from
ubiquitous but are found in different forms in a number of species: incorpo-
ration of the environment into the extended phenotype (insect hives, spi-
derwebs, beaver dams), social distribution of cognitive function (whether
in the different operations performed by different castes of ants, which pre-
sumably involve differential expression of a common genotype, or in social
animals like vervet monkeys or prairie dogs taking turns as sentries), and
the possession of redundant neural systems (such as our multiple systems
for extracting depth cues) that not only increase resiliency but also extract
information relevant to the same ends differently and under different con-
ditions. Other advances have appeared more recently in our evolutionary
lineage, such the increased curiosity and capacity for exploratory play char-
acteristic of mammals, and tool use, which is found in rudimentary form in
a number of species but is enormously expanded in human beings.

vicinity for cognitive ethology and evolutionary psychology, such as which
species have particular capacities, when they might have appeared in evo-
lutionary history, what adaptive advantages each was selected for, and how
some might have been precursors or enabling conditions for others. What
