Once we have characterized language and language-like thought in this
fashion, two implications that might otherwise be surprising seem clearly
to emerge. The first is that much and perhaps all of what we think of as
“semantics” does not really arise out of language at all. Many (perhaps all)
truly semantic connections arise out of models. Lexical units can express
and encode such semantic properties, but without models, language would
be largely devoid of semantic content. And this leads to the second impli-
cation: without such a relationship with mental models, language and
language-like thinking would be of little use and would confer little or no
adaptive advantage. They would be something analogous to purely formal
languages, in which the symbols can be rearranged syntactically but have
no further connection to one another or to the world. And if this is true,
it is no accident that a capacity for language arose only once model-based
understanding was firmly in place. Language contributes a great deal as an
addition to model-based cognition but confers little or no adaptive advan-
tage without it.
11.3 Two Objections
Before passing on to further discussion of the relation between language,
language-like thinking, and models, let us consider two possible objections.
The first comes from the computationalists, who would likely concede that
natural language is a recent and uniquely human trait, yet also claim that
thinking in general must take place in some sort of language of thought,
even if it must be in some ways very different from public language. The
second is implicit in discussions of causal semantics: that what we need for
a semantically pregnant language (whether public or private) is not a sys-
tem of mental models but merely reference-fixing relations for lexical units
in a language-like representational medium.
