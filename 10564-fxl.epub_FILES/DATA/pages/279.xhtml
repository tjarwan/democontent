While judgments of this first type are often prominent in philosophy
and the sciences, the vast majority of judgments we make are not of this
type; they are judgments in which models are employed in how we frame
particular states of affairs: “this body is at rest,” “this body will take five
seconds to reach the ground after it is dropped,” “the white bishop is in a
position to capture the black queen.” Such judgments are neither derivable
from nor warranted by models alone, as they also depend on facts in the
world. Nevertheless there are important relationships between the framing
models and the truth and warrant of the judgments. A judgment can go
wrong in one way if it is framed in terms of an inapt model. In the context
of cosmology, it makes no sense to speak of absolute rest. The Archbishop
of Canterbury and the Queen of England may be standing in a diagonal
relation on a checkered dais, and someone glancing at the scene might take
it to be part of a game of human chess; but the observer would be mistaken,
and the bishop is not poised to capture the queen (because he is not a chess
bishop or she a chess queen at all, and the notion of “capture” employed in
chess does not apply to them). If I use an inapt model to frame a situation,
any warrant that its inferential rules might provide for a judgment based
on them is nullified.
We can also go wrong even when we use apt models, if we do not use
their resources to properly represent the particulars of the situation. In traf-
fic court, it does make sense to speak of “being at rest” or “standing still”
(with the surface of the Earth or the roads treated as a fixed frame of refer-
ence), but people routinely seem to think they have stopped at the inter-
section at the base of my street when in fact they have never slowed down
below five miles per hour. The chess player may not see the board properly
or not realize that moving his bishop would expose his king to checkmate.
In such cases, the model employed did provide ways of representing things
in a fashion we would count as true, but the judgment that was actually
made represented things in another way, which we count as false. Again,
in such cases, any warrant that might have been conferred by the model is
nullified. If the board had been as I thought it was, my judgment that I was
in a position to capture the opposing queen by moving my bishop would
have been warranted, but the antecedent of the conditional is in fact false.
