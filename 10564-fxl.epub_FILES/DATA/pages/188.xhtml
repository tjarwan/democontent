Active curiosity and exploratory play, which seem to be characteristic of
mammals and are also found in at least some birds, are also highly comple-
mentary to the capacity to form and refine models. Indeed, being curious
in ways that motivate systematic exploration of novel objects and environ-
ments seems to be so well paired with the capacity to form models of new
content domains that it is tempting to suspect that there might have been
a close historical relationship between them: that one was an enabling con-
dition for the other, or they coevolved, or if they appeared independently,
each made the other far more valuable than it would have been in isola-
tion. Exploratory play likewise seems to be well paired with the formation
of models, as it allows models of interaction with physical and social envi-
ronments to be acquired and refined in a fashion insulated from selection
pressures.
Identifying complementary relationships between cognitive systems or
cognitive traits in present-day species can help to motivate further questions
about their evolutionary history and how they interact in development.
Two systems may have appeared independently, and their complementar-
ity might be a happy coincidence. One system might be a necessary precur-
sor for the other. Or they might each have appeared initially in different
and more limited forms, and their interaction might have provided the
conditions for each to be expanded and shaped in ways that exploit their
combined benefit. While I am enthusiastic about the project of exploring
such questions, I will not pursue them in any depth here. My principal con-
cern here is with the relationship between models, language, and language-
like thought.
In the case of the relationship between model-based cognition and
natural language, it seems clear that model-based cognition and even the
modeling engine appeared long before the capacity for natural language.
