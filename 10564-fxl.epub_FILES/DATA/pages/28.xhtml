Two Levels of Intentional Explanation
Advocates of language-like mental representations often appeal to “the suc-
cess of intentional explanation” to justify their position. But it is impor-
tant to distinguish two very different types of explanation here. The first
kind is the sort of explanation of a person’s words or deeds in terms of her
beliefs, desires, and other intentional states that we find ubiquitously in
ordinary life, like the explanation, earlier in the chapter, of why Jane went
to the refrigerator. Fodor and other representationalists do indeed appeal
to the success of such explanations to justify their position, claiming that
such explanations could work only if there really are such things as beliefs
and desires with propositional content that play causal roles in the pro-
duction of behavior, and that the only viable account of how this could
work involves treating them as occurrent mental representations. Dennett
agrees that this kind of explanation works well, but he denies that it should
be taken as the type of explanation that posits causal mechanisms or the
existence of occurrent states called “beliefs” and “desires.” Both of these
positions strike me as at least coherent accounts of the nature of intentional
explanation, and they involve very different commitments to features of
cognitive architecture.
Cognitive science also offers a second and deeper type of explanation,
involving the postulation of mechanisms that explain not only the result-
ing behavior but also things like the time it takes to perform a cognitive
task, what additional forms of information are sought in the accomplish-
ment of a task, characteristic patterns of error and breakdown, and so on. It
is this sort of explanation, not the commonsense explanation of behavior,
that is the real work of theoretical cognitive science. Cognitive scientists
propose accounts of the types of representations and processes that might
underlie different types of competence and performance. There might be
any number of possible cognitive architectures involving different types of
representations and processes that could, in principle, produce the same
end result; but this does not render them indistinguishable, because how
they produce it can have testable consequences. We can prove, for exam-
