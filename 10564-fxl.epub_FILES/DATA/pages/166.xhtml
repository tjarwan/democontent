Marr also introduced what has become an influential distinction
between three levels of abstraction that are needed in understanding cog-
nitive systems. The most abstract of these, which he calls the “computa-
tional” level, specifies what a system does and its function. The next level,
the “representational” or “algorithmic” level, describes how it does them
in terms of what kinds of representations it uses and the computational
processes it uses to manipulate them. The third, the “physical” level, speci-
fies how the system is physically realized: in the case of human vision, the
neural structures; in the case of computers, the hardware implementation.
This three-level system has been adopted widely by subsequent writers in
cognitive science (though see Danks 2014 for some important clarifications
of its limitations).
Discussions of cognitive architecture tend to be seen as involving Marr’s
computational or representational levels or the intersection between the
two, addressing issues of the number and types of systems and at least a
rough specification of the types of representations (e.g., models or propo-
sitional representations) they employ, independently of questions about
their neural or physical implementation; and at least to the extent that
one accepts Marr’s classification of levels, my account is no exception. My
account is committed to the existence of model-sized units that do a certain
amount of their processing autonomously, and hence to at least some con-
straints on the kinds of representations and processes involved, and also to
a separate system for language-like thinking, which requires different sorts
of representations. I have not, however, proposed more specific features of
the representations or representational systems or the algorithms that oper-
ate over them. This is in part because I wish to present a model-based cog-
nitive pluralism as a plausible and broad thesis, which is compatible with
various more specific hypotheses about the kinds of information-process-
ing structures that might underlie the use of mental models. But it is also
