assumption that every physical object has a determinate location at any
one time. This assumption is encoded into classical physics, as well as the
core object system, folk physics, and likely a lot of special-purpose models
in between. And the assumption is safe unless we are faced with some of
the problems of quantum mechanics. In those cases, any representational
system that is forced to represent objects as having determinate positions
is forced into error.
Note, however, that we are not forced into these errors, even if particu-
lar models we employ are. I can measure the lines in the illusory pair and
confirm that they are of equal length. And with sufficient intelligence and
training, I can understand quantum mechanical descriptions of the behav-
ior of electrons and abstain from insisting on the assumptions of intuitive
and classical models. What gives us the ability to go beyond the limitations
of a given model is the very fact that we can have multiple models and
use them to triangulate the world more precisely. If we could only think
of physical objects through the core object system—or, for that matter,
through classical physics or quantum mechanics—we would lack some of
the cognitive flexibility that we in fact possess. So for anyone inclined to
suppose that a single-model cognitive architecture would be preferable to a
pluralistic one, ask yourself the question: which model would you choose?
And what would be lost in abandoning all the others?
I wish to conclude this line of thought by considering a particular appli-
cation of it. Kant held that the faculty of Sensibility always organizes sensa-
tion in terms of Euclidean geometry—so far, an empirical claim about the
psychology of perception. But he went on to use this in a transcendental
argument to the conclusion that space necessarily has a Euclidean geom-
etry. I think the argument is a valid one, though I think the conclusion (and
hence at least one of the premises) is false. One Kantian assumption is that
“the world” we are speaking about in science is what he calls the phenom-
enal world: the world-as-interpreted-through-the-Forms-of-Sensibility-and-
Categories-of-the-Understanding. So if the Forms and Categories force us to
represent any possible object of thought or experience in a particular way—
say, as located in a Euclidean space—then it is a necessary truth about the
phenomenal world that any phenomenal object will have that property.
And so if we are forced to think about physical objects in Euclidean terms
and cannot think of them in any alternative terms, then the phenomenal
world (of humans) is indeed necessarily Euclidean.
