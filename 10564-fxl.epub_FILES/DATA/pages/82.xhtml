But how broad a category “representation” turns out to be will then
depend on how broadly we construe “thinking” and “intentionality.”
Should we count the cognitive states of nonlinguistic animals as thinking,
and hence potentially as representational? And if so, which nonlinguistic
animals? Chimps? Cats? Lizards? Flatworms? As I have argued elsewhere,
I think there are significant issues here (Horst 1996). We conceive of “the
mental” through the lens of our own mental life, drawing on paradigm
examples that are conscious, available for scrutiny and reasoning, and
articulable in language. A kind of terminological choice is involved when
we decide either (a) to apply terms like ‘mental’ and ‘representational’ to
creatures without consciousness, reasoning, or language or (b) to restrict
those terms to cases closely resembling our own. I think we have good
reason to treat the primary uses of words like ‘mental’ and ‘intentional’ as
closely and perhaps constitutively linked with consciousness, reasoning,
and language. In the present enterprise, however, our concern is not with
consciousness, nor is it immediately with reasoning or language as special
features of human minds. Rather, it is with cognition. And we have good
reason both to use the word ‘cognition’ so that it covers processes that take
place in nonhuman animals, and to stress the continuities between human
and animal cognition.
I thus propose to use ‘representation’ in a stipulative and technical
way, in which representing-things-as-being-thus is something that is
situated in the internal economy of a cognizing system, paradigmatically
a living organism. It may be that the cognitive capacities of some species
are so simple that we cannot plausibly apply this notion to them. Like-
wise, it may be that this usage is naturally applied to certain nonbiological
homeostatic or autopoietic systems as well. But it is applied only analogously
to things like computer simulations of cognition that are not embedded
in homeostatic devices where the “representing” plays an adaptive
function in those devices maintaining their characteristic form of
being. I stress that this is a technical and stipulative usage of the word
‘representation’.
To represent things as being thus-and-so, an organism requires two
types of things. The first is a representational system; the second, a particular
representation, in the sense of a representational vehicle through which the
organism counts as representing things as being A rather than as being B.
