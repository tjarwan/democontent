Intension and Ostension
First, the distinction between intensional and ostensive components. It is
easier to find a point of entry by considering our concepts of individu-
als rather than our concepts of kinds, properties, relations, or processes.
We think of individuals as having enduring properties and properties that
change over time. Being able to think about an individual person or object
at all requires the ability to think of it as one and the same thing despite the
constant changes in variable properties: Socrates is first sitting then stand-
ing, asleep then chatting in the Agora, bearded and then newly shaven.
The boundary between “enduring” and “changing” properties is a bit hazy,
though: we may think of a person’s physiognomy and personality as rela-
tively stable things, but both in fact change substantially throughout the
life cycle and can undergo rapid and dramatic changes as well. Just what
can change without forcing us to conclude that the individual we knew
is no longer present seems somewhat open-ended, a fact reflected in rival
theories of personal identity and of the identity conditions of objects, as
well as in the problems involved in deciding when an embryo becomes a
human person.
The simplest and perhaps the only explanation for this would seem to be
that concepts of individuals (whether persons or objects) involve multiple
components. Some of these involve attribution of properties, and it may be
that there is an important psychological difference in the way we represent
properties we regard as enduring (perhaps even assumed to be part of an
essence) and those that are evanescent. But in addition to these there seems
to have to be a component that is more ostensive—one that has the func-
tion of tracking a particular individual through changes. And the “changes”
we need to track through are not only real changes in the individual but
changes in our beliefs about that individual. I see Socrates and take him to
be a Spartan but then change my mind and decide he is an Athenian. I see
something on the path and take it to be a rock until it moves and I see it
from a different angle and conclude that it is some kind of animal. I do
not simply first think, “There is a Spartan,” and then deny that and think,
