Metaphor
In chapter 8, I briefly explored Lakoff and Johnson’s (2003) claim that a
great deal of human understanding is based in metaphor. I related this to
model-based understanding by suggesting that metaphorical transposition
involves taking a model of one domain and using its structure as the scaf-
folding for a model of a different domain, with subsequent revisions to
accommodate the differences between domains. In the standard sense of
the word, metaphor is a linguistic phenomenon, and so it might seem to
follow that if Lakoff and Johnson are correct, a great deal of human under-
standing is possible only because we have the linguistic capacities that can
form a basis for metaphor. We should, however, be cautious in drawing this
conclusion, as nonlinguistic mechanisms might also be available for trans-
posing the structural features of a model to a new domain. This strikes me
as an important empirical question we should be careful not to prejudge.
But even if nonlinguistic methods can achieve this result, mechanisms
based in linguistic metaphor may be more efficient and have broader scope
and are clearly powerful tools for the transmission of model-based forms of
understanding. To take one of Lakoff and Johnson’s examples, just using a
conflict metaphor for argument can prompt the hearer to activate a conflict
model and begin to use it as a way of thinking about argument. She might
eventually have thought of this way of looking at things on her own, but
language allows it to be transferred quickly and to proliferate through a
community, enlarging the stock of shared models that can be used in rou-
tine communication.
