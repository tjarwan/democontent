Like Johnson-Laird, I view models as distinct from language-like rep-
resentations and believe that understanding of discourse often involves
the conversion of a linguistic representation into a model. We also agree
that one thing that distinguishes models from language-like representa-
tions is their structure, which has a systematic correspondence to the target
domain. However, what Johnson-Laird calls “models” are temporary struc-
tures in working memory, used in specific and transient tasks of compre-
hension and reasoning and forgotten sometime thereafter. The things that
I have called “models,” by contrast, are more like Gentner’s: structures for
understanding kinds of situations that are generally retained once acquired
and reused as needed. Of course, there is no reason to suppose that the two
hypotheses need be mutually exclusive. Indeed, it seems plausible that a
“modeling engine” should be capable of producing models of systematic
relations that are added to our permanent cognitive tool kit when they are
useful for recurring kinds of situations, or of constructing them only tem-
porarily to think on the fly about more particular situations.
9.2 Models in Philosophy of Science
The literature of philosophy of science has paid growing attention to the
role of models in science, though this literature is complicated by the fact
that the word ‘model’ is used in many different ways, some of which I
discuss hereafter. In introductions of model-based approaches to science,
it is standard practice to contrast a model-based approach with approaches
that treat scientific laws and theories as sets of statements or propositions
and scientific understanding as knowledge of, and inferences using, such
propositions. Ronald Giere, for example, writes:
The assumption that scientific theories are sets of statements goes along with the
view that scientific representation is to be understood as a two-place relationship
between statements and the world. A focus on the activity of representing fits more
comfortably with a model-based understanding of scientific theories. (Giere 2004,
743–744)
