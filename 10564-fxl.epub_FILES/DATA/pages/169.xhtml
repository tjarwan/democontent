177 Other Model-Based Approaches
A graphical model is a family of probability distributions defined in terms of a direct-
ed or undirected graph. The nodes in the graph are identified with random variables,
and joint probability distributions are defined by taking products over functions
defined on connected subsets of nodes. By exploiting the graph-theoretic representa-
tion, the formalism provides general algorithms for computing marginal and con-
ditional probabilities of interest. Moreover, the formalism provides control over the
computational complexity associated with these operations. (Jordan 2004, 140)
A distinctive feature of such models is their uses of a “graph” in a technical
sense of a structure of “nodes” representing variables and “edges” repre-
senting probabilistic relations between them. (Diagrammatically, these look
much like the structures used to illustrate semantic networks and frames.)
The framework is flexible, in that it can be adapted to an open-ended
number of contexts involving different variables and relations. Graphical
models thus supply a kind of mathematical orthotic for, say, statisticians
or economists reasoning under uncertain conditions, and Danks suggests
that the same kind of structure already exists in the mind as a fundamental
feature of our cognitive architecture.
At their core, graphical models can be understood as compact representations of rel-
evance relations, where different types of graphical models represent different types
of relevance (e.g., informational, causal, probabilistic, communicative). They thus
address a key challenge for any cognitive agent: namely, determining what matters
and, often more importantly, what can be ignored. The term “graphical model”
encompasses many different types of mathematical models, including Bayesian net-
works (also called Bayes nets), structural equation models, random Markov fields,
hidden Markov models, influence diagrams, social networks, command-and-control
structures, and more. At their cores, these models all have a graph: a diagram of
nodes and edges that encodes the qualitative relationships.
From a purely mathematical perspective, the nodes are simply objects, and the
edges can be undirected (A—B), directed (A→B) or bidirectional (A↔B). (Danks 2014,
39–40)
Danks argues that the graphical-model hypothesis can be used to account
for causal inference and reasoning, provide a useful reconstruction of major
theories of concepts, and explain decision-making procedures that effi-
ciently use the relevance information encoded in graphs. Graphical mod-
eling, moreover, encompasses a broad class of more specific model types,
differentiated by their features, such as whether they allow cyclic or only
acyclic structures.
