It would at this point be a matter of speculation just what proportion of
the mental models we use depend essentially on language. I am prepared
to allow that the proportion may be quite large indeed. Language provides
new forms of representation, communication, and interaction—not least
in the ability to form concrete questions—and together these greatly expand
the sphere of things we can form interests in, explore to find the relevant
patterns, interact with, and think about. Mastery of a domain still requires
us to form a mental model of it, but there are domains that are revealed
to us only because we can think in language-like ways and communicate
through language.
Representation and Reasoning across Models
One fundamental advance afforded by language is that it provides a
medium in which we can think thoughts that combine elements from dif-
ferent models. The inferential patterns associated with lexical items in a
language or concepts used in language-like thought may come from the
life they lead within models; but from the standpoint of language and
language-like thinking, a lexical or conceptual item’s model of origin is
irrelevant. It is simply a unit of a particular logical or grammatical type.
Concepts and lexemes originating in different models can be combined
grammatically, propositions involving them can be joined with logical con-
nectives, and the resulting propositions can be assembled into forms of
logical inference whose validity does not depend on their semantic values.
This endows us with capacities for cross-domain representation and reason-
ing that go beyond simply using two models in tandem, such as triangulat-
ing a common referent.
