2.1 The Central Role of the Concept of Belief
Intentional states, and particularly beliefs, figure prominently in several
major areas of philosophy: truth theory, epistemology, semantics, and logic.
Intentional states are states that are about something in the sense of having a
content, which is often something that can be formulated as a proposition.
I can believe that the cat is at the door, and belief is one kind of intentional
state. I can also want the cat to be at the door, hope that the cat is at the
door, fear that the cat is at the door, doubt that the cat is at the door, and so
on. These would all be intentional states with the same content (the cat is
at the door), but different “intentional attitudes” of believing, desiring, hop-
ing, and so on. Both belief and other intentional states, particularly desire,
are also centrally important to standard theories of action.
Truth is generally conceived as a relationship of correspondence between
beliefs (or their propositional contents) and states of affairs in the world.
There are various accounts of just what this correspondence relation-
ship amounts to, but the relata are the same: the belief (or the proposi-
tion believed) and an extramental state of affairs. There is also a minority
view to the effect that truth should be understood in terms of relationships
between beliefs, generally described as “coherence.” (This coherence theory
of truth is not to be confused with the coherence theory of knowledge that I
will describe shortly.) But both the correspondence theory and the coher-
ence theory of truth treat beliefs as the only mental units relevant to truth,
the difference between them being that the correspondence theory also
regards extramental facts as essential to an account of truth.

assumption that knowledge is warranted (or justified) true belief. (This charac-
