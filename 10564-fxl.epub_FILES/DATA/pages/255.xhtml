under uncertainty that which models and heuristics are apt for a given case
will depend crucially on details of the case and the information available to
the reasoner. Using a model that is inapt for framing a problem is likely to
produce an erroneous assessment.
Of course, this kind of case illustrates that it is often difficult or even
impossible to know for certain whether a given model can aptly be applied
to a particular situation, at least in part because the limited information
involved may prevent us from knowing what kind of situation it is. The
kind of “error” involved may often not be one where we “should have
known better.” The inaptness is ultimately determined by external stan-
dards, though we can also learn a great deal about different models and
what kinds of situations they are aptly and inaptly applied to. When we
do not actually possess an apt model and can only apply the models we do
possess, we may be subject to a weak form of forced error. (It is a weak form
in that it is forced only by the de facto lack of an apt model, as opposed to
the stronger cases in which we are incapable of having an apt model or in
which the use of an inapt model is automatic and cannot be interrupted or
overridden.)
Note, however, that the mere fact that I apply an inapt model that results
in an error is not itself sufficient to make the result count as an illusion.
There are, after all, different ways of applying models. At one end of the
spectrum, I might uncritically assume that the model frames the situation
adequately, and completely trust the resulting judgment, as in the case of
the intuitive judgments described in chapter 13. This would indeed seem
to merit the label “illusion.” At the other end of the spectrum, however, I
might be reflectively uncertain about what would provide an apt model of
a situation, and try out one or more models tentatively and experimentally.
