Explicit Rules and Principles
One can use a model with its principles remaining implicit and unarticu-
lated. Indeed, the many nonhuman species that can use model-based cog-
nition are probably capable of using a model only in an unarticulated way.
Language, however, gives humans the means to make principles of mod-
els explicit. Zebras can systematically treat lions as dangerous predators,
and so can we; but we can also think and say, “Lions are dangerous preda-
tors.” The ability to think it as a judgment affords certain aids to learning
through memorization, and the ability to say it enables social transmission
of knowledge without the need for each individual to learn through experi-
ence. It would be difficult to overestimate the role that learning through
the adoption of explicit principles plays in human life. (Certain familiar
styles of pedagogy are based almost solely on it.) Of course, making a princi-
ple explicit also enables us to examine, test, and refine it, though an explicit
principle can also be used and even believed uncritically.
Principles often take the form of grammatically general claims:
Xs are Ys
Xs do A
Xs have property P
Such a form can of course be an informal way of expressing universally
quantified claims like “All Xs are Ys.” But probably the vast majority of
even the useful general claims would be false if interpreted as universal
claims. And it is not clear that there is some other logically exact meaning
that they “really have,” unless perhaps the form “Xs are Ys” is itself counted
as a logical form. To spell out the apt use of such generalizations, we would
probably have to do something equivalent to defining boundary condi-
tions for scientific theories and would have to do so separately for each
such generalization. But we are seldom in a position to do this; we may
think a particular generalization is a useful rule of thumb, and also realize
that there are exceptions, but not know what the full range of exceptions is
