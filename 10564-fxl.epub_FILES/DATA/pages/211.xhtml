about it, it seems utter hubris to suppose that there is nothing about the
world that human minds are cognitively closed to. I think this is a plausible
idea, but what I wish to explore is a fourth alternative: that there may be
features of our cognitive architecture—of the things we do understand in
particular ways—that may themselves lead to disunities between the ways
we understand them; and that minds like ours may be unable to produce a
set of beliefs that is globally consistent or a single comprehensive model of
everything without losing some of the epistemic grip on the world that we
gain through many more localized, idealized models and the beliefs they
license.
12.3 Model-Based Understanding as a Source of Disunity
The basic idea here is fairly straightforward. It is the idea that there are
features of models that have implications for how two models are related
to each other, and sometimes these involve incommensurability, irreduc-
ibility, and inconsistency. Not all combinations of models are fraught with
these problems, but the fact that some are is simply a consequence of the
fact that models are each idealized ways of thinking about particular con-
tent domains that involve particular systematic ways of representing and
reasoning about their target domains. Viewed as a system for representing
and reasoning, a model has a particular formal shape that can be described
by rules. If two models do not represent their targets in the same ways,
