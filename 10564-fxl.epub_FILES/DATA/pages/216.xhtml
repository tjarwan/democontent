12.4 Scientific Disunity
For approximately the past century, physics has had two highly success-
ful, well-confirmed theories: a relativistic account of gravity and space-time
and a quantum mechanical account of strong, weak, and electromagnetic
forces. One of the major projects in theoretical physics is an attempt to
unite the two into a single theory. It may be that there would be some
physicists who would care about finding a grand unified theory simply
because they found the postulation of two distinct fundamental forces that
require different models to be inelegant or ontologically profligate. But this
cannot be the entire reason that physicists care so much about a grand uni-
fied theory. After all, for a long time, physicists operating within classical
mechanics were quite content with the idea that there are several inde-
pendent fundamental forcesâ€”mechanical force, gravity, electromagnetism,
chemical bonding. The difference was that scientists assumed that precisely
because these forces were independent, and all assumed a common Newto-
nian understanding of space and matter, their contributions to a comprehen-
sive and consistent mechanics could be computed through vector algebra.
There were several models of what were thought to be independent funda-
mental forces, but they were not inconsistent, precisely because they were
independent. The problem with relativity and quantum theory is that they
are held to be incompatible with each other. Now, if you look into just how
they are incompatible with each other, you will find an interesting vari-
ety of answers: there are differences in assumptions about renormalization,
local causation, and an absolute or relative temporal frame of reference;
and if you combine the mathematics in special cases like black holes, the
results are either inconsistent or mathematically incoherent. Some of these
concern incompatibilities between the formal features of the two models;
others are about how, when combined, they lead to contradictory predic-
tions or even mathematical nonsense.
It would be foolish to try to predict whether the search for a unified field
theory will be successful. What my cognitivist account does is to supply at
least a partial diagnosis of why such a puzzling situation might arise. We can
gain some perspective on this conundrum if we assume that each supplies
an apt but idealized model. Relativistic gravitation and quantum theory are
each extremely well confirmed, and to an extraordinary degree of accuracy.
For the phenomena to which they are applied, each is a highly apt model.
