It thus seems decidedly odd to characterize models as epistemic founda-
tions. Models may indeed be in some sense prior to beliefs and judgments,
and the epistemic credentials of beliefs and judgments ultimately depen-
dent on the qualities of the models in which they are framed. But given
that there are multiple learned models, often possessing different constitu-
tive beliefs and licensing contradictory judgments, the resulting picture is
closer to Neurath’s metaphor of a raft we are always reconfiguring than to
Schlick’s model of a pyramid of knowledge.
Alternatively, the modest foundationalist might be inclined to view
at least some of the processes by which models give rise to judgments as
forms of inference, distinct from, but not necessarily less respectable than,
deductive inference. Some foundationalists have long embraced nonde-
ductive inference forms—induction, retroduction—as conferring warrant,
and there seems no principled reason that other inferential processes could
not also be included. This strategy, however, strikes me as problematic as
well. I have no objection to calling some of the model-based processes
that produce judgments “inferences”; indeed, it seems a natural way to
characterize them. The potential sticking point is that if we do so, they
are inferences to things with propositional form but not from things with
propositional form, as models neither are, nor are made up of, beliefs or
judgments. The problem here is that foundationalism characterizes the
inferential grounding relation as one between beliefs, which would not be
true of an “inferential” process in which model-based processes generate a
judgment through their own nonlogical, nonargumentative mechanisms.
Broadening the foundationalist notion of “inference” in this way would
seem to risk losing a distinction that lies at the heart of foundationalism:
the distinction between the kind of warrant conferral that comes through
argument and whatever other ways a judgment or belief might come to
be warranted.
If models are themselves subject to some form of epistemic evaluation
and play a role in conferring warrant on judgments and beliefs, founda-
tionalism is an inadequate account of knowledge. Most fundamentally, it
