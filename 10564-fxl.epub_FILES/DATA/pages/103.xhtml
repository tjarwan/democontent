6.3 Blueprints
Architectural blueprints have a great deal in common with maps. They are
two-dimensional representations of three-dimensional physical phenom-
ena and are usually drawn at least roughly to scale. Geometric relations in
the blueprint thus usually reflect similar relations in the building, at least
along the horizontal axes. Of course, blueprints are normally constructed
before the building is built, while maps are usually drawn to depict exist-
ing geographical features. But this need not be the case: one can construct
a blueprint of an existing building, and planners create maps of cities and
road systems that are not yet built.
Also like maps, architectural blueprints employ special conventional
symbols to note standard elements of their domain: doors, windows, elec-
trical wiring, lighting, plumbing, and the like (fig. 6.7). They may also indi-
cate the locations of appliances and furnishings. Unlike the lines used for
walls, these conventional symbols do not always mirror the geometry of
the features they represent. A symbol for an electrical outlet, for example,
is proportionally larger in comparison to the representation of the wall it is
housed in than is the actual outlet to the wall, and the arc used in the sym-
bol for a door indicates the direction in which it opens and not a curved
object. Likewise, the different shading patterns used to indicate materials
(wood, metal) are not spatially accurate representations of objects but con-
ventions that indicate the materials used.
Blueprints, like maps, employ labels. But they also employ a special sort
of label not always found in maps: labels indicating dimensions. Indeed,
while blueprints are often scale drawings that preserve geometric propor-
tions, they need not be drawn in this way. A hand sketch noting dimen-
sions and features contains the information needed by the architect and
the builder.
