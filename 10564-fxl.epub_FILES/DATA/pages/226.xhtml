Arguments for the irreducibility of consciousness proceed along similar
lines. Levine (1983), Jackson (1982), and Nagel (1974) argue that the frame-
works provided by the physical sciences do not provide even candidate
explainers from which we could derive the characteristic features of the
first-person phenomenology of experience. Chalmers (1996) makes things
more explicit: our understanding of the physical world is cast entirely in
terms of structural and functional properties, but neither structural nor
functional properties supply the right sorts of resources for deriving the
qualitative character of experience.
These considerations might suggest that we encounter problems of irre-
ducibility specifically in the case of human phenomena: intentionality,
consciousness, normativity. And perhaps we find special kinds of problems
for reduction here that we do not find elsewhere. However, as I argued in
Beyond Reduction (2007), we find very few true reductions in the natural sci-
ences, either. And at least in some cases, when we look at particular types
of models and their would-be reducers, we can see principled reasons why
this might be so.

tionships in nature. Here I am concerned with a type of “emergence” that
would consist in phenomena that occur only in systems of a certain size
