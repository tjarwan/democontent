Eliminativism
Some philosophers and psychologists (a minority within both professions),
called “eliminativists,” completely deny some portion of the three-tiered
picture. For example, some claim that there are actually no concepts (Mach-
ery 2009, e.g., is sometimes characterized in this fashion), and others that
there are no beliefs (Churchland 1981; Stich 1983). Sometimes such claims
turn out on closer inspection to be more modest than they might initially
sound. Machery’s Doing without Concepts, for example, is really claiming
that the various rival theories of concepts describe separate phenomena
that cannot be accommodated within a single theory. It is not really the
eliminativist proposal the title might suggest, but a pluralist proposal.
Churchland’s claim that intentional states are “theoretical posits” of what
he deems to be a failed theory does not argue that we never make judg-
ments with propositional content, but argues that intentional psychology,
viewed as a theory that provides causal explanations of behavior, is a stag-
nant research program that even at its best would explain only a limited
range of behavior. These criticisms are indeed responding to the kind of
characterization given to intentional psychology by Fodor, and they raise
real (if controversial) questions about its explanatory scope and success. But
they leave unaddressed the larger question of whether intentional states are
an important structural feature of cognitive architecture (Horst 1995).
Connectionism
Connectionism, which treats cognition as emerging through dynamic
processes that take place in interconnected layers of neuronlike nodes,
involves a very different proposal about cognitive architecture (Rumelhart
and McClelland 1986; Churchland and Sejnowski 1989; Ramsey, Stich, and
Rumelhart 1991). It is controversial (among friends and foes alike) whether
it should be called a “cognitive architecture,” as some writers reserve the word
‘cognitive’ for states, processes, and architectures that operate on units with
representational content (e.g., Pylyshyn 1991), while others apply it more
broadly to subcognitive implementations of cognitive states and processes,
or even to anything that involves information processing, even early pro-
cessing of perceptual information. (Marr’s [1982] book on vision, after all,
