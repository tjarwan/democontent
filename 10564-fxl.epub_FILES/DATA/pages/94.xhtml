Multiple Models and Incommensurability
There can, of course, be multiple scale models of the same type of object.
For example, model trains come in different gauge sizes defined by different
scales. The engineer’s model of a car or airplane will model different features
than will a toy model. Two kits for modeling molecules may be in different
scales, use different materials, employ different coloring conventions, and
use different types of connectors. The kinds of information available from
two models of the same type (say, two HO-gauge train models or two mod-
els of organic molecules using the same kit) will afford information about
comparisons (one engine is longer than another) or combinations (the
two molecules can or cannot be combined into a larger molecule). But the
use of models of different types is more complicated. One cannot directly
compare the length of two train engines using an HO-scale model of one
and an O-scale model of another, though if one knows the scale of each,
one can calculate and compare the sizes of the originals by using the mod-
els, measuring, and applying algebraic calculations. Model molecules built
from different kits may allow some types of comparisons (the geometry, the
number of carbon atoms) but not allow the models to be used for other pur-
poses: the two models’ failure to fit together into a larger molecular model
may be due to different scales or connectors that do not match and tell us
nothing about what chemical combinations are possible; or if the connec-
tors do fit together by chance, the ways they do so may not correspond to
actual physical possibilities, and the geometry of the resulting model will
be incorrect if the components are of different scales. A mismatch between
the types of models combined can thus result in artifacts, and two models
may even be incommensurable in the sense that they cannot actually be
combined.
