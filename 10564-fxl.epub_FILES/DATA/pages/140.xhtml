The essence of metaphor is understanding and experiencing one kind of thing in terms
of another. It is not that arguments are a subspecies of war. Arguments and wars
are different kinds of things—verbal discourse and armed conflict—and the actions
performed are different kinds of actions. But
ARGUMENT is partially structured, un-
derstood, performed, and talked about in terms of
WAR. The concept is metaphori-
cally structured, the activity is metaphorically structured, and, consequently, the
language is metaphorically structured. (Lakoff and Johnson 2003, 5)
In metaphor, the language and the manner of understanding the source
domain (in this case, war) are taken over as ways of understanding the tar-
get domain (in this case, argument).
What Lakoff and Johnson say about metaphor has a great deal in com-
mon with what I have said about mental models: each metaphor works
systematically and obscures (idealizes away from) some things so as to high-
light others.
The very systematicity that allows us to comprehend one aspect of a concept in
terms of another (e.g., comprehending an aspect of arguing in terms of battle) will
necessarily hide other aspects of the concept. In allowing us to focus on one aspect
of a concept (e.g., the battling aspects of arguing), a metaphorical concept can keep
us from focusing on other aspects of the concept that are inconsistent with that
metaphor. For example, in the midst of a heated argument, when we are intent on
attacking our opponent’s position and defending our own, we may lose sight of the
cooperative aspects of arguing. Someone who is arguing with you can be viewed as
giving you his time, a valuable commodity, in an effort at mutual understanding.
But when we are preoccupied with the battle aspects, we often lose sight of the co-
operative aspects. (Lakoff and Johnson 2003, 10)
Moreover, we can use multiple metaphors to highlight different features of
the target domain. Lakoff and Johnson note several different metaphorical
frameworks for understanding ideas (as people, plants, products, commodi-
ties, resources, money, cutting instruments, fashions) and love (as a physi-
cal force, a [medical] patient, magic, war).
Lakoff and Johnson’s book (which is both eminently readable and well
worth reading) contains far more interesting observation and theory than
can be explored here. From the perspective of model-based cognitive plural-
ism, the important lesson seems to be this: sometimes we can form a model
of a domain by taking a model of some entirely different domain and trans-
posing its structure to be used as the basis for a new model. The system
of already-understood types, connections, and inference patterns from
the source model provides a kind of ready-made scaffolding for a model of
another domain. This is an exceptionally powerful learning technique, as it
