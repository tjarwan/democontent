14 Cognitive Illusion
Everyone knows about optical illusions like the Müller-Lyer illusion, in
which two lines that are really the same length appear to be of different
lengths because one is terminated in inward-pointing arrows and the other
in outward-pointing arrows. In a perceptual illusion, things seem one way
but are not really the way they seem. But not just any old mistake counts
as an illusion. At least as psychologists use the term, perceptual illusions
are persistent (e.g., the illusion does not go away just because you know
the lines are really equal) and are products of properties of our perceptual
systems.
We may also speak of cognitive illusions, in which it regularly and persis-
tently seems (intellectually rather than perceptually) as though something
is or must be the case due to properties of our minds. In philosophy, the
idea that the mind has operations that can, under certain conditions, pro-
duce cognitive illusions was most influentially explored by Kant. But Kant
meant something different by ‘illusion’ from what we would usually mean
in ordinary language. In the ordinary sense, an illusion involves a misrepre-
sentation: for a belief to be illusory, it would have to be false. Kant’s notion
of illusion, however, does not require falsity. What is illusory is the appear-
ance of being knowledge, regardless of whether the belief is true or false.
Kant’s own discussion of what he calls “transcendental” or “dialectical illu-
sions” has additional special characteristics that I will examine later, but
first it is useful to distinguish between two notions of a cognitive illusion.
Kant’s notion—an illusion that a belief is knowledge—we may call an epis-
temic illusion. From this we may distinguish something closer to ordinary
usage, an illusion in the sense that what is represented is false, as an alethetic
illusion (using the Greek word for truth, alethea). A Kantian epistemic illu-
sion can also be an alethetic illusion. But Kant was principally concerned
with epistemic illusions of a sort where we in fact cannot know whether the
belief in question is true or false.
