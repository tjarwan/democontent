5.8 Knowledge and Understanding
The distinction between models and their representational systems, on the
one hand, and token representational vehicles, on the other, has thus far
provided further distinctions between different types of alethetic virtues
(aptness and truth, respectively) and corresponding types of error. It is also
useful to distinguish the kind of epistemic grip on the world that is afforded,
on the one hand, by occurrent cognitive states that are the epistemic “size”
of single representations—that is, sentence sized—and afforded, on the
other hand, by a systematic model of some set of features.
I will speak of the representation-sized or proposition-sized units as judg-
ments or beliefs and (when the occasion befits) knowledge, and the model-
sized units (when they are apt) as understanding. This usage is, once again,
stipulative. These are all terms that have been used in a variety of techni-
cal ways by philosophers for centuries (and if we count equivalent words
in Greek and Latin, for millennia). My preference for applying the terms
‘belief’ and ‘knowledge’ to proposition-sized units is largely influenced by
recent epistemology, which has treated individual propositions or proposi-
tional contents as the units of belief and knowledge. (If one were to follow
ancient, medieval, or early modern usages, ‘knowledge’ might have been
aligned with differently sized units.) Indeed, even many coherentists are
content to use the justified-true-belief formula for knowledge, though what
