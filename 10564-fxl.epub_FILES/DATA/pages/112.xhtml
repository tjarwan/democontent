6.8 Further Considerations
As a first step toward this goal, let us first ask whether all the types of mod-
els we have surveyed in fact need to be (a) objects of attention that are (b)
understood to be models of some independently understood domain. In
at least one case—that of computer models—I think we find an exception
to this rule. It is true that the designer of a program needs to understand it
as a model of some phenomenon—say, of weather systems. But it is not
necessary that the user or consumer of the outputs of the program needs to
do so. When I watch the weather forecast on television, I am aware of the
diagrams that represent predictions of tomorrow’s weather. But I am gener-
ally not actively aware that they are being produced by a meteorologist’s
model. And I am, in point of fact, completely ignorant of how the program
does what it does, even at the level of what variables it takes into account.
Another viewer might even be ignorant of the fact that these maps are pro-
duced by computer models at all.
The same holds true when I check the forecast using the weather widgets
on my home computer. There is a modeling program running somewhere;
but for me, the widget and the program from which it gets its information
are more like a cognitive prosthesis. I have a skill at obtaining information
about tomorrow’s weather, just as I have a skill at looking out the window
to see what the weather is right now. But I am unaware of how the forecast
is produced, just as I am unaware of how my visual system allows me to see
that it is clear and sunny. Indeed, one can imagine that a day might come
in the not-too-distant future when people will be able to buy chips that will
be directly connected to the brain to supply such information without the
need to attend to some external object like a television or computer screen.
If such prostheses were implanted early in life, a child might actually have
to learn that his ability to check the weather forecast depended on a com-
puter running a model in another location. The point of this is that there
do seem to be ways that at least some types of external models can be used
without attending to the model and without understanding that there is
an external model operating somewhere that is the source of our informa-
tion. Such models still need to be designed by people who understand that
they are models, but this need not be a limit on their use. This is a useful
analogy in thinking about mental models: having and using a mental model
does not require that we understand the underlying processes of modeling
or information extraction, or even that we recognize that there is a mental
model at work.
