A third vision of unity is that an ideally completed science would employ
a single comprehensive theory of everything. This would not mean that
more particular theories of things like chemical reactions, thermodynam-
ics, or neural dynamics would cease to be employed, only that they would
turn out to be either derivable from the grand unified theory as special
cases applicable (and perhaps much easier to use) in particular contexts,
or else sufficiently close approximations of the implications of that theory
to be useful in those contexts. As theories are, in my view, models and not
propositions, we may regard this as a vision for replacing our many dif-
ferent models of distinct phenomena with a single comprehensive model
without a loss of explanatory power or predictive accuracy. The search for
a grand unified theory in physics is generally seen as an attempt to find a
single theory to unite a relativistic theory of gravitation and a quantum
theory of strong-weak-electromagnetic force. Many theoretical physicists
assume (and we might well ask why they so readily assume it) that it is only
a matter of time until we will hit upon such a theory, or at least that it is
available in principle, even if no one in fact uncovers it. But this is by no
means an obligatory view. Stephen Hawking, for example, has changed his
mind on this question:
Up to now, most people have implicitly assumed that there is an ultimate theory,
that we will eventually discover. Indeed, I myself have suggested we might find it
quite soon. However, M-theory has made me wonder if this is true. Maybe it is not
possible to formulate the theory of the universe in a finite number of statements. …
Some people will be very disappointed if there is not an ultimate theory, that can be
formulated as a finite number of principles. I used to belong to that camp, but I have
changed my mind. (Hawking 2002)
I will return to Hawking’s views shortly, as they are tied up with his “model-
dependent realism”—a view that I discovered only late in the process of
writing this book, but which has a great deal in common with cognitive
pluralism.
