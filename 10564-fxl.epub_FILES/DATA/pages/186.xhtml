Two very different types of complementarity deserve mention. The first
occurs when each type of system does something that the other cannot.
“Nativistic good tricks” provide an organism with ways of doing things that
could not be learned through “blank-slate conditioning.” (Or even if they
could be so learned in principle, the costs of having to learn them would be
astronomically high.) For example, animals more or less need developmen-
tally canalized ways of recognizing potential mates by responding to highly
specific types of cues and likewise need to perform the mating acts typical
of their species without having to learn them through observation or trial
and error. The vast majority of species also are born or hatched with some-
thing much closer to full adult capacities for perception and locomotion
than human infants and need to have such abilities immediately because
they do not have the benefit of an extended period of parental care. Being
able to learn through conditioning, by contrast, supplies precisely what
“nativistic” abilities lack: the ability to increase the organism’s adaptive
repertoire beyond what has been selected for and in response to demands
of its current environment, which is sometimes an environment that has
changed significantly from that of its ancestors.
But there is also a second type of complementary relationship between
canalized species-typical abilities and conditioning. There are some spec-
tacularly “closed instincts,” like the Sphex wasp’s routine for depositing its
eggs in a locust it has paralyzed. But many “instincts” are more “open,” in
the sense that they can be fine-tuned through conditioning. Some species
of wasps, for example, can build respectable nests on the first try but build
better nests on subsequent attempts. A kitten has an instinct to pounce on
small moving things but becomes an efficient hunter only through prac-
tice. “Built-in” abilities and conditioning are in one sense distinct building
blocks of intelligence, but much of an animal’s adaptive competence is a
result of their interaction.
