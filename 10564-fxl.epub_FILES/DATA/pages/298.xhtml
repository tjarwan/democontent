But how are we to distinguish which other concepts and inferences are
implicated in the semantics of a concept? In light of the previous chapters,
an answer suggests itself: the ones that are involved in the same model
are likely to be thus interrelated, the ones outside it are not. Inferential
semantics gets something right but does not itself have the resources to
differentiate between different types of inferential disposition. We need a
principled way of delineating such boundaries. A model-based cognitive
pluralism supplies a promising candidate: connections within a model are
likely to be constitutive of a concept’s semantic value, while dispositions
grounded in something outside the model are not.
There is, however, an important issue here that I will take up at the end
of the chapter: some concepts seem to appear in multiple models. The con-
cept
PLANET might occur both in a model of our own solar system (where
it applies to a small number of objects) and in a more general model of
the mechanics of gravitational systems (where it applies to an open-ended
number of objects). Concepts like
FRUIT and VEGETABLE seem to appear in bio-
logical, culinary, and nutritional models, but their extensions are differ-
ent in the different models. A tomato is a fruit according to a biological
taxonomy, but a vegetable according to a culinary taxonomy. Concepts of
mass are found in classical and relativistic theories but have different impli-
cations and different meanings within them. This raises problems about
the identity conditions for concepts and suggests that we may need to also
find a way to say that the life of concepts is not fully contained within indi-
vidual models, or else say that concepts found in different models are really
distinct concepts, though we use the same words to express them.
16.2 Cognitive Pluralism and Other Semantic Theories
Let us now turn to the relationships between a model-based cognitive plu-
ralist semantics and the most familiar alternatives, and the implications of
cognitive pluralism for those theories. The principal semantic theories are
atomist and holist theories, each of which is problematic from the stand-
point of a model-based cognitive pluralism.
