While I am not in a position to take a stand on whether there is a viable
unitary notion of species, or a variety of such notions, corresponding to the
units of evolution, Dupré’s analysis at the least sheds light on why we find
such a variety of ways of constructing the notion of species, and of classifi-
cations of organisms into species. There are a variety of practical scientific
(and nonscientific) standpoints from which the classification of organisms
is called for, and these call for different ways of conceptualizing species.
This is, moreover, not merely a matter of arbitrary convention or taste:
the different approaches reveal different real patterns in the things studied,
even if which patterns are regarded as salient depends on one’s theoretical
and practical interests. From the standpoint of cognitive pluralism, the fact
that different pursuits—including various scientific pursuits—require us to
adopt different models that cross-classify to bring the various salient pat-
terns to light means that we have a need for a variety of models that are
not fully aligned with one another, which produce different verdicts on the
boundaries of species but are apt for different purposes. And this may well
be a principled and abiding disunity, as the different purposes and salient
patterns may well not prove to be reducible to a single common denomina-
tor. Dupré’s other point—that there is more than one factor relevant to the
“units of evolution”—likewise gives us reason to suspect that we may require
multiple conceptual frameworks to fully understand them separately, and it
may not be possible to compress these into a single model that can serve as
an optimal successor to the intuitive notion of species.
