While this criticism undercuts a quick and easy way of explaining the
meanings of mental states, and a too-close assimilation of whatever under-
lies mental states to public-language symbols, it was never meant to imply
that there is no important use of the notion of representation in the cogni-
tive sciences. However, it is necessary to try to make explicit the notions
of “representation” and “representational system” that are relevant to the
current enterprise.
First, I view the principal form of the family of words ‘represent’, ‘repre-
sentation’, and their cognates to be the verb form: that is, to represent things
as being thus-and-so. Various senses of representation are derivative from this.
1. A “representation” in the primary sense is thus an act of representing
(that things are thus-and-so) (e.g., “Smith objected to Hill’s representa-
tion of the facts of the case”).
2. In a secondary sense, we may speak of the vehicle through which this
representing-as-thus-and-so takes place as a “representation.” It is in
this sense, for example, that we speak of token symbols and diagrams as
representations.
3. To the extent that there are features of the vehicle that allow vehicles
of that kind to be used systematically to represent things as being thus-
and-so, we may apply the term ‘representation’ to the type as well as the
token. (This distinction is necessary because some kinds of representa-
tion tokens, such as scribbled diagrams, may not fall into representa-
tional types, while others, such as symbols, typically do so.)
We may use the notion of “representing things as being thus-and-so” so
broadly that it can apply both to cases that are communicative (A repre-
sents things as being thus-and-so to B by uttering sentence S) and to cases
that are purely mental (A represents things as being thus-and-so [to her-
self] in the sense of, say, judging that they are thus-and so). The notions
of “representing” and “representation,” one should note, are mental and
