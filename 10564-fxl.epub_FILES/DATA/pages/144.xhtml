So the problem shapes up like this: given that the color of an object is
represented in one part of the brain, and shape in another, and there is no
downstream area for color plus shape, how is it that we know what shapes
and colors go together? If we are presented with a red triangle and a blue
circle, how do we know that
RED goes with TRIANGLE (and not with CIRCLE or
indeed with nothing at all) and
BLUE with CIRCLE? Similarly, if we are inspect-
ing things with eyes and hands and exploring a fuzzy triangle and a smooth
circle, how do we know that
FUZZY goes with TRIANGLE and SMOOTH goes with
CIRCLE, and not the other way around, or indeed that representations in
different modalities should be paired at all? One important hypothesis
about how the brain accomplishes such “binding” of different properties
was proposed by Crick and Koch (1993). The basic idea is that when two
representations in different sensory areas are bound together, the bound
representations are firing in phase with one another. The suggestion is thus
that, at least in perception, binding of representations of the same thing is
accomplished through a kind of phase locking.
The binding problem is a scientific variant on one aspect of Hume’s prob-
lem: specifically, as a problem about the ability to unite different represen-
tations synchronously as representations of a single thing in perception. To
the best of my knowledge, proposed solutions like the Crick-Koch hypoth-
esis have not been extended to nonperceptual intentional states (say, the
imagination of a red triangle) or to the integration of perceptual states over
time as representations of the selfsame enduring object (the same triangle
I saw yesterday).
The Frame Problem
As mentioned in chapter 4, one of the fundamental problems that second-
generation AI encountered was what Dreyfus calls the frame problem.
1
Even if we assume that routine thinking is accomplished through the appli-
cation of what Minsky calls “frames” and I call mental models, a further
problem arises of how to know which model to apply. Dreyfus argues—plau-
sibly, in my view—that this problem cannot be solved through standard
computational methods, and this seems to be a significant problem for any
theory that says that cognition is nothing but computation. It need not be
a refutation of model- or frame-based approaches, however. On the one
hand, a model-based approach to understanding need not be committed to
the view that model-based cognition is purely computational. On the other
hand, even if we assume that model-based cognition (in the sense of think-
ing within a model) is computational, this is compatible with the thesis that
the processes that determine which model to apply are noncomputational.
