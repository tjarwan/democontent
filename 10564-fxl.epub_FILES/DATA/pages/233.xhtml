13 Models and Intuition
In ordinary speech, calling something an “intuition” is tantamount to call-
ing it a hunch: an idea that pops into someone’s head as though from
nowhere and seems compellingly plausible even though they have no rea-
sons to support it. The use of the label often carries pejorative implications:
a judgment that we can give reasons for is regarded as better than a hunch,
precisely because we can give reasons for it. And there are good grounds for
such a suspicion: thoughts that occur to us spontaneously and without pre-
senting their epistemic credentials are often the products of bias, prejudice,
self-interest, fear, or wish fulfillment. At the same time, common sense also
recognizes that some people have “good intuitions”—indeed, some peo-
ple’s intuitions are regarded as brilliant, latching onto insights that escape
the rest of us and even the person who thought of them cannot explain.
In philosophy, “intuition” has traditionally been contrasted with rea-
soning. John Locke distinguished “intuitive knowledge” from “demonstra-
tive knowledge,” describing things that are immediately self-evident (e.g.,
the elementary propositions of Euclidean geometry) as “intuitive,” and
conclusions we reach only through explicit reasoning methods (e.g., work-
ing through a geometric proof) as “demonstrative.” In more contemporary
terminology, Lockean “intuitive knowledge” consists in judgments that are
true, justified, and produced in a fashion that does not depend on a prior
train of reasoning. Locke, however, did not regard intuitive knowledge as
inferior to demonstrative knowledge. In fact, he regarded intuitive knowl-
edge as the most certain form of knowledge: it is self-evident, and the other
kind of knowledge (demonstrative knowledge) needs to be based on prem-
ises that are known intuitively.
