But one might object that this is a strained and unnatural usage of the
word ‘model’: that the individual things I might build or imagine are mod-
els of molecules, but the modeling kit and the suite of mental abilities that
underlie the construction of the mental models of molecules are not. The
kit and the abilities might be specifically resources for forming models, but
this does not make them models. We might call them modeling systems
instead. And the same might be claimed for several of the other examples:
the chess-related skills I have are a modeling system that can be used to
build models of particular games or particular states of play; my abilities to
imagine and rotate physical solids make up a modeling system that I use to
form models of particular solids and their transformations; the folk biologi-
cal notion
ANIMAL is not itself a model of an animal but a kind of template
that can be used to form mental models of individual animals or types of
animals and thereby to reason about them; and so on.
The distinction between a model of X and a modeling system that pro-
vides resources for modeling many different possible Xs strikes me as a use-
ful one. But it is not clear that we should see models and modeling systems as
mutually exclusive categories. It is an important distinction of analysis, but
not a classificatory system.
In one sense, a chemical modeling kit is a set of resources for building
models of particular types of molecules. But it can also be viewed, at a more
abstract level, as a general model of various types of atoms, bonds, and
the ways they can be combined to form molecules. It is a model of the
molecular bonding potentials of molecules. In this sense, a model of a par-
ticular molecule is (also) a representation within the space of possible repre-
sentations generated by the representational system of the more abstract
model.
Similarly, in one sense, a mental model of chess is a set of resources for
playing games of chess. As such, it provides the framework for representing
particular states of play, and for mental models of particular games as they
evolve over successive turns. In this sense, it is a modeling system. But it can
also be viewed as a model of “the game” in the broader sense of “the game
called chess.” The model of “the game” provides a modeling system that is
a framework for representing particular states of play, moves, and games.
