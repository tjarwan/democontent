None of this is incompatible with the standard view’s supplying a useful
framework for describing the aspects of our mental life that philosophers
have cared most about, which arguably make up a substantial portion of
what is distinctive of human minds. But it does raise the question of how
“open” an architecture it is: whether it is relatively self-contained or can-
not really be separated from other aspects of the mind. Perhaps the old-
est version of this worry in philosophy concerns the relationship between
intentionality and perception. Any epistemological theory faces the ques-
tion of how sensation and perception provide warrant for beliefs. If we
view perception wholly in nonintentional terms, a problem arises for how
perceptual information can be translated into a form that provides warrant.
Conversely, if it has some element of intentionality within it, the realm of
meanings and reasons extends beyond sentence-like intentional states such
as judgments. There are similarly old problems about semantics, such as
whether intentional states include “nonconceptual content,” and to what
extent concepts are dependent on (perhaps even constructions out of) sen-
sations or perceptions.
A slightly different sort of issue is occasioned by psychological theories
claiming that there are undisputedly intentional phenomena that none-
theless seem to be segregated from the rest of our thinking: for example,
special-purpose systems for detecting danger, contagion, or sexual cues,
and special forms of reasoning that are applied only in social cognition.
Evolutionary psychologists suggest that these are isolated cognitive pheno-
typic traits that may well have their own neural substrates, adaptive histo-
ries, and modes of representation and inference. If this implies that they are
separate parts of the mind that work on independent principles, we would
need to posit a much more complicated kind of cognitive architecture. In
chapter 3, I explore an influential strategy for insulating a domain of “cen-
tral cognition,” characterized by the standard view, from threats stemming
from other mental “modules.”
