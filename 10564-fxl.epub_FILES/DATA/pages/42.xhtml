Parts 3 and 4 of Modularity of Mind are devoted to making the case for
two major claims. First, part 3 argues that “input systems”—including both
early sensory processing and the mechanisms underlying language pars-
ing—are modular. (A similar claim could probably be made for “output”
systems involved in motor control.) In the course of arguing this claim,
Fodor explores a number of features that he finds in such systems, and
these features can be viewed as a second list of features associated with
modules. Since they are spread across the section headings of part 3, I will
simply list them here:
1. Domain specificity
2. Mandatory operation
3. Limited central access (cognitive impenetrability)
4. Fast processing speed
5. Informational encapsulation
6. “Shallow” outputs
7. Fixed neural architecture
8. Characteristic breakdown patterns
9. Characteristic ontogenetic pace and sequencing
Domain specificity appears at the top of each list, but beyond that, the
reader is left to figure out how to map the two lists onto each other. I will
sidestep this issue by simply treating the second list as canonical.
The Modularity of Input Systems
Fodor regards what he calls “encapsulation” as the most important typical
feature of modules. Actually, Fodor sometimes seems to conflate encapsula-
tion and cognitive impenetrability. Both of these ideas are concerned with
what kinds of information from other parts of the mind are available to a
system. At a first approximation, we might think of cognitive impenetra-
bility in the following way: higher cognitive processes (including, but not
limited to, conscious introspection) have access to some but not all of what
goes on in the mind. In vision, I am aware of objects, and sometimes shapes
and colors, and perhaps even that my vision is a bit fuzzy this morning. But
I am not aware of the early visual processes that lead to visual experience,
like edge detection or the operations performed in the ganglion cells or
visual cortex. Indeed, it is not only that I characteristically am not aware
