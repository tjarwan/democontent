10.1 A Good Design Strategy for Evolving Smarter Animals
The easiest question to address is why the minds of “simpler” organisms
consist in—or at least rely heavily on—a kind of “grab bag of good tricks.”
This is well-explored territory. First, and most fundamentally, accrual of dis-
tinct traits that have individually proved adaptive is something one finds
quite generally as an outcome of variation and selection. We have no rea-
son to think that it should prove any different when the traits in question
are cognitive traits. This does not make it a good strategy, but it does mean it
is one we are likely to get.
Second, cognitive traits that are products of evolution and developmen-
tally canalized also tend to have dedicated and efficient neural circuitry,
and as a result, they tend to be fast. And when it comes to the things that
count for selection, like avoiding predation, fast is good. In fact, speed is
often more important than accuracy. In the case of predator detection, at
least, false positives (i.e., thinking a predator is present when none really
is) are less costly than false negatives (i.e., failing to detect a predator that
is really there). An animal can afford to bolt for cover when a cloud passes
overhead many times, but cannot afford to miss a predator that is stalking
it even once. It also cannot afford to stand around thinking carefully about
whether a stimulus was really caused by a predator. Careful rumination
is good for some things, but avoiding a charging tiger or a swooping owl
is not among them. So even if it were possible to start out with animals
that represented the world through deliberative, general-purpose reasoning
rather than reflexive good tricks, there is a good chance that they would all
be eaten by tigers.
Third, with special-purpose mechanisms that are adaptive, more is bet-
ter. Precisely because they are special purpose, each one is useful in only
a limited range of situations. Consequently it is a sound evolutionary
meta-strategy to accumulate individual adaptations that do different useful
things.
Fourth, care of the young requires special cognitive mechanisms of its
own, and these are lacking in most species. As a consequence, most animals
(but particularly those that do not have parental protection) need to be able
to behave adaptively very quickly. They thus need a basic tool kit of “good
tricks” that are products of early development, even if they are capable of
more sophisticated types of learning if they survive long enough.
