One obvious observation about the units of the three-tiered picture is
that they clearly correspond to the structural units of language and logic as
well. Words in a natural language and predicate letters in logic correspond
to concepts, sentences and well-formed formulas correspond to intentional
states such as beliefs, and both language and logic have larger units called
“arguments” corresponding to inferences, though of course natural lan-
guages have many other types of structured discursive units as well (stories,
jokes, etc.) that are absent from logic. It seems clearest to characterize the
structural size of the units with terminology drawn from the description of
language: that is, as word-sized, sentence-sized, and argument-sized units.
Moreover, some of the ways theorists have expanded on the three-tiered
picture are modeled on things found in linguistics and logic as well: for
example, the idea that the meanings of judgments are a function of the
meanings of concepts plus compositional syntax, and philosophers’ preoc-
cupation with the particular (deductive) forms of inference that predomi-
nate in standard logics. Of course, this raises a number of questions about
the relationship between thinking, language, and logic that I cannot fully
address here. For example, is our capacity for language simply an ability to
express publicly what was already available mentally in the forms of think-
ing characteristic of human minds, or is the ability to think in these ways
a consequence of learning a public language, or at least being a member
of a species whose brains have capacities that were originally selected to
produce competence in spoken language? Does thinking literally take place
in a “language of thought” (Fodor 1975), or is the relationship between
thought and language more analogical—say, that thinking and language
have structural units with similar sizes and functions? I shall not attempt
to settle these questions here. For now, suffice it to say that the theories
of a number of core areas of philosophy suggest that thought, or at least
a significant portion of thought, is “language-like” in the sense of having
structural units analogous to those found in languages.
