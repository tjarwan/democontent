These considerations really boil down to two things. The production of
separate special-purpose cognitive traits is something we should expect from
evolutionary processes of variation. And the accumulation of such traits is
something we should expect from evolutionary processes of selection.
10.2 Still a Good Design Strategy for Animals That Learn
It is not really all that controversial that multiplication of separate adap-
tive cognitive traits is an evolutionary strategy whose imprint is seen in
simpler animals. Indeed, it was not uncommon for both philosophers and
biologists of previous generations to think of human and animal minds in
starkly contrasting terms: nonhuman animals have instincts but not rea-
son, while humans have reason but few if any instincts. This bifurcation,
of course, obscures the vast differences found among the minds of different
nonhuman species. But it also misrepresents the human mind and implies
a highly unrealistic evolutionary history.
The fact is that the human mind is by no means devoid of special-purpose
mechanisms that are developmentally canalized and products of selection.
Indeed, moral philosophy has often recognized this, though primarily in its
castigation of those whose conduct is governed by “the appetites.” But spe-
cial-purpose mechanisms, ranging from the blink reflex to the contagion-
avoidance reaction of disgust and incest avoidance, are not all appetitive,
and many still serve useful functions. Now, if we are “rational animals,”
why is it that we still have all of these nonrational cognitive mechanisms?
