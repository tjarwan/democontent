6 Models
One of the crucial theses of cognitive pluralism is that a fundamental unit
for the analysis of understanding is a mental model. Chapter 5 presented
a summary overview of what I mean by a mental model and its role in
cognitive pluralism: that models are idealized domain-sized units of under-
standing, each of which has its own internal ontology for representing the
objects, properties, relations, events, and processes of its content domain
and its own rules for inferences about the properties and transformations
of these. Perhaps some readers will have found these to be clear and intui-
tively plausible claims in the form in which they were initially presented.
But the notion of a mental model may also need more deliberate explica-
tion, not only because it is of such central importance to cognitive plural-
ism, but also because the notion I wish to develop could easily be confused
with other notions that have gone by the same name.
Over the past thirty years or so, model-based theories have grown in
popularity in both the cognitive sciences and philosophy of science, even
if they have made curiously little impact on other areas of philosophy,
including philosophy of mind. But if their advocates agree on one thing, it
is that many different uses of the expressions ‘model’ and ‘mental model’
are at work in their respective literatures. Some of these are quite different
from the idea I have in mind, some roughly consonant with it, and some
would count, by my standards, either as types of models or as narrower
theoretical claims about how models are implemented in human cognitive
architecture.

intuitively, first examining several familiar instances of things called “mod-
els” in this chapter before proceeding to look at specifically mental models
