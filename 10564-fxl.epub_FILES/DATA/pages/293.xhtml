15.4 A View of the Status of Accounts of Epistemology
The basic line of criticism I have leveled at standard epistemological views
has been that while each may pick out factors that are relevant to the assess-
ment of warrant, none of them can tell the full story. In the case of foun-
dationalism and coherentism, the problem is that, as general theories, they
leave important things out or get some things wrong. In the case of reliabi-
lism, the issue is that a simple appeal to “reliability” does not tell us much
about the psychological nature of the reliable mechanisms.
Foundationalism, coherentism, and reliabilism are often treated as rival
theories of warrant. In philosophical writing, proponents of one account
will often show how their theory handles a particular range of cases well,
but argue that the rival accounts yield counterintuitive assessments of
which beliefs are warranted. The underlying assumption of such arguments
is that each theory is taken to be offered as a comprehensive and unideal-
ized account of the same thing: something called “epistemic warrant” or
“knowledge.” But cognitive pluralism suggests an alternative way to view
them. Each account is an idealized account of one dimension of epistemic
evaluation. Beliefs can be justified by their relationships to other beliefs
in various ways, and the ways we reason from some beliefs to others are
relevant to the overall status of the derived beliefs, as the foundationalist
suggests. The ways various beliefs cohere or conflict with one another are
also relevant to their epistemic status, as are the ways they are produced
by mechanisms that are or are not reliable in a given type of situation.
Arguably, each account provides a useful way of looking at how beliefs can
go awry that is not captured by the others. We might thus look at differ-
ent epistemological theories not as rival accounts but as multiple ideal-
ized ways of looking at an issue (epistemic warrant, or how beliefs come to
