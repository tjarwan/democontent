J. J. Gibson, the founder of ecological psychology, called such properties
affordances and would say that the nectar “affords eating” to the butterfly—
that is, makes it available in a fashion the butterfly can exploit (Gibson
1966, 1977). Affordances are “real” in that they are not imaginary and in
that detecting an affordance is not making a mistake but getting something
right. They are “objective” in the sense that they do not depend on what
anyone believes or how she feels about it. But they are not “objective” in
the sense of being located solely in the object to which they are attributed. (In
one philosophical terminology, they are not intrinsic.) Both metaphysically
and scientifically, they are perfectly respectable on a par with gravitation,
though of course they are not fundamental, and many of them may not
appear among the principal theoretical terms of any science.
On the other hand, the concepts and models that we use to think about
affordances can differ greatly, from the standpoint of psychology, from the
models we use to think about a world of objects and properties. In particu-
lar, an organism can detect and respond to affordances without conceiving
them as things or as properties attached to things. We can think of edibility or
dangerousness as properties of things, to be sure. But even organisms that
probably lack anything closely resembling human concepts or even object-
oriented thought need to be able to detect and respond to affordances if
they are to do things like eat and avoid danger. Mosquitoes are sensitive to
differences in levels of carbon dioxide in the air and use these differences
to navigate toward mammals to obtain a blood meal. They probably do not
represent any of this to themselves in terms of objects and properties but
merely have circuitlike mental models in their tiny brains that allow them
to surf the affordance trail. Much of our nonconscious cognition consists
in affordance detection as well: we simply register that a particular surface
does or does not afford a stable foothold and adjust our path accordingly,
for example. But much of our conceptual thought is oriented toward affor-
dances, too: we see a chair as a comfortable spot to sit down or a water foun-
tain as a potential source for slaking our thirst. When reliable connections
exist between features of the environment and particular things we need
or do, affordance detectors pick out real patterns as well. They are simply
