This description, of course, leaves a number of questions open, such as
whether various techniques of critical thinking are directed toward thought
tokens, thought types, Fregean propositions, or linguistic representations
such as sentences. It also leaves open the question of whether critical lan-
guage-like thinking depends essentially on public language. I think there are
two types of (potentially defeasible) reasons to assume that it does. First,
critical thinking generally involves thinking about representations formu-
lated in a public language, even if this is done without overt speech. Sec-
ond, we generally learn to think critically through dialectical interaction
with others. While I cannot rule out the possibility that a clever individual
might develop forms of critical thinking on her own, it seems quite likely
to me that, in general, the capacity for individual critical thought arises by
way of a kind of internalization of interpersonal dialectic. (Of course, even
a brilliant individual who pioneers critical thinking from the ground up on
her own would do so using linguistically expressed thoughts, even if some
of the vocabulary is of her own invention and has not been shared with
others.)
External Symbol Storage
An important extension of natural language is provided by writing. While
a good portion of the earliest extant examples of writing are accounting
records, anything that can be said can also be represented in external sym-
bols. Merlin Donald (1991) has argued that external symbol storage is itself
one of the great watersheds in the history of human cognition, particularly
because it allows the storage and retrieval of information to be freed from
the vagaries of individual memory. Before writing, the stock of informa-
tion available to a community was largely confined to what was known
and remembered by its elders. Writing allows information to be preserved
indefinitely and to be conveyed to broad audiences, widely separated in
space and time.

prosthesis that expands and refines our ability to think and reason more
clearly. A written sentence can be scrutinized and reexamined, allowing
