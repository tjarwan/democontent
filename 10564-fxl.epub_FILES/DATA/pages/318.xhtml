Projective classification. The constitutive rules of models can determine projective
classes of objects or properties for their CMs—i.e., the set of things characterized by
the rules of the model applying to the CMs.
The “outward” relationships between CMs and features, however, are
potentially of special interest, as they might provide a way of explaining
what is right about theories of semantics that treat concepts as determin-
ing the conditions for membership in a class of objects. There is a sense in
which this is correct: models do project a subjective ontology, and CMs are
the natural locus for the units that determine a projective class of objects.
On the other hand, concepts also have an ostensive element. We can
in some sense have a concept of a thing—in the sense of a concept that
has the function of tracking that sort of thing—without having a concrete
understanding of its nature. And we can still have a concept of a kind of
thing even if we misunderstand its nature and the projective ontological
classification arising from a CM does not in fact pick out something as we
suppose. This requires that we add another element to our schema: a com-
ponent of a concept like Husserl’s “pure x” that has the function of tracking
an object, kind, property, relation, or process. Again, there are variants on
this theme where the normative ostensive relationship is tied to a CM, to a
