Rosch’s emphasis on “the perceived world” suggests that a core concern of
the theory may be more narrowly about categorization in perception. This
is even more clearly evident with exemplar theories, which treat concepts
as being (or involving) stored memory traces of exemplars of a category
(Smith and Medin 1981).
Perceptual categorization, or indeed categorization in general, is not the
only thing a theory of concepts should explain. But it is one important role
concepts play in our mental economy. Most theories of categorization in
psychology treat the categorization process as one that assigns a category
based on features. In classical theories, the features might be thought to pro-
vide necessary and sufficient conditions for the applicability of the concept.
Prototype and exemplar theories, by contrast, are probabilistic: a concept is
activated by some matching process involving either a set of prototypical
features or a set of exemplars stored in memory. The process may involve
a comparison of the stimulus to multiple prototypes or exemplars, and its
dynamics may be tuned to activate the concept with the best fit with the
stimulus. The kinds of inferential relationships that models are supposed to
underwrite need not figure in this, and it seems clear that mechanisms for
categorization could exist that do not require mental models.
Moreover, it seems reasonable to suppose that there are types of animal
minds that possess categorizing mechanisms that stand between percep-
tual inputs and behavioral control without these being embedded in mental
models of content domains. There are useful types of cognition that you
can obtain only once you have interposed something like a concept (or, if
you prefer to restrict that word to humans or other language users or other
conscious beings, a protoconcept or a feature aggregator) between the stimulus
end and the motor end of the cognitive architecture. Think of it this way:
the success of a motor response often depends little on what sensory chan-
nel an affordance or object is detected through, and very much on what
kind of affordance or object is detected, and the state it is in. Moreover, there
can be many stimuli that are cues to a single type of affordance (typed rela-
tionally) or object (typed “objectively”), and there might be many motor
schemas that are useful for responding to the affordance or object. It is
