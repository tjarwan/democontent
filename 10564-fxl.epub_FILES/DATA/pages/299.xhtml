We may distinguish, in terms of analysis, between a weaker and a stron-
ger thesis here:
Compositionality (weak): The semantic value of each sentence-sized unit is
a function of (a) the semantic values of its constituent word-sized units
plus (b) its combinatorial syntax (or whatever plays a role analogous to
syntax within judgments).
Semantic independence (strong): The semantic value of each word-sized unit
is independent of the sentence-sized units in which it is used, including
the beliefs one has involving the word-sized unit and the judgments
using it that one is disposed to make.
Whether these two theses are equivalent depends on the notion of “inde-
pendence” assumed. There is a clear sense in which the concept
BACHELOR
cannot be “independent” of a commitment to the judgment or belief that
bachelors are unmarried. One cannot be said to possess the concept

But whether or not one holds there to be this unidirectional depen-
dence between semantic value and epistemic commitment, there might
nonetheless be a compositional dependence: the sentence “all bachelors are
unmarried” means what it does as a function of its constituents and its
compositional syntactic structure. The potential confusion to be avoided
