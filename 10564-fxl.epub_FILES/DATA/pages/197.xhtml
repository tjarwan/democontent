The purely communicative use of language generally succeeds only to
the extent that speaker and hearer already share the kind of frame of ref-
erence provided by a mental model. When models are shared, a compact
utterance can provide enough information for a rich set of inferences. But
without such a shared understanding, there is unlikely to be uptake about
the implications or even the sense and reference of the lexical items. If I
read a random sentence from a book on a topic I know nothing about, I
can probably recognize it as a grammatical sentence but will have no real
understanding of what it is saying. It is the models that lie in the back-
ground that generate a space of possible sensible representations in lan-
guage. To understand what is meant, I may have a great deal of work to do
acquiring the requisite models.
But to do so, I need not engage in a process of rediscovery de novo.
Language can also provide more efficient means for guiding me through
the process of model acquisition. Indeed, the art of using language in this
manner is precisely what is needed for a good explanation or the composi-
tion of a good textbook. In some cases, a really good verbal explanation
may be all the external prompting a reader or hearer needs to form a model
that at least roughly corresponds to the speaker’s or writer’s. In other cases,
language may at least play important assisting roles, such as pointing out
where the important variables lie or correcting or sharpening the learner’s
understanding. Learning a manual skill, a form of dance, or the structure of
a new type of music probably cannot take place without exposure to, and
practice with, the domain of study, and things like mimesis, pointed rep-
etition, and reshaping of what the student is getting wrong may be indis-
pensable. But even these can often be greatly aided by the use of verbal
prompting. (“You are swinging the hammer from the side and bending the
nail rather than driving it in.” “In this style of playing the jig, the accents
go on the first and third of six beats, not the first and fourth.”) Commu-
nication using terminology based in other, already-shared models can thus
help the learner quickly revise a new model she is building while learning.
