While philosophers are concerned primarily with these accounts as
frameworks for evaluation, they can also be used to guide conduct. A per-
son can try to foresee the consequences of various alternative actions and
evaluate their likely costs and benefits. She can take stock of what duties
she believes herself to be under and reason out what they require of her.
A person can practice the things needed to acquire a particular virtue like
patience and can inspect her motives and veto prospective action plans
that would be done out of vices like impatience.
The philosophical subdiscipline called metaethics contains a great deal
of literature that attempts to adjudicate which of these frameworks is a bet-
ter account of what is morally good. For our purposes, however, the more
important point is that each framework provides unique tools for evaluating
and shaping action and character. Each framework picks out different fac-
tors that are relevant for such evaluations—consequences, relation to duty,
and character—and each idealizes away from certain things that another
brings into focus. It seems almost certain to me that the ordinary person is
better off having multiple models of human goodness than having just one
of them. And I am inclined to suspect that even careful theoretical formula-
tions are not enough to build all the insights of all three types of model into
a single framework, unless perhaps it is a metaframework that uses each by
moving back and forth between them. There are some things for which
we have concepts—like goodness—that we need to approach from various
angles, through a number of distinct (and sometimes conflicting) models.
