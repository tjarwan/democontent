Distinguishing two types of holist claims will help here. The first is that
anything, from any domain of understanding (including things we do not
yet understand and are completely unaware of), could in principle prove
to be relevant to any other concept, belief, or inferential commitment, and
hence a change to any unit anywhere could require adjustments anywhere
else. The cognitive pluralist can accept this as a “for all we know” kind of
claim: I cannot rule out in advance the possibility that some change in my
understanding of physics might force me to rethink how I understand some
other domain like chess, dining, or ethics. It may seem unlikely, as I cannot
see how it could do so. But this is in part because I cannot anticipate what
change in my understanding in physics might someday come along that
would require such adjustments; and for this reason, I cannot definitively
say in advance what other areas of understanding are insulated from it.
The second and stronger type of holist thesis is that each concept, belief,
and inferential commitment is constitutively bound up with every other,
so that any change is eo ipso a change to everything else in the system.
Cognitive pluralism differs from such a constitutive holism in precisely the
areas where constitutive holism seems most vulnerable. It seems correct
to say that a change in meaning, belief, or inferential commitments has
ramifications to other concepts, beliefs, and inferential commitments, but
those ramifications have limits. If I change my understanding of, say, how
a knight moves in chess, this clearly has constitutive implications for how
I need to adjust my understanding of the concept
CHESS_KNIGHT and other
aspects of my understanding of chess. But it does not follow that I need to
similarly adjust my understanding of mechanics or of restaurant etiquette.
Similarly, a transition from classical to relativistic mechanics involves a
change in concepts of
MASS and SPACE and changes in beliefs and inferen-
tial commitments within the domain of mechanics, but it need not have the
slightest impact on my understanding of chess or the semantics of a con-
cept like
GRANDMOTHER. What cognitive pluralism adds to this commonsense
insight is a theory of why it might be correct: namely, that there is a kind
of psychological unit (a mental model) distinct from concepts, beliefs, and
inferences, which sets the bounds on how far such changes should ramify.
