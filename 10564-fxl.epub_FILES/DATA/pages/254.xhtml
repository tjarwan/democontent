follow conventional practice by using the term ‘normative’ to describe the use of a

for the particular problem” (Nisbett & Ross, 1980, p. 13). (Gigerenzer 1991, 2)
The word illusion is used here in a sense suggesting an error, either in the
outcome of the judgment or in the choice of a heuristic unsuitable to the
problem because it differs from a statistical norm. This is readily translated
into the framework we have been developing, as Kahneman and Tversky’s
explanations of the errors in fact invoke mental models:
Many adults do not have generally valid intuitions corresponding to the law of large
numbers, the role of base rates in Bayesian inference, or the principle of regressive
prediction. But it is simply not the case that every problem to which these rules are
relevant will be answered incorrectly, or that the rules cannot appear compelling in
particular contexts. The properties that make formally equivalent problems easy or
hard to solve appear to be related to the mental models, or schemas, that the prob-
lems evoke. (Kahneman and Tversky 1982, 129–130)
In our terminology, the diagnosis of the errors would seem to be that sub-
jects frame the problem with which they are presented using a model that
is not apt for that problem, and derive an answer using heuristics associ-
ated with the model. Gigerenzer likewise appeals to “probabilistic mental
models” as a general framework for explaining people’s confidence in their
general knowledge (Gigerenzer, Hoffrage, and Kleinbölting 1999).
What seem to be primarily at issue between Gigerenzer and Kahneman
and Tversky are (a) the latter’s use of judgments of professional statisti-
cians as a normative standard for determining when the results of heuris-
tic methods are erroneous and (b) the extent to which the heuristics that
people routinely use are truly inapt, and hence whether the results are truly
“illusory.” These differences are not relevant to our topic here, as we are
concerned with epistemic rather than alethetic illusions, and so what is at
issue is not whether the resulting judgments are correct but merely that we
confidently assume them to be known. What is germane is a conception of
how the mind—or more specifically the application of particular models
