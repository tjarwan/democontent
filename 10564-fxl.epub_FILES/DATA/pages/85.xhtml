While each representational system has its internal ontology, these vary
in their degree of “objectivity.” By this I mean that there are varying degrees
to which the internal ontology is an artifact of things about the organ-
ism itself. The human color space, for example, does not neatly map onto
electromagnetic frequencies. It is heavily conditioned by the properties of
human cone and ganglion cells in the retina and is presumably a prod-
uct of selection that has been shaped over evolutionary history to track a
particular set of visual invariants that have been relevant to the adaptive
performance of the species in an ancestral environment or a succession
of such environments. At the opposite extreme, the ontology of objects
and properties in contemporary physics has been carefully developed to
exclude, so far as it is possible, artifacts of human perception and cognition,
and to track properties that objects have independently of how we perceive
or think about them. Such “objectification” is never entirely complete, as
we must always think about objects in some particular way, and the avail-
able ways are constrained by our cognitive architecture. (For example, our
tendency to think of the world in terms of discrete objects with properties
is probably due to a deep bias of human cognition, one that proves prob-
lematic when we turn to quantum phenomena. But even our representa-
tions of quantum phenomena reflect human concepts imported from other
domains by metaphorical transposition (e.g., “wave”), and a stock of math-
ematical concepts that the human mind is suited to dealing with. And,
indeed, many people seem to be unable to comprehend a system without
classical objects at all.)
Each representational system has its own formal shape. In some cases,
such as theories in physics, the formal shape is made explicit, and the
system is partly defined by mathematical equations. In other cases, it is
implicit and is made explicit only by formal modeling, such as is under-
taken in computational psychology and computational neuroscience.
