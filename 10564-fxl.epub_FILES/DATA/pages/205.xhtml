12.1 Visions of Unity and the Problems They Face
Let’s start by reviewing what has been said so far about the units of knowl-
edge and understanding. If knowledge is justified true belief, then the units
of knowledge are sentence sized. I have used the term ‘understanding’ for a
broader and more interconnected sort of comprehension of a domain—an
understanding of chess, or of Newtonian mechanics, or of the Japanese
tea ceremony—and argued that mental models are fundamental units of
understanding. The implicit rules of a model are among the things that
might be counted as dispositional beliefs (say, that bishops move only
diagonally or that bodies exert a gravitational attraction described by the
inverse square law). The conceptual resources of models are used to frame
more specific judgments (say, that the white bishop can capture the black
rook on the next turn or that this rock is accelerating in free fall toward the
ground). The understanding associated with a model also includes abilities
to recognize particular situations as ones to which the model may aptly be
applied and to act in a fashion guided by the model.
To get clearer on ways that beliefs and models might be “unified” or
“disunified,” it is useful to start with science, if only because there has been
so much prior discussion of “the unity of science.” Science has often been
characterized in terms of a set of beliefs about theories, laws, and empirical
facts, but scientific theories are really paradigm examples of mental models.
We can have beliefs about scientific models—say, that Newtonian mechan-
ics provides an apt model for certain types of situations. Explicitly formu-
lated laws express basic commitments of such models. And more specific
types of scientific beliefs and judgments involve the use of a model to frame
a situation and apply the rules of the model to reason about it. But scientific
models also stand in various relationships to one another: they involve
consonant or dissonant commitments, representational systems, and forms
of reasoning and can be used to license judgments that are consistent or
inconsistent with one another. I thus begin by discussing three visions of
scientific unity, along with brief summaries of reasons that they have been
difficult (and perhaps impossible) to achieve.
