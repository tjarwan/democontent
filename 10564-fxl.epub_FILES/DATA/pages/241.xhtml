Another type of intuitive process might involve a simulation—say, a sim-
ulation of what would happen in the game if I tried a particular strategy.
This uses the internalized rules of the model to produce a series of represen-
tations of board states, or perhaps a representation of how a strategy is likely
to unfold. Here the internalized rules guide the dynamics of the simulation
process. Similarly, when I imagine the rotation of a solid, the rules of the
model I employ for spatial reasoning generate a simulation, producing an
intuition about the end result. I may well regard the result with confidence,
as it is produced by the rules of the model.
Another type of intuitive process is what is often called “semantic infer-
ence.” Many things seem intuitively obvious about dogs, even if we have not
thought about them before: dogs are animals, carnivores, quadrupeds, and
mortal; dogs have hair, hearts, kidneys, and emotions; dogs are offspring
of other (pairs of) dogs; and so on. We would correspondingly find other
claims counterintuitive: dogs are vegetables, vegetarians, bipeds, immortal,
or robots; dogs have scales, gills, or opinions on philosophical issues; dogs
are created on engineers’ workbenches, are made of fire, or have parents
that are fish; some dogs have masses greater than that of the earth; and so
on. Some of the intuitively obvious claims might have been learned and
stored as facts about dogs, perhaps even appearing in part of a definition or
characterization of dogs you learned; but many of them are things you are
unlikely ever to have thought of before, and yet they are intuitively obvi-
ous. Why might this be so? An answer that recommends itself is that the
concept
DOG either is itself, or is part of, a kind of richly structured semantic
entity that affords a rich set of inferences that go beyond the judgments
that have previously been formed.
