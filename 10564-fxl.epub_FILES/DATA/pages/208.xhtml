There is also a set of problems about the relationship between mathemat-
ics and the world of nature: mathematical truths are not a consequence of
how the world is, and have a kind of necessity that could not be underwrit-
ten by empirical facts and laws. Mathematics cannot be reduced to phys-
ics. But neither can physics be reduced to mathematics (despite the hopes
of some thinkers of the seventeenth century), as there are purely physi-
cal properties like mass as well, and various mathematical systems provide
descriptions of various alternative forms space might take, and waves,
particles, and fields that behave in alternative ways. And the discovery of
non-Euclidean geometries in the nineteenth century brought the startling
realization that while individual mathematical systems might be internally
consistent and derived from a small set of axioms, there are indefinitely
many such systems whose axioms are inconsistent with one another.
We might, moreover, raise similar questions about many more ordinary
domains of understanding. Chess pieces and chessboards are typically gar-
den-variety physical objects—pieces of wood or plastic. (The ontology of
the “pieces” in computer chess is more complicated, but still physical. The
status of those in mental chess turn on whether the mind itself is physical.)
But a complete understanding of the pieces and board in physical terms
would not confer an understanding of the game of chess. Perhaps the die-
hard reductionist might insist that “the game of chess” is simply a set of
conventions that could be reduced to the mental states of those who share
the conventions, which could in turn be reduced to fundamental phys-
ics. And if such reductions could be carried out, they might well explain
the behavior exhibited in chess matches. But it is unclear that they would
explain the game as an abstract entity any more than an explanation of the
mental states of mathematicians would explain mathematics.
