Understanding chess seems to require a mental model of the game,
which in turn involves a number of kinds of abilities. At the least, these
would include a way of representing the board as a geometrically and topo-
logically describable space of possible piece positions, along with an under-
standing that at most one piece may occupy a square at any one time,
representational types for kinds of pieces and permissive rules for their
movement, definition of an initial state, and rules for turn taking, capture,
and ending states (checkmate, draw, resignation). Together, these gener-
ate a rich (yet also tightly constrained) possibility space, including possible
game evolutions and possible states of play. (Note that the possible legal
states of play are generated by game evolutions. Not all permutations of
pieces in spaces are legal—something that the expert recognizes but the
novice does not. A chess game cannot, for example, result in a configura-
tion in which a player’s pawns occupy the row closest to him.)
The different components of understanding chess are tightly, even con-
stituently, interwoven. My understanding of the piece type
KNIGHT cannot
be separated from my understanding of the rules of movement. Change
the rules for how a knight moves, and you have redefined the type
KNIGHT
as well, and vice versa. Change the size or configuration of the board, and
the move type
CASTLING must either be altered or lost entirely. You can, to be
sure, generate alternative games by starting with chess and changing things
about the board, the pieces, or the rules. But what you have then is a new
game, one that requires a distinct mental model, albeit one that might be
produced by starting with a model of chess and making alterations.
As I move beyond the baseline understanding of chess—being able to
play the game in accordance with the rules—I gradually begin to under-
stand things about tactics and strategy as well. These are normative rather
than permissive or constitutive in character, but they involve a subset of
sequences of moves permitted by the rules and ultimately guided by the
definition of the end state of having the opposing player in checkmate
(or, if that becomes impossible, forcing a draw). Both the baseline compe-
tence and the increasing mastery of competitive play may start out guided
by explicit rules and heuristics; but the more expert I become, the more
automatically they occur to me, and the more intuitive and less calculated
they seem.
