8.2 Variants
One model might be counted as a variant of another insofar as they share
much of the same representational system and rules. As a simple obser-
vation about how we might compare features of two models, this is not
in itself particularly interesting, though it does raise questions about how
models should be individuated and differentiated from one another, analo-
gous to questions about whether two people ever really speak “the same
language” or possess “the same concept.” I am interested in the notion of
variant models, however, not so much as a classification by similarity as a
classification by etiology: that is, I am interested in the psychological fact
that we can alter models for a purpose.
There are at least two types of cases of creating a new model through
alteration. In the first, we become dissatisfied with a model—say, a scien-
tific theory—and make some minor change in it. If the change is successful,
we likely no longer use the older version. I believe there is some reason to
think of the “before” and “after” state of the mental model in question as
two states of a psychological continuant that has undergone change over
time. The model possessed at time t
1

The second case occurs when we see a use for a second model, very like
one we already possess, and produced by using the first as a basis and mak-
ing adjustments. The invention of non-Euclidean geometries might be seen
in this way. A mathematician like Bernhard Riemann began with a model
that he already understood well (Euclidean geometry) and then altered a
part of it (the parallel postulate). The result was a new model with different
properties. We also see such cases in scientific change. Whereas the former
type of change is more or less what Kuhn called incremental change, the
latter is revolutionary change.

the public sphere. And it seems likely that having a model in a form that
