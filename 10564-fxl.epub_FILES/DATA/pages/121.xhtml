Let us now try to be a bit more careful and precise. I am suggesting that
I have mental models of things such as my childhood home. (Perhaps there
is a single integrated model of it in my mind, perhaps a constellation of
models of parts and aspects of it that I can move between and coordinate.)
But by this I do not mean that “having a mental model” means that there
“is something” in my mind that has the same geometric features as my
house. That, indeed, would strike me as a category error. What, then, do I
mean by “having a model” of my house in my mind?
One thing I mean is that there is something in my mind that allows me
to store and explore features of the house itself. And in these examples, I
am particularly concerned with forms of “exploring features” that bear a
good deal of phenomenological resemblance to what I might do to extract
information about the house by interacting with it if I were physically situ-
ated within it. Phenomenologically, I am imagining standing in a point in
the room and looking in a direction, attending to the way the curtains are
hung. This allows me to extract some of the same information about the
room (assuming the imagining is accurate) that I would be able to extract
were I actually situated in that fashion within the room. Phenomenologi-
cally, I imagine turning 45 degrees to the right, and other features of the
room become available to my mind’s eye. These correspond to some of
the features that would become available for perceptual inspection were I
really thus situated. Of course, the information extracted is accurate (and
hence only “information” in the normative sense) only to the extent that
my mental model of the room is accurate. It is surely finite in detail, and
likely inaccurate in some respects. Mental models are abstract and can be
erroneous. But for such things to take place at all there must be some kind
