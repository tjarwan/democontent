Disunities of Knowledge, Science, and Understanding
12 Disunities of Knowledge, Science, and Understanding
We all find ourselves having some number of conflicting beliefs. We make
different and conflicting judgments on different occasions or when primed
in different ways. Sometimes, even upon deep reflection, we find ourselves
committed to two opposing claims and do not know how to resolve the
tension between them. It is not always easy to tell where the sources of
conflicting commitment arise from, but they are often tied to the ways
in which we frame situations through different models. And upon inspec-
tion, the different models may involve not only different ways of framing
things but also different commitments and different patterns of inference
whose internal logics lead to contrary conclusions. We might describe this
by saying that our beliefs and our understanding are disunified in various
ways. Moreover, we find such disunities not only in ordinary life but also in
philosophical and scientific investigation. Indeed, recognition of the ways
our beliefs and understanding as we find them are disunified is among the
primary drivers of philosophical and scientific inquiry. Sometimes such
inquiry leads to new ways of thinking that dissolve the old problems, but
often it does not, and in some cases it presents us with new problems to
replace the old.
This chapter seeks to do two things. First, it attempts to apply the
resources of cognitive pluralism toward a diagnosis of why some types of
disunity of belief and understanding arise as a consequence of a model-
based architecture for understanding. Second, it examines the question of
whether a model-based architecture might give us reason to suspect that
some such disunities might be principled and abiding—not simply symp-
toms of our current ignorance but predictable and lasting artifacts of a
particular type of cognitive architecture. First, however, we would do well
to try to clarify some different notions of how beliefs and understanding
might be “unified” or “disunified.”
