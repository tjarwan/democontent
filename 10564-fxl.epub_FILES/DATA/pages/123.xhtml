1. Many types of skilled bodily interaction are “skilled” in the sense of
using abstract mental models of what one is interacting with.
2. Human minds are capable, to varying degrees, of decoupling the use of
these mental models from bodily interaction, affording capacities for
imagination, information extraction, simulation, and reasoning.
7.4 Chess
Suppose I wish to learn chess. I might begin (though it is probably not
the best way to begin) by reading a rulebook for the game. I then sit down
with a chessboard and another player or with a computer chess program
and begin to try to apply the rules in actual play. At first, I may consult the
rulebook frequently. (Alternatively, I might learn to play by sitting across
the board from someone who already understands the game and gradually
introduces me to the rules, intermixed with practice of the perceptual and
motor skills involved in playing chess on a board with a particular set of
pieces.) But as time goes on, I begin to simply see pawns and knights as
capable of particular types of movement, and of the state of play as afford-
ing particular types of strategic development. As this progresses, I am clearly
developing some type of internalized understanding of chess.
It might be tempting to think of this process as though it were a mat-
ter of memorizing the rules. Of course, some people do memorize a set
of rules from a book on chess, and it is a minimum threshold for compe-
tence in chess that one can determine which moves are permissible, what
constitutes a capture, a checkmate, and so on. But a person can memorize
the rulebook without understanding how to apply its principles properly,
and one can learn to apply the principles without memorizing the rules as
verbal formulas. One does end up with some form of understanding that
mirrors the rules, but this understanding need not involve any reflection
on the rules. Rather, we perceive the board through a kind of cognitive tem-
plate that has been shaped by the rules. We might even say that this sort
