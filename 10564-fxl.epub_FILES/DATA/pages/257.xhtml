While the production of cognitive illusions is in itself always lamen-
table, the mechanisms that produce them are arguably grounded in fur-
ther features of our cognitive architecture, which confer distinct cognitive
advantages. I will discuss two of these features: intuitive essentialism and
the augmentation of understanding through explicit rules or principles.
Intuitive Essentialism
A good deal of psychological evidence indicates that, even as young chil-
dren, human beings are “intuitive essentialists”—that is, in dividing the
world into kinds, we tacitly assume those kinds to have hidden inner
natures that are responsible for their characteristic observable properties
and behavior. In an article reviewing research on essentialist thinking in
early childhood (including preschool children), Susan Gelman writes:
Essentialism is the view that certain categories have an underlying reality or true
nature that one cannot observe directly but that gives an object its identity, and
is responsible for other similarities that category members share. In the domain of
biology, an essence would be whatever quality remains unchanging as an organ-
ism grows, reproduces, and undergoes morphological transformations (baby to man;
caterpillar to butterfly). In the domain of chemistry, an essence would be whatever
quality remains unchanging as a substance changes shape, size, or state (from solid
to liquid to gas).
Essentialist accounts have been proposed and discussed for thousands of years,
extending back at least to Plato’s cave allegory in The Republic. Numerous fields,
including biology, philosophy, linguistics, literary criticism, and psychology, stake
claims about essentialism. Here we are concerned with essentialism as a psychologi-
cal claim. Although there are serious problems with essentialism as a metaphysical
doctrine, recent psychological studies converge to suggest that essentialism is a rea-
soning heuristic that is readily available to both children and adults. This article
reviews such evidence, and discusses the implications for human concepts. (Gelman
2004, 404)
Psychological essentialism is arguably a deep principle of how children
think and learn about the world. It is not simply that children impute par-
ticular features to kinds of things—say, to species—as essential properties;
rather, they expect kinds (or at least some types of kinds) to have essences,
even if they do not know what those essences might be.
