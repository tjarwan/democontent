Cognitive pluralism, of course, is also a cognitivist philosophy. Discus-
sion of its possible metaphysical implications will have to wait for another
book, but clearly there are important questions to be answered about matters
such as its relationship to realism and idealism. I shall confine myself here
to a brief discussion of the question of projective illusion. Because aptness
is ultimately a pragmatic matter, the fact that object-oriented cognition and
schemas for causation are features of our cognitive architecture that provide
basic ways of understanding the world does not debar them from counting
as apt. Indeed, to the extent that models that allow us to constitute a world
of objects standing in causal relations provide good epistemic and practical
traction upon the world, this renders them apt. But to this we should add
two important caveats. First, contemporary science, unlike the science of
Kantâ€™s day, includes models that posit phenomena that resist characteriza-
tion as classical objects. To the extent that the mind strives to force these
into the mold of notions of objects or causation, this may well be a process
that can produce cognitive illusions. Second, when we move beyond the
application of models in ordinary cognition and empirically constrained
science and attempt to think about substance and causation as fundamen-
tal metaphysical categories, we may well move beyond the realms in which
they are aptly applied. In particular, if we adopt the naive realist stance that
treats such categories as mind-independent ways the world divides itself up,
we indeed fall into something akin to projective illusion.
