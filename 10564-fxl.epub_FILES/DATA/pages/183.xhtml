This argument was actually quite influential, so much so that it set back
mathematics by about a generation. One of the first people to conceive of
non-Euclidean geometries was Carl Friedrich Gauss, a nineteenth-century
mathematician of the first order, who made many important discoveries,
such as the field of differential geometry. Gauss, however, did not follow up
on the idea of non-Euclidean geometries. Why? Because Gauss was a con-
vinced Kantian and supposed that Kant had demonstrated the synthetic a
priori truth of the claim that space is Euclidean. (Because of his deference to
Kant, I like to think of Gauss as the father of deferential geometry.) But other
mathematicians—the Bolyais, Riemann, Lobachevsky—did pursue the idea
and discovered consistent non-Euclidean geometries. So even if Kant was
correct that we can only perceive things through the lens of one particular
geometry, we can nevertheless think in the terms of alternative geometries.
And by early in the twentieth century, Einstein had proposed on empirical
grounds that space is not Euclidean, a thesis that was to receive subsequent
confirmation.
My point here is not to show that Kant was wrong about spatial geom-
etry. (Plenty of previous writers have done that.) What I would like to point
out is that if we were “Kantian cognizers”—that is, beings whose minds
were really built in the ways Kant suggests—we would have been incapable
of even conceiving of an alternative to Euclidean geometry. A Kantian mind
could not concretely think or imagine possibilities contrary to the Forms
of Sensibility and Categories of Understanding. And perhaps there are ways
other beings could conceive of the world that our cognitive architecture
does not permit us to conceive. But non-Euclidean geometry is not one of
them.
A mind that can entertain and create multiple models with different
representational systems has a far better chance of avoiding forced error
than a mind that has only a single representational system. The errors that
are forced by a particular model need not be forced on the mind, unless it is
incapable of thinking through models that do not produce the same forced
errors. Without recourse to alternative models, it would be impossible to
distinguish forced error from synthetic a priori truth in one’s own case. I
will return to the somewhat disturbing question of whether there may be
principled limits to what sorts of things we can understand in chapter 12.
For now, the point is that a pluralistic cognitive architecture is a good strat-
egy for avoiding such limits as far as they can be avoided.
