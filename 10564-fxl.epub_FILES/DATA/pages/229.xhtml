Models provide ways of representing and reasoning about any number of
states of affairs within their domains. The range of models available to us
is further expanded through their complementary relationship with lan-
guage and language-like thinking, by social transmission, and by more regi-
mented processes of inquiry such as those found in philosophical analysis
and the sciences. These allow us not only to create new models, but also to
refine existing models, to consider and use multiple models, and to test one
model against another.
On the other hand, the very features that make this strategy so power-
ful—that models are used to frame and produce judgments, that model-
based inference methods are well suited to their domains, and above all
that models are idealized and employ particular types of representations
and inference rules—can also result in disunities in understanding and
belief. Most obviously, just having multiple models is itself a modest form
of disunity. More troubling is that models with different representational
systems and inference rules can produce inconsistent beliefs and judg-
ments. And this offends a second epistemic sensibility: a desire that the
things we understand and believe be consistent with one another.
A model-based architecture for understanding is characterized by a built-
in tension between comprehensiveness and consistency. We acquire more
comprehensive understanding first and foremost through having more
models, but the addition of each new model carries the risk of generating
more inconsistencies with the commitments of other models. Sometimes,
of course, apparent inconsistencies turn out to be illusory: two models may
have incompatible commitments, but these imply inconsistent judgments
about the world only in cases where both models are aptly applied to the
same situations. In cases like quantum complementarity, it turns out that
the conflicting models are aptly applied to disjoint sets of cases and as a
result do not commit us to inconsistent judgments about particular cases.
And sometimes we are able to resolve the problem by replacing one or both
models with one or more successors that do not jointly produce inconsis-
tent judgments. Our drive to resolve conflicts and inconsistencies provides
a kind of regulative norm for both philosophy and science. But there is no
guarantee that it can be carried out to its ideal conclusion, whether in the
form of a single comprehensive and self-consistent model or at least a set of
distinct models that play well together.
