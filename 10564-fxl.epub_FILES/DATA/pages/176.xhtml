10.3 The Advantages of Model Proliferation
What has been said so far has been an exploration of why minds like ours,
in which learning plays such a weighty role, would still have a “grab bag of
good tricks” as well. But another issue for cognitive pluralism is waiting in
the wings. Why would the more flexible types of cognition we possess come
in the form of many distinct models? The answers to this question basically
fall into two categories: (1) it is a good strategy for increasing understanding
and intelligence, and (2) there may not really be any alternatives, at least
not for beings remotely like us.
Complex World, Many Patterns, Finite Minds
The world is a complex place. Indeed, even the little bit of the world we per-
ceive at any one moment with our senses is quite complex. More is going
on in the room you are in during the time it takes you to read this sentence
than you could possibly think about in a lifetime. Perhaps God and the
angels have minds that can take it all in, but you and I do not. Nor does
any living organism on the planet. Our minds are not only finite but quite
limited in their capacity when compared with all the facts and events that
there are, or even those in our immediate vicinities.
To have any understanding at all, a finite, embodied mind has to rep-
resent the world in an abstract, idealized way, one that compresses much
of the messy complexity into a finite number of concepts and disregards
the rest (Simon 1977). And to be of any use to the organism, these con-
cepts must be concepts of things that are salient to it and its interests and
do a good-enough job of tracking real patterns (Dennett 1991b; Gibson
1966, 1977), either “out there” in the world or else at the interface between
organism and environment. As minds become more powerful, the “inter-
ests” may become increasingly disconnected from biological needs—say,
an interest in opera or in physics—and the tracking may well increase in
fidelity. But even so, the form that understanding takes is bound to be heav-
ily influenced by what interests the organism happens to have, by what
regularities in the world are salient to those interests, and by which of those
regularities the organism can perceive, conceive, and act on.
