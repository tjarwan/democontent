Again, there is a good deal of individual difference in how well different
people can perform such an exercise. One person can do it successfully only
if there is a single carbon atom, another can do it with chains of two or
three, and so on. What the upper bounds might be is an empirical question.
There are probably savants who can imagine much larger molecular struc-
tures than you or I, and each of us would likely be able to expand our upper
bound through practice; but it might be that a DNA molecule would prove
too large and complex for anyone to imagine in detail. But this is an obser-
vation about performance limits rather than competence, similar to the fact
that there is some limit to the size of sentences you are able to parse, even
though you have the necessary grammatical competence required. You can
understand the sentences “Noah was the son of Lamech” and “Noah was
the son of the son of Methuselah,” and you understand the iterative nature
of the “son of …” construction, but at some point you lose track of the gen-
erations because you have exceeded some kind of limit on storage or pars-
ing ability. Similarly, you might be able to concretely imagine one or two
carbon atoms and what they are bonded with, but there is some upper limit
to how many you can imagine concretely, though you are perfectly capable
of imagining and thinking about the properties of any suitably small por-
tion of a very large molecule.
How should we describe what you are doing when you perform a task
such as imagining a chain of two carbon atoms with hydrogen atoms occu-
pying the remaining positions? It seems natural to speak of this as “build-
ing (or using) a mental model” of such a molecule. You substitute an oxygen
molecule or an OH group in one of the slots, and you have produced a men-
tal model of a different type of molecule. But something deeper is going
on as well. Your ability to imagine particular types of molecules and per-
form specific transformations on them—structural possibilities and trans-
formations that correspond to those afforded by the modeling kit (and,
assuming it has been well designed, to the ways molecules can actually be
configured)—suggests that you have internalized the structural principles
and possibilities that were engineered into the external model, and you
now have a general and flexible capacity that you previously lacked: to
model molecular configurations in your mind. With the external model, the
mechanical properties of the pieces—the number and locations of the slots
in the balls into which struts can be inserted, the lengths of the struts—
enforce the “rules” for their combination and generate a possibility space
