While uses of the word ‘model’ are diverse, they reflect the common
themes that scientific understanding involves the use of some kind of sur-
rogate, be it mental or external, that has formal or structural similarities to
its target domain. Moreover, while some particular uses of the word ‘model’
may differ—referring to external models, or to modes of thought involving
analogy or idealization—the overall accounts in which they occur are more
consonant with my cognitivist notion of modeling than at first it might
appear.
The difference between using an external model and using a mental
model is not really all that great. External models are already in some sense
“mental,” in that they are models only in light of how they are used in think-
ing and reasoning (though, of course, they are not “purely mental” in the
sense that would imply that there is no external model). Moreover, often
when one uses an external model, one is in fact thinking about the tar-
get system and not the model: one thinks about the target system through
the model. Similar things can be said about analogies like the billiard ball
model of particle collisions: while the model is inspired by the collisions of
actual billiard balls, one uses the model to think about collisions of gas mol-
ecules or other particles, and in practice the source metaphor is forgotten or
becomes transparent. The person using such a model is thinking about gas
molecule collisions in the same way that she thinks about billiard ball colli-
sions, but she is generally not thinking about billiard balls at all in so doing.
Simplification through idealization is likewise already built into my char-
acterization of models, and I do not regard this as a distinction between
different modes of scientific cognition. Some models, theories, and laws
are more simplified and reflect their targets less exactly than others, but
all involve bracketing and distorting idealizations (sometimes called “Aris-
totelian” and “Galilean” idealizations, respectively, in the philosophy of
science literature).
