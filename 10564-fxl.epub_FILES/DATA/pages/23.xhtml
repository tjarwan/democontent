Interpretivism
This leads us to a deeper concern. Some philosophers—call them intentional
realists—assume that when we talk about beliefs and inferences, we are talk-
ing about occurrent episodes of thought involving sentence-like representa-
tions with propositional content and inferential processes that operate on
them (Fodor 1975, 1978, 1990). Their reason for doing so is not merely
an introspective sense that sometimes we really do think in propositions
(or even in natural-language sentences) and follow syllogistic patterns of
reasoning. Rather, they tend to appeal to “intentional explanation”—expla-
nation of what a person says or does in terms of what she believes and
desires—which they view as a form of causal explanation in which judg-
ments, occurrent desires, and processes of theoretical and practical reason-
ing play the role of the explainers. If the claim “Jane went to the refrigerator
because she wanted some milk, and believed that the milk was in the refrig-
erator” is supposed to be a causal explanation, that explanation can be true
only if Jane really had the imputed mental states and they played a causal
role in producing her action.
But there is an alternative account of what we are doing when we ascribe
intentional states, and even when we use them to explain behavior. To use
Daniel Dennett’s terminology, what we are doing is adopting an interpretive
stance, which he calls the intentional stance (Dennett 1971, 1981/1997). Here
is how Dennett explains the intentional stance in one of several papers in
which he defends the notion:

ist), it can be discerned only from the point of view of one who adopts a certain
predictive strategy, and its existence can be confirmed only by an assessment of the
success of that strategy (that apparently makes me an interpretationist).
