Another way of putting it is to say that evolution does not operate like
an engineer, who can make large-scale changes between models and even
go back to the drawing board and start from scratch. Evolution is limited by
the range of mutations on genes that can produce organisms that are viable
throughout their developmental sequence and capable of breeding. This
evolutionary constraint also sets a limit on how drastic a change can be in
species that reproduce sexually: a mutation that produces greater success
for the individual organism in everything save breeding will not get passed
on if the genetic changes make it reproductively incompatible with the rest
of the population. We now know that mutations do not need to be as tiny
and gradual as Darwin supposed. But compared with engineering, evolu-
tion is still a gradual and incremental process. When traits are lost because
they no longer confer an adaptive advantage, this is likely to happen on
the glacial timescale over which evolution operates on species. Perhaps our
descendants will have progressively fewer special-purpose canalized mecha-
nisms; but if so, the process will likely take thousands or tens of thousands
of years. (This is, of course, something that could change if we take an
active role in altering the human genome.)
“Heritage” Traits Are Still Useful
Moreover, it is by no means clear that losing some of these traits would be
a good thing. Take the reaction of disgust. Scientists now generally believe
that disgust evolved as a highly flexible mechanism for avoiding sources
of contagion, such as excrement, blood, open wounds, skin diseases, ver-
min, and rotting tissue. The mechanism is triggered by many stimuli, oper-
ates quickly, and makes it difficult to force oneself to come into contact
with whatever is regarded as disgusting. Of course, sometimes it is triggered
by things that are not really dangerous. Most slithery things are not really
dangerous. As I child I had a strong reaction of disgust to mushrooms,
including the innocent and nutritious mushrooms used in cooking. And,
as it turns out, leprosy—perhaps the paradigmatically disgusting disease
afflicting human beings—turns out to be among the least contagious of
diseases.
